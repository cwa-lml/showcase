Level 2: Paper Weaving

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

During the processes of discussion, drawing, and weaving students can develop the knowledge and vocabulary to talk about art. Teachers should encourage students to continually use the art vocabulary associated with this unit by:

  • stopping frequently to consider and discuss the students work;
  • focusing on selected elements using images and artists work for reference;
  • encouraging students to respond verbally to their own and others work.

The following elements and principles will be the focus for discussion in this learning example.

Elements

Texture – Consider the surface quality of an object and how it may or may not be used in weaving (e.g., rough/ smooth, wet/ dry, hard/ soft, shiny/ dull, slippery/ sticky).

Principles

Pattern – Consider how shapes grouped together, or shapes repeated many times create a pattern within a weaving (e.g., repitition, rotation, reflection, symmetry)
WARP: strips of paper the same length used as the base for weaving
WEFT: paper strips used for weaving through the warp.

Possible starting points

Select one or more of the following:

  • Arrange for a visit to your local art gallery or museum to look at kete, woven mats, or woven cloaks.
  • Invite a local weaver to visit the class to show and discuss their work.
  • Set up a display of various everyday woven objects eg. cane baskets, "hinaki" eel pots, a selection of simple flax baskets, big woven cylinder shapes, mats, kete (encourage students to contribute to the display).
  • Display a collection of images of woven objects.

More information on art elements http://www.cyberschool.k12.or.us/~wallace

Possible learning approaches

Discuss the objects on display:
  • What materials have been used in the weaving?
  • How can these be used?
  • How are they decorated?

After looking at, exploring, and discussing the objects/images, encourage students to ask questions about the function and significance of traditional Māori weaving.

Basic weaving

Use rolls of paper such as crepe paper or long strips of material to create a plain weave in which successive rows of weft material alternate under/over with over/under in relation to the warp. This weave should make a firm construction as the warp/weft are interlaced all the time.

Handy hint
It is useful to use different colours for warp and weft so that the main pattern is easily identified.

Drawing ideas for weaving

Using large sheets of paper (newsprint) and two colours of wax crayon ask students (working in groups of no more than four) to draw parallel lines onto the paper to represent the warp using one of the colours. Use the other colour to draw the weft. Encourage each group to try a woven pattern. Discuss the patterns that emerge and choose the most interesting to create a more advanced and complex weaving.

Weaving with other materials

Students explore possibilities using other materials:

  • add other fibres or objects to develop surface texture and pattern, e.g., feathers, dried seaweed, electricians wire, toitoi, shells, beads or stones;
  • chicken wire, rope, or flax as the warp;
  • tree branches as the weaving frame.

Assessment

Possible specific learning outcomes include:

Students will be able to:

  • generate a range of patterns using weaving methods (PK, DI),
  • discuss the selected pattern and explain its significance and the process involved in its creation (CI).
