Level 3: Personal Adornment/ Clothing

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Throughout the unit or lesson, teachers should be conferencing both individuals and groups of students whilst encouraging them to respond to and reflect on their own work and to the work of others by:

  • reinforcing their knowledge of basic elements and principles;
  • focusing on specific principles using images and artists work for reference.

The following elements and principles will be the focus for discussion in this learning example.

Elements

Focus on line, colour, texture, shape and form

Principles

Focus on balance, harmony, rhythm and contrast

Possible starting points

Mindmapping

As a class or in groups, develop mindmaps around some of the following ideas:

  • Tribal (e.g., African Masai), Aboriginal, Papua New Guinea
  • Royalty of different cultures
  • M–ori/Pacific Island
  • Contemporary pop culture, eg Punk Rock, Theatre bands (eg Kiss Queen)
  • Times in History, e.g., medieval, 1920s-1930s

Consider: the social, cultural aspects of the role, the status, or rank of the individual and the costume adornment and clothing design associated with the position.

Class discussion

A selection of the following questions would be useful as initial discussion points:

  • Why do you choose the style of clothes you wear?
  • How do people express their roles in life and their status in society by the clothes they wear?
  • Does your family dress up for a special occasion, e.g. birthday, wedding, funeral?
  • How is this style of dress different to your everyday wear?
  • What effect do special occasion clothes have on others?
  • How does the location (e.g., a marae) affect how people think about traditional Māori clothing?
  • How do you feel when you see someone wearing a gang patch, a silver fern, a St Johns Ambulance badge, lots of jewellery, tattoos?
  • How does clothing contribute to status/ rank/ hierarchy within a culture/ society?

Display a wide range of images that show different styles of clothing and personal adornment from various cultures/social groups. Use photographs/still images/paintings/Internet images and printouts of clothing/adornment. (See Teaching and Learning Materials. )

Consider the following:

  • People in uniforms (e.g., sports team member, pipe band member, school uniforms). At an advanced level students may explore how people in uniforms attempt to show individuality, uniqueness, non-conformity.
  • Social groups e.g., surfies, gangs, guides, scouts, skateboarders, jazz dancers, cyclists.
  • Cultural groups e.g., Māori, Dutch, Somali, Chinese, Indian, Pacific Island, Australian Aboriginal.
  • Costume/clothing and adornment from other countries and cultures (e.g., Papua New Guinea, African tribes, Aboriginal).
  • Changing fashions/ fads – clothing designs and styles over the years
  • Clothing design – Benson and Hedges Fashion Awards and Wearable Arts show work with a distinctive New Zealand/ Pacific Island flavour.

Possible learning approaches

Drawing/ painting / mixed media collage on a variety of surfaces (e.g., card, paper, fabric).

Create a character with costumes (select clothes with strong decorative/ interest features). Ask the model to pose "in character".

Make drawings of the figure. The aim of the exercise is to reflect the social, or cultural identity of the model. Emphasis should be placed on the specific features of the costume, personal adornment, or pose that help to identify the character.

Make large-scale artwork using drawing and painting media. Photocopies and cut-out text and images could be included as part of the painting to reflect the social, or cultural aspects of the role. Consider viewpoint (e.g., looking up at the figure from below) to emphasise status of the person.

Follow-up discussion

Assemble a range of portraits (reproductions in books, photocopies, computer printouts, originals), include photographs, paintings, or sculptures.

  • Discuss how the portraits reflect status and cultural identity.

Display the students' paintings

  • Discuss the devices used to reflect status and cultural identity and consider how effective they are.

Materials

  • Selection of clothes/costumes.
  • Paper, large paper for class mindmap.
  • Drawing – drawing materials – pencils, chalk, crayons, felts, ink, pastels.
  • Painting – acrylic, brushes, sponges, card, paper, paper photocopied material, torn/cut paper photographs, text, glue and scissors.

Assessment

Possible specific learning outcomes include:
Students will be able to:

  • discuss the different ways in which costume and personal adornment can express identity, role and status (CI);
  • identify which aspects in images of people convey their role and status in their particular culture (UC);
  • use imagination, observation and invention with materials to communicate identity, role and status through the use of appropriate imagery and materials in their own paintings (DI).

At a more advanced level, students' ability to research and identify the symbolic qualities of colour (e.g. "blue" for the Virgin Mary's cloak), the meaning of cultural symbols (e.g., the dove representing "peace") and the changes of these over time can be assessed.
