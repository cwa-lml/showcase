Level 5: Waka Building

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Activities | Teaching Tips | Assessment

Possible Starting Points

  • Show students video excerpts, photographic images of dances based upon narrative structure, e.g. Romeo and Juliet, dance dramas from India, Aboriginal dances based upon the "Dream Time", African tribal dance rituals. (See Teaching and Learning Materials)

  • Take students to a museum to look at a waka

  • Bring in a member of a local waka crew to talk to the students

  • Students research the history and development of waka in Māori society using books, audio, film and video resources

  • Read students stories about the journey of the waka from Hawaiiki to Aotearoa. (See Teaching and Learning Materials).

Activities

  • Discuss marae rituals which is the setting of the hui

  • Students create a dance/ drama based upon the different roles which people play (see Teaching and Learning Materials). An example might be the exchange between the locals (tangata whenua) and visitors (manuhiri), the karanga (local and visiting callers call and answer), the wero (challenge), the hongi (nose to nose greeting), the powhiri (action chant of welcome)

  • Students depict the above using movement and gesture rather than words

  • Discuss the rituals associated with finding and felling the tree which will become the waka. Why does it need to be blessed? What is the significance of Tane Mahuta in Māori spiritual beliefs?

  • Students create a dance depicting the search for and felling of the tree and the associated rituals

  • Discuss with students the significance of whakairo (carving) in both traditional and contemporary Māori society. Invite a Māori carver to talk with the students about the practical and spiritual aspects of Māori carving

  • Students create a work rhythm dance based upon carving actions

  • Use recorded music (see Teaching and Learning Materials), or students create their own percussion score to accompany the dance (drums, woodblocks, claves, pate)

  • Students create a dance drama based upon launching the waka and taking it on its maiden voyage

  • Look at video/ film footage (see Teaching and Learning Materials) of waka races and create dance movements based upon the rowing action

  • Put each section of the dance drama together and present to others.

Teaching tips

  • Use community expertise, i.e. bring in local or visiting artists

  • Create a research approach to this learning example, where the students bring lots of information to the actual dance drama

  • Plot the story, scenes, events with the students on a large piece of paper so that everyone has an overview of the activities and how they fit into the whole

  • Appoint a student as a director of each scene with responsibility to ensure that it is ready to present and links with the other scenes.

Assessment

Possible specific learning outcomes include:

  • Students can demonstrate their understanding of the rituals associated with a hui by enacting a dance/ drama based upon these protocols

  • Students can demonstrate research skills on a topic and feed this information into the creation of a dance drama

  • Students can demonstrate their ability to work in a group to create a dance drama

  • Students can demonstrate physical dance skills associated with each activity

  • Students can demonstrate dance making skills associated with each activity.
