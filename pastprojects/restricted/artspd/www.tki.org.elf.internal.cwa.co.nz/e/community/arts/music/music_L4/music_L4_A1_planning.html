Level 4: Creating and Representing Sounds

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Key teaching and learning concepts

Pitch – the degree of highness or lowness of a note.

Dynamics – the varying levels of loudness and softness in music; or the signs used to indicate such levels.

Tempo – the speed of the beat in music.

Rhythm – duration of notes forming patterns.

Tone colour – the specific tone or sound quality of a musical instrument, combination of instruments, or sound source (eg., a saxophone and a trumpet playing the same note each has its own distinctive sound); also called timbre. Sound sources include found, environmental, vocal, instrumental and electronic sounds.

Symbolic representation of sounds, that is, notation (graphic, conventional, pictorial).

Prior learning

Students need to have:

  • an understanding of the capabilities of instruments to create sounds and how to experiment with a single sound source;

  • an understanding of elements of music;

  • an understanding that sounds can be notated in different ways– graphic, conventional, pictorial.

Possible specific learning outcomes

The students will demonstrate the ability to:

  • experiment with, manipulate and select a range of musical ideas (PK);

  • structure musical ideas to create a brief piece (DI);

  • create a musical score that others can accurately interpret and perform (PK).

[tip]

Ideas for activities

  • Listen to a variety of sound-makers, including examples of skin, wood, bamboo, plastic, metal, and whatever is readily available in terms of found sounds.

  • Listen to music that displays examples of sound experimentation, e.g., prepared piano works by Dan Poynton, Threnody to the victims of Hiroshima by Penderecki, Te Ku Te Whe by Hirini Melbourne and Richard Nunns, Taonga Puoro, and the "Our Music" resource for different ways of playing drums (refer to Teaching and Learning Material).

  • Listen to some stylistic differences of performances, e.g., double bass jazz, versus classical bass playing – same instrument, different sound production techniques and playing style.

  • Explore a variety of different sound sources including instruments, body percussion, voice, found sounds. Where equipment is available, use processors, mixers, and samplers to edit and create a range of different effects e.g., cutting out frequencies, applying distortion, panning.

  • Take different methods of sound production, i.e. hit, strummed, blown, and investigate the nature of these with respect to other sound sources.

  • Compile a "kete of sounds" – a large selection of different ways to perform on a single sound source. This "basket" is likened to a resource bank of many ideas that students can build up over time and use examples from when required. Make an audio tape recording of the kete of sounds discovered on single sound source.

  • Record own individual piece incorporating sounds from a single sound source. Compare this with other class members' work through shared listening and discussion about the processes employed.

  • Experiment with a variety of ways to represent sounds symbolically. Provide a key code and share the representations to assess how accurately others can interpret them.

Assessment approaches

Formative

During learning ask the students the following so that their progress can be monitored in an ongoing way:

  • How could you make some of the different sounds you've heard yourself?

  • How did you change sounds on the single source?

  • How could you combine a variety of sounds to make your brief piece? What order works best?

Which symbols most accurately represent your sounds? Can others interpret them to make the sounds you expect to hear?

Key questions

  • Which symbols were easy to understand and why?
  • What could be done to make this piece of music clearer to read and play?

Peer review criteria:

  • excellence – score is clear, detailed and easy to follow
  • merit – score is clear and easy to follow
  • credit – score is relatively clear and generally able to be followed
  • not yet achieved – score is unclear and difficult to follow

Possible strategies for assessment

Peer reflection on interpreting the score in performance

Self-assessment based on peer review
