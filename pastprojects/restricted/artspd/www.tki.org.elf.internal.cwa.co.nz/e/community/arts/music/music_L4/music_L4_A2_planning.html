Level 4: Exploring and Charting Contrast

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Key teaching and learning concepts

Creating music – experience with the elements of music and structural devices:

  • Pitch – the degree of highness or lowness of a note.

  • Dynamics – the varying levels of loudness and softness in music; or the signs used to indicate such levels.

  • Tempo – the speed of the beat in music.

  • Rhythm – duration of notes forming patterns.

  • Tone colour – the specific tone or sound quality of a musical instrument, combination of instruments, or sound source (e.g., a saxophone and a trumpet playing the same note each has its own distinctive sound); also called timbre. Sound sources include found, environmental, vocal, instrumental, and electronic sounds.

  • Silence – absence of sound

  • Texture – a piece of music's density of sound, which may range from thin (e.g., A single strand or instrumental line) to thick (e.g., several strands or instrumental lines).

  • Contrast – differences within and between the elements of music.

  • Structure – the way in which the parts (or sections) of a musical work are arranged.

Reading and recording original music – experience in notating and interpreting scores:

  • Notation – means of representing sound in a wirtten format e.g., graphic, conventional, time-chart, wave forms (computer).

Playing/singing original music – experience in working alone and in groups and presenting musical ideas to others through performance:

  • Group work – negotiation of use of ideas by members of a group

  • Presenting compositions – performing live or via a recording of the work, representing in a form of notation.

Prior learning

Students need to have:

  • an understanding of contrast and the elements of music (Experiment with sound contrasts);

  • a knowledge of the basic concept of structure/ form and possible ways to represent notate it;

  • listened to a range of music.

Possible specific learning outcomes

The students will:

  • identify which uses of contrast are displayed in a wide range of music examples (PK);

  • present structural representations of pieces heard in the form of charts/scores (PK);

  • create and present to others a group composition based on a specific chart that represents structural contrast (DI, CI);

  • identify the use of contrast created by other groups' compositions and reflect on possible connections between pieces heard (CI).

Ideas for activities

  • Observe use of contrast in music/ dance setting, where percussion and movement are used to create improvised contrasting sections in a performance. A structural chart can be created of the performance, representing the types and placement of contrasting sections.

  • Listen to short extracts from a range of music examples and identify contrasts between them (elements and structural devices) e.g.,

      Vivaldi's Spring from The Four Seasons

      Bizet, L'Arsienne suite

      Mussorgsky, Pictures at an Exhibition

      Bach, Brandenburg Concertos (solo and tutti contrasts)

      Handel, Hallelujah Chorus

      Lilburn, Soundscape with Lake and River

      Queen, Bohemian Rhapsody

      Beatles, A Day in the Life from Sgt Peppers album

      Body, Duets and Choruses Musik Dari Jalan.

      ENZSO, Strait Old Line, I Hope I Never

      Moana and the Moahaunters, Tahi


  • Experiment with different ways of representing sounds heard – graphic using symbols, time-chart with seconds represented, and stave representation. Investigate ways of representing these sounds using a key code.

  • Sing "call and response" type songs and talk about structure and contrasts created in these songs.

  • Investigate common types of structure (e.g., song form) and provide well known example title/composer of each.

  • Use a Structure Matching Game – match the structure of the extract heard with the correct card that represents the section heard, e.g., individual cards prepared with Verse 1, Chorus, Bridge, Verse 2, Chorus, Intro, Outro for students to put in order as they listen to a song, or cards that show textures of thick and thin, changes in dynamics, repeated ideas as opposed to different musical ideas, and so on.

  • Perform existing graphic notation examples to each other e.g., examples in Elizabeth Kerr's Our Music. Try changing the order of sections and ideas within these examples.

  • Provide groups with an identical graphic score to take away and rehearse privately. Return to perform to the rest of group. Are they all identical? Why, or why not?

  • Have students listen to one particular piece of music and individually chart the structure heard. Put students in groups to form a combined chart and create a composition based on this chart. Have groups present this composition to others, either live or through a recording on tape. Have class evaluate the connections between the group composition and the original piece heard.

[tip]

Assessment approaches:

Formative

During learning ask the students the following so that their progress can be monitored in an ongoing way:

  • Which elements of music are being contrasted and how in the piece heard?

  • How are the sections heard in a piece of music different from each other?

  • What would be the best way to show in written form, the contrasts heard in a piece of music?

  • Which written symbols were understood better by others and why?

  • How can you make an interesting new composition from the written chart you have formed as a group?

Group assessment of the following possible focus learning outcome:

"Create and present to others a group composition based on a specific chart that represents structural contrast."

Key question

To what extent does the group's chart represent musical contrasts and how does the heard composition relate to the chart?

Criteria for assessment could include:

  • identification of a range of contrasts presented on the chart;
  • identification of the extent to which the heard composition represents the written intentions of the chart.

Possible strategies for assessment:

  • peer and teacher discussion;
  • teacher-presented written feedback to each group.
