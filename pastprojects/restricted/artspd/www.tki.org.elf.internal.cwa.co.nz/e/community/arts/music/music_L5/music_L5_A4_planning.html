Level 5: Exploring and Using Structural Devices

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Key teaching and learning concepts

  • Structural devices – enable the different ideas in a piece of music to be put together and relate to each other.

  • Motif – a brief, identifiable musical idea that may be repeated or developed throughout a work.

  • Sequence – the repetition of a musical phrase at a higher or lower pitch.

  • Repetition and variation – two contrasting ways of developing ideas in music.

Prior learning

Students need to have:

  • an understanding that patterns and structures are used to build a piece of music;

  • an understanding of the elements of music and how to use them in compositions;

  • completed a range of focused listening activities in which they identified a range of musical features including patterns.

Suggested learning outcomes

The students will:

  • experiment with, select and combine the structural devices of motif, sequence, repetition and variation to create a brief piece of music (DI);

  • identify structural devices used in own and others compositions (PK).

[tip]

Ideas for activities

Students can:

  • Take a collection of pitches like the pentatonic or whole tone scale. Put some of the pitches in an order to create a short melodic idea then give this short idea a rhythm. (Alternatively, start with a rhythm idea and then add a collection of pitches to it). Call the idea a motif. Play the motif, then play it again either at a pitch higher or lower. Identify this as a sequence and notice how this device offers a way of varying and developing ideas in music. Play the original motif four times. Do this again but this time on the third playing, vary the motif slightly (perhaps vary one pitch or one part of the rhythm).
    Think of this 4x (same, same, different, same) pattern as a phrase or segment of a potential composition. Play this 4x-pattern again and then try playing it all down a pitch or up a pitch. Identify that sequence has been used as a way to relate phrases or segments to each other. Try other variations of the 4x pattern e.g., same, different, same, different.

  • Listen to a range of music that offers clear examples of the use of motif, sequence, repetition and variation, for example:

    • Stravinsky, Dance of the Youths and Maidens from Rite of Spring – opening 8 bars make use of a rhythm motif which is both repeated and yet varied, with different accents

    • Bach, Prelude no.1 from 48 Preludes and Fugues – use of 8-note motif sustained throughout the piece through repetition and variation

    • Tim Rice and Andrew Lloyd Webber, "Heaven on their minds" from Jesus Christ Superstar, – "Nazareth your famous son..." downward sequence, and slight variation of motif at end.

    • Strike, Taku Manu E – rhythm motif (same, same, same, different) is repeated many times and eventually varied through increase of tempo

    • Jazz standard, C Jam Blues – use of repeating motif as entire melody line.


  • Find examples of the structural devices in their own listening. Bring the music to class and share the examples with others. Discuss them.

Ideas for assessment

Formative

During the learning experiences ask the students the following so that their progress can be monitored in an ongoing way:

  • What musical elements did you use to create your motif?


  • How can you vary your motif?


  • What is the effect when you strictly repeat your ideas as opposed to varying them?


  • How many times did you hear a particular motif in a piece?


  • How did the composer vary the motif or other ideas in the piece?


  • If you heard a sequence, was it based on a pitch above or below the original ?

Assessment Approaches

Assessment task:

"Either individually or in pairs, compose a brief piece that makes use of the structural devices of motif, sequence, repetition and variation."

After plenty of experimentation with motif, sequence, repetition, and variation, either individually or in pairs, the students compose a short piece that uses most or all of the structural devices. Try to make the different phrases or segments of the piece relate to each other through the devices used. Record the piece or practise it to play it live. Have the class listen to the piece, then listen again while the composer(s) describe the structural devices used.

Key question:

  • How effectively has(ve) the student(s) been able to use the structural devices of motif, sequence, repetition, variation to create a brief composition?

Criteria:

excellence structural devices and musical ideas combine to form an effective, brief piece.
merit structural devices and musical ideas relate to each other to form a brief piece.
credit structural devices and musical ideas relate at times to form a brief piece.
not yet achieved structural devices and musical ideas are difficult to follow.

Strategies:

Teacher observation through formative reflective questioning and practical experimentation activities.

Self and peer assessment of task based on agreed criteria.
