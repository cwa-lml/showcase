Level 2: Creating Musical Ideas from the Community

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Key teaching and learning concepts

  • Tempo – the speed of the beat in music

  • Dynamics – from doc

  • Rhythm – duration of notes forming patterns

  • Tunes – series of pitched notes

  • A musical idea – a short series of notes that make a musical statement eg either a rhythm idea/pattern or melodic idea/pattern

Prior learning

Students need to have:

  • listened to and viewed a range of music from the school community and beyond;

  • played and sung a range of different music representing the make-up of the community;

  • experimented with improvising short musical ideas;

  • discussed musical ideas from music they have listened to.

Suggested learning outcomes

The students will:

  • listen to and identify musical ideas heard in music around the community (PK);

  • play short tunes on instruments, with control over dynamics, tempo, rhythm, pitch, and physical coordination (CI);

  • share an original tune based on ideas gathered from the community with others by playing it on instrument/s (DI, CI);

  • share ideas about music they listen to and perform from the community (UC).

Ideas for activities

Create rhythm patterns around names of trees in the local environment.
For example, ngaio, rangiora, totara, rimu.

  • The students could individually or as a group:
    • Chant the words
    • Use body percussion to play the rhythm of the words
    • Use untuned instruments to play the rhythm of the words
    • Say/play the words at the same time
    • Say/play the words using different tempos, dynamics, pitch
    • Organise the word patterns in some way based on their exploration. For example:
      • first time through – chanting all the words together
      second time through – different groups chanting different words at the same time.
      third time through – different groups playing word patterns on different instruments.
      fourth time through – same as above but with increasing or decreasing dynamics.
  • Throughout this process, encourage students to share their explorations with others. Encourage feedback on how the chant sounds.

Ask
Show me all the different ways you can say/play your word pattern?
Which way was the most interesting way of saying your word pattern?
Why?
Which way was the most interesting way of playing your word pattern?
Why?
Which instruments/sound sources best suited your rhythm pattern?

Create rhythm patterns around a sports chant.

  • Listen to and recreate some sports chants that may be heard in the local community, e.g., school sports chant
    chants about particular players, for example Oo Ah Umaga (Wellington and All Black rugby player).

  • Discuss the purpose of sports chants, e.g., to encourage the player to develop a positive crowd atmosphere.

  • Decide what sorts of words could be used in a sports chant.

Ask
What are the different ways you can say these words to create a great atmosphere at a sports game? (consider dynamics, tempo)

  • In small groups, get students to create their own sports chant. Encourage students to incorporate movement and body percussion sounds like claps, clicks, claps, stamps, jumps.

    Sometimes it is useful to provide a framework to for the students to create within. This gives guidelines and also focuses on particular musical elements that may need to be included.
    For example:
    • your chant is 4 lines long
    • the chant will need to become louder near the end
    • you need to use body percussion sounds as well as your voice
    • you need to use movements that give a sense of excitement

However, it is also important to encourage a variety of approaches to an activity. A framework may not be appropriate for all students.

Ask
What sounds can you use to make your chant sound better?
What movements can you use to make your chant look better?
Are there places where you need to change the tempo or the dynamics

  • Provide opportunities for the students to share their chants – between groups within the class or in a syndicate/school assembly. Encourage feedback from the audience.

  • For further ideas about sports chants, see Music Education Standard 2 - Form 2 pp. 110 – 113 and Choose Music pp. 112 – 113.
Create a simple tune based around a cellphone ring.
  • Listen to a range of cellphone rings - discuss which rings sound good/bad and why? Explore what are the essential ingredients of an effective cellphone ring, e.g., short, repetitive, able to be heard easily.

Ask
Are the tunes long?
Are the tunes played on one or more instruments?
Is there any change in tempo, dynamics, tone colour?
Are there rhythm patterns?

  • Get the students to use tuned instruments to explore repeating melodic patterns using a range of notes. Start with 3 or 4 notes. Here are three possible approaches:

    1. Base the melodic patterns around a word using the letters of the C scale
      e.g., DAG, EDGE.
      e.g., 1© 2(D) 3(E) 4(F) 5(G) 6(A)7(B) 8 (C)
      These numbers represent the eight notes of any scale. In this example, the C major scale has been used.
      Play with a rhythm pattern that is catchy.

    2. It may be useful to use the five notes of a pentatonic scale.
      e.g., C pentatonic scale - C, D, E, G, A , C,
      See Into Music 1, Classroom Music in Years 1 - 3, (Playing and Creating and Representing chapters).

    3. Another approach is chance music. Make a large dice and on five of the faces, write the notes names of the five notes on any pentatonic scale. On the sixth face, write an X sign meaning silence. Toss the dice and use the notes that turn up to create melodic patterns. See Our Music pp. 55 - 56 for more details.

Ask

What pattern sounds the best? Why?

What are the different ways you can play that pattern? Different tempo, dynamics, pitch, rhythms?

Does the pattern sound better being played on different tuned instruments?

  • Following this exploration, get the students to individually or in pairs, use these musical patterns to create their original cell phone ring.

Ask
What patterns have you decided to use? Why?
See how many different ways you can play the patterns.
What way sounds the best? (Keep returning to the purpose of cellphone rings)

  • Throughout the process, encourage students to share their efforts with others and seek feedback.

  • If possible, programme the original tunes onto an actual cellphone or record their efforts on a tape recorder.
    It may be worthwhile sending the students' efforts to Vodafone or Telecom for feedback.

[tip]

Assessment approaches

Formative

Refer to ask questions included throughout. Use these as a means to monitor understanding and to provide feedback to students on their progress.

Summative

Focus Learning Outcome 1:

Key Question – How well do the musical ideas created by the student link to the particular stimulus they have used to develop it?

Criteria for assessment could include:

Ability to unpack the stimulus in terms of sound sources needed
Ability to organise the sounds in a way that links clearly with the stimulus.

Focus learning outcome 2

Represent the short musical idea using symbols (lines, shapes and patterns) (CI).

Focus question – How well has the student's explanation of their musical idea identified a link between the sounds heard and the symbols used?

Criteria for assessment could include:

Ability to link parts of a musical idea with particular symbols.
Ability to describe the links between sounds and symbols.

Possible strategies for assessment – observation, discussion, reflective questioning, conferencing (teacher/student, student/student).
