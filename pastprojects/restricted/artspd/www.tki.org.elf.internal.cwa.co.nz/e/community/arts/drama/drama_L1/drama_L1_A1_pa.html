Level 1: A Personal Celebration Scene

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT [tip]

Planning:

Possible contexts include a range of celebration events in the lives of the class such as:

  • a golden wedding

  • the arrival of a family member from overseas

  • a prizegiving with certificate ceremony (sports, or cultural activities)

  • a hair cutting ceremony

  • religious days, for example, Easter

  • a dinner for a special occasion, for example, Christmas Dinner

  • cultural celebrations, for example, Chinese New Year

  • the unveiling of a headstone, or other memorial

      The teacher should be aware that although an example of death is included it should only be used if it is a student suggestion. Careful explanation will ensure students understand that immediately a person dies we mourn their passing but some time later we may celebrate their life with a special occasion such as an unveiling or a scattering of ashes.

Possible strategies for exploring these contexts could include:

  • Creating a "moment" with the whole group and modelling one of the scenes with volunteer students.
  • Talking about placing, for example, Who is at the centre? How would the people in the scene stand?
  • Using starter questions, for example: [tip]
    • Who was there?
    • What were they doing?
    • What were they saying to each other?
    • What did their faces look like?
    • Are there times to be still and times to be quiet? Are there times to listen and times to talk?
    • Is there music?
    • Who is the person guiding the others or in charge of the proceedings?
    • How do we know how to behave at these celebrations?
    • What order does the celebration follow? (A simple sequence of three ideas would be appropriate.)
  • Explaining "emotion" words for example, excited, happy, sad, serious, thoughtful, pious and experiencing how they are shown in facial expressions, sounds and body movements. This can be done by asking the students to remember something that made them feel happy and tell about it. Then they can practise showing what they did with their bodies when it happened. Contextualise this by making it a game. Blow out the candles "as if..." you had just recieved a suprise.
  • Discussing whose birthday it is and how they are made to look and feel important.
  • Considering what else would be happening at the time and what the environment could look like, for example: speeches, decorations, seating arrangements.

Once students are placed in the scene to represent the people in the celebration they could suggest:

  • what they might say (based on the discussion they have had);
  • what gestures they might make, for example feeling nervousness, anticipation, or relief;
  • how they might walk to show their feelings.

Students could the develop their own individual scenes by:

  • sharing in pairs, for example, guest and host – the moment of arrival and greeting;
  • reacting to teacher in role as a whole class. For example, the teacher may take the role of a photographer who has come to record the event. This provides an opportunity to deepen discussion on what is happening at the party.
  • Playing "artist and clay" game by moulding a partner into a body shape, for example, feeling anxious. Here it is important to preteach students that their clay is magic and that even though they should move their hands very close to it, it will only take shape if they do not touch it. The person being the clay must watch very closely to see how they will be moulded and try to become the shape the artist is showing.
  • Developing scenes in groups of four, for example, introducing a special visitor

Assessment

Assessment activities could include:

  • Teacher's observation of practical evidence of appropriate or enthusiastic facial expression, gesture, and movement to express feelings and show actions.

  • Self and peer assessment to address ways in which the sense of celebration was conveyed, observed, and interpreted. For example What did you see when...? What did he/she do when...? What else did you think of when...? This could be written, drawn, or discussed.

  • Individual, peer, and teacher assessment could monitor developing skills at listening, working with others, remaining involved with the task.

  • The scene could be recorded in still images or on video. This recording could provide a resource/s for discussion. For example, What do you notice about the way the people stand? Did students try out facial expression and gesture with confidence?

  • Observation of the student activity on video to assess visible levels of student engagement in the scene

  • Observation of student discussion to clarify which students can speak about new ideas

  • Assessment of associated written/visual responses. For example, pictures of the performance, a mosaic of the most important ideas.
