Level 3: Contrasting Roles

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Planning

This learning example explores the concept of status and how status can be shown dramatically using the techniques of voice, gesture, movement, facial expression, and language.

Some possible contexts might be:

  • Looking at a society removed in time or space. Exploring the different roles in that society, for example, leaders, warriors, merchants, explorers, farmers, village dwellers, servants and roles within families, for example, male and female roles.
  • The different roles people play in their own lives, for example, a student is a member of a family, a student in class, a friend, a member of a particular cultural group.
  • The students could discuss the status roles in the school. They could also discuss well-known TV programmes and how status is used in those for example, The Simpsons, Shortland St.
  • Exploring the relationships between characters in literature, for example, C S Lewis' Narnia stories, Harry Potter stories, Roald Dahl's Matilda. This unit could also use medieval society or local history as a context.

    The drama work should not start until students have some knowledge of this society, that is, prior knowledge will be required to look at the people in the society. This could be established through sharing a story; bringing in pieces of history in the way of letters, myths or historical accounts; or viewing videos about the time and discussing them.

Possible contexts include:

  • Using pictures as starters
  • Study medieval prints which illustrate medieval life, for example Breugel, and prior knowledge; Artchive (a website to help you find 16 of Breugel's paintings to choose from)


  • Students brainstorm a list of people found in medieval society.


  • Role card continuums

    • Using roles drawn from the list distribute role cards at random to the class.

    Students arrange themselves in order from highest status to lowest status.

    Students discuss their placing on the line with the person next on the line.

    Each student speaks briefly about their place in society – who they are above and below.

  • Role-on-the-wall

    • Students devise a list of the key levels of medieval society, for example, ruler, nobles, knights, merchants, clergy, guildsmen, small landowners, minstrels, peasants, beggars, criminal, and outcasts.

    Teacher divides the class into equal groups and gives each group a role card from the list.

    Groups create a role-on-the-wall of their person and one member of the group physically represents that role.

    Group presents their character to the class talking about what they might wear, how they would stand and what they would do and say.

  • Small group and whole group role play, signing through drawing and ritual

    • This activity uses a large group gathering as its setting, for example a tournament.

    Teacher distributes roles for people who might be at the gathering. These roles should cover as many levels of society as possible. To deepen engagement it is possible for the teacher to enter in role as a court steward in charge of the organisation of the tournament.

    Whole class discusses the tournament and what activities would take place there, for example, "Which ritual would be associated with these activities?" It may be the herald summoning the knights to a joust or the presentation of trophies to the victor.

    The class divides up into smaller groups involved in activities, for example, a knight, his squire and his page preparing his armor for the tournament; the King's court making an entrance to their marquee; a merchant showing off his new garments to a group of admiring onlookers; local yeomen selling mead; a band of wandering minstrels packing away their show in order to watch the tournament; serfs arguing over a small strip of common land; monks preparing to bless the participants in the tournament; a criminal in the stocks being punished for the crime of stealing.

    Divide into family groupings, for example, a noble family, a merchant's family, a yeoman's family, a peasant family, members of the church. Each individual in the group creates a quick line drawing of an object their group might use. Group uses the objects as symbols in a brief role play about the morning before the tournament. Each group rehearses what they would do, how they might stand, move and speak in the scene at the tournament.

    Whole class sequences their involvement and rehearses ways in which the scenes might join together.

    Whole class creates three final scenes, for example, the ritual of the knights entering the jousting field and the audience response; a sentence-at-a-time story to tell of the decisive moment in the tournament, which may be moved in slow motion to music; a simple five line dialogue with action and gesture, between the winner and the King at the moment when the prize is presented.

Assessment

Assessment activities may include:

  • Students will choose and use drama techniques to convey the status and/ or feelings of a role they have selected. For example, after close watching they may reflect upon physical levels used, varying speeds of movement, positioning of different social groups in the space, tone of voice, choice of words and volume near the end of a first rehearsal of their small group scenes. This may be done as verbal or written response. It is particularly useful to develop response sheets for students to fill in so that they know exactly what they are looking for in a drama.
  • They may repeat this exercise to add further discoveries after they have sequenced the group scenes.
  • Results may form part of a joint journal of findings to be displayed in the classroom. Students will watch another's portrayal of a role and comment on the techniques used and the information conveyed. The teacher may also choose to comment.

In this way self-, peer- and teacher-assessment can reflect on links observed between the subjects explored in process (the material), the techniques used, and the messages communicated.
