How do I create a supportive environment for students to express their views?

Phil:
I am really interested in assessment and how to gather formative assessment information. I recognise that students need feedback about what they have and have not achieved as well as genuine praise. It's sometimes hard, however, to provide students with clear goals, and a clear idea of what these goals look like, while at the same time motivating them, yet not giving them too much praise. I'm encouraging my students to talk about what they are trying to learn. My first goal being to reach, with them, a shared understanding of what we are doing. What sort of strategies do you suggest that I put in place to ensure that these discussions are supportive and create a safe environment for students to express their views?

Merryn Dunmill:
If we focus on listening today it will hopefully provide an assessment model for other music learning areas. Formative is, as we all know ongoing, observational, interactive, responsive and reflective. Our students need to be, as you point out, part of these processes, to own their learning, monitor their progress, set goals and challenges, and negotiate strategies to improve. With listening, there are many aspects to consider in terms of monitoring student learning: e.g., 'The Hall of Mountain King'

  • how well can they use the language of music/music vocabulary to identify and describe what they are hearing? e.g., 'there were more instruments playing as it went through and it got louder and faster and really exciting.' The focus here shows this student's ability to focus on texture, dynamics and tempo. The teacher uses these key words/elements of music to consolidate (not praise) e.g., 'Yes, the dynamics got louder and tempo got faster. Do you think that hearing more instruments play made this happen? What might be happening to make the music sound exciting? What instruments can you hear playing?'
  • can the students find the beat to the music and maintain the beat as the tempo changes? Here repeated playing of the music are necessary with a range of approaches to keep up interest, e.g., patting knees, moving (from a curled up ball to a dancing troll! Jumping to the beat).
  • more advanced students may notice a pattern and be able to tap it back to you – the rhythmic ostinato/pattern that repeats over and over in this music. All students can learn this pattern and play on instruments.

Feedback to students at these process points should be motivating, e.g., "I noticed that we managed to remember the first part of the rhythm. How can we remember the whole pattern so that we can play it clearly with the music?" (You could for example, add names of people in the class, just a few, whose names match the rhythm such as Hana, Peter, Miriam, Miriam, Miriam Tessa, Sasha, Hone, Tusi, Maddy, Jason, Sue".

You could ask these questions: "When is it easy to keep the beat? When does it get harder to keep the beat? How might we move/play the beat to match the way it sounds on the stereo?

You could write the following summary on the board:

  • What were the easy things to hear in today's music?
  • What was harder to identify? e.g. instruments.
  • Tomorrow's challenge is to listen again and see if anyone can hear something new in the music and share it with the class.
  • Research question: find out about some instruments that play in an orchestra. (To support this, you could gather some pictures, CD tracks and Internet live players into the school etc.)

The next assessment, i.e., the next orchestral piece of music listened to and discussed, will draw out the learning, and ongoing monitoring of learning is obvious with this new application. You are looking for students at this age to not only recognise (first formative stage) but to identify and describe sound across all the elements.

Take one element at a time as a focus and choose music that allows for this. TONE COLOUR: Tihore Mai for example has female voices, a karanga weka (M&257;ori flute), and poi. Ask questions such as:

  • how are these used?
  • what effect do they have?
  • how are these instruments different to the orchestra?

Phil:
I really like the way you suggest a progression. That is, 1) 'On the board a summary of easy things to hear in today's music can be made and those that were harder to identify e.g. instruments'. 2) 'Tomorrow's challenge is to listen again and see if anyone can hear something new in the music and share it with the class. A little research could be to find out some instruments that play in an orchestra, pictures, and CD tracks Internet live players into the school etc all help. The next assessment i.e., the next orchestral piece of music listened to and discussed will draw out the learning, and ongoing monitoring of learning is obvious with this new application'.

Merryn Dunmill:
To finish your question about listening to strategies Phil, the old "OK" to each response.

Saying there is no right or wrong is a good explorative and provocative statement. If the answer is not on the right track then say 'let's listen again and see how we hear it this time, because as we listen again, we hear things differently, which is exciting. Put your listening ears on everyone and let's work out how we hear things this time.' Or simply, 'listen again and put your hand up if you hear something different this time.'

Be aware of peer pressure as stronger leaders dominate and unsure students simply follow. Another strategy when you can't entice shy students to contribute after all is to say, " Hands up if you agree as a group approach. Each group can have a card with an element on it, e.g., tempo/speed of the music, another will have dynamics etc. They all listen but for only one aspect. The group has a chat and comes up with a response for the class on what they heard after agreeing on their ideas to share. Eventually, after rotating around these 'stations' over a few pieces of music, confidence grows and individuals are better able to respond in a meaningful way. Listening/aural perception takes time and concentration, teacher guidance and focus, shared language and understandings. There is always room for difference of opinion to some degree, e.g., how it made you feel, or tricky instruments to identify.

Phil:
So, what is really important is getting that first step underway, getting a common vocabulary and clear goals so that everyone knows what everyone else is talking about.

Merryn Dunmill:
Thank you Phil for your succinct summary.

What does 'sonorities' mean?

Carol:
I have had a question put to me from a teacher in the South Island. She asks 'in the curriculum statement, page 61, it states: 'Listen to selected instruments and discuss their sonorities...' When I looked in the glossary, I could not find the word sonorities. Can you help me?'

Merryn Dunmill:
Re instrument sonorities – really why and how one instrument sounds different from another. Just as we all look different to each other. Each sound source (not just conventional instruments either, e.g., electronic, environmental) has special qualities that make it unique. Wood is different to metal and even within the wood, the actual type of wood makes a difference. That's why Stradivarius violins were the world's best – the craftsman and his wood, the way he shaped each fragment of the instrument, the placement of its parts, the thickness and length of the strings and of the fingerboard etc. The height of the bridge. Ah, such architecture and technology and aesthetic pleasure all combining to make sound sources for the enjoyment of humans, plants and animals...and.....

Do you need experimentation or is questioning enough to produce an appreciation of quality in students?

Win:
One of the many challenging aspects of formative assessment is developing children's 'nose for quality'. Does this occur through experimentation or is questioning enough?

Merryn Dunmill:
I always encourage kids of all ages to reflect on their experimentation's and explorations – which sound did you like? Why? Where could you use this sound? After this type of questioning students begin reflecting critically on their own craft. Can you tell me about the sounds you are using/hearing? Talk about why you chose those.

To be able to stand before your peers and describe music with confidence, without teacher prompting is an ultimate goal. As students develop the vocabulary and aural skills others can be encouraged, stimulated and educated by their peers. I think parents should have a music appreciation class with the children, so that they can listen to them talk about the way they hear. To be able to carry on conversations at home makes a healthy listening environment for all aspects of life.
