Introduction

Sarah Wilson:
This week we are un-wrapping the latest collection of New Zealand Curriculum Exemplars for The Arts.

The new arts exemplars have been distributed to your school and are also now online at http://www.tki.org.nz/r/assessment/exemplars/arts/index_e.php

The exemplars are samples of authentic student work annotated to illustrate learning, achievement, and quality in relation to levels 1–5 of The Arts in the New Zealand Curriculum.

This will be a great opportunity to explore ideas with others from The Arts Online community about how to make the most of this rich collection of exemplars in your classrooms.

Joining us online is Tracey McLennan, Senior Project Officer for Teacher and Leadership Capability for the Ministry of Education.


Background information

Sarah Wilson:
What is an arts exemplar?
The purposes of the arts exemplars are to:

  • illustrate key features of learning, achievement, and quality at different stages of student development
  • help students and teachers to identify the next learning steps
  • guide teachers in their interpretation of curriculum levels.

Availability of the arts exemplars
The arts exemplars materials are available in three formats:

  • print
  • online
  • two CD-ROMs.
In all of the dance, drama, and music exemplars, and some of the visual arts, video clips have been used to capture important sequences during the students’ work over a period of time, as well as those elements, such as performing movement or singing, which cannot be displayed in a static image.

To give teachers maximum access to video clips, the arts exemplar materials are being provided on two CD-ROMs (in a dual high-impact case), as well as online. Note that It is essential to view the video clips on the CD-ROMs or online when working with the print materials for drama, dance, and music.

A pack, which includes the arts exemplars print materials and CD-ROMs, has now been sent to your school and the online resources were made live on the 1st November at http://www.tki.org.nz/r/assessment/exemplars/arts/index_e.php

If you haven’t seen a copy of the resource yet make sure you track it down! The materials are wonderfully rich and quite inspirational.

Topic: Exemplars and ‘Unwrapping the arts’ units

Angela:
I teach drama from year 9–11 at Stratford High School. I have had a good look at the exemplars and they look very useful. Will we be getting any drama exemplars at level 5?

Simon Chiaroni:
In answer to your question, Angela, yes, there is a level 5 exemplar being worked on at present. The topic is Parihaka, and it's hoped it'll be sent out to schools and up on the Web early next year.

Angela:
Sounds great, especially a resource based on life under our wonderful old mountain!

It would be great if the exemplar idea was extended to cover the sample tasks/units included in the 'unwrapping the arts' material on TKI... any thoughts?

Simon Chiaroni:
I see the ‘Unwrapping the Arts’ units having a different purpose to the exemplars. The sample units are examples of teaching activities that are analysed to show how they meet the curriculum aims, strands and AOs [achievement objectives]... whereas the exemplars are examples of students' work that are annotated to show and discuss the learning that's taken place. So they are different, but closely related too! And there will be lots of connections possible between the two.

Sarah Wilson:
This idea of connections between the resources available on TKI is one that will, I think, continue to develop as links between different resources, collections, and complementary areas as they continue to be created.

Angela:
Simon, I appreciate the differences between Unwrapping the Arts and the exemplars... I just thought, after seeing the exemplars, that it might be interesting to see the units taken one step further with trialling and outcomes recorded. I know that the exemplars project is still growing ... just a thought for the future.

Tracey McLennan:
I think that the reason that the Arts is in such an exciting position at the moment, is that we have such a rich array of resources at our finger-tips, both in print and online media. As Simon points out, ‘The Arts Unwrapped’ is different to the exemplars and serves quite different purposes, but seen as part of a package for teachers, they are very valuable. There seems to be many different directions that we can point people in at the moment.

Topic: Sharing exemplars with students

Tracey McLennan:
I would be interested in knowing if any of you have shared exemplars yet with parents/students and what their response has been?

Shirley:
I’ve been working with the exemplars recently with Carol, and looking for ways to help teachers find the resources that they need so they can implement our long-term planning. The exemplars have got really useful annotation. It helps to read these and we like the modelling. It helps our teachers to ask the right questions, to look for the teachable moment. It helps us to speak meaningfully and help the student grow towards their own expressive creativity.

The work itself helps us to judge the levels we must try to reach. The videos are fantastic and the instructions about using them are very clear.

Simon Chiaroni:
Thanks, Angela. One of the thoughts behind the exemplars is that teachers will view them with students. Would you find it helpful do you think to view and discuss the students' reflections in the level 4 exemplars with your students? Or do you think that might be a bit intimidating or off-putting for them?

Angela:
Intimidating? No, I think quite the opposite. My students find it a real confidence booster when they see other students doing stuff. It's not just me telling them they can do it – I have evidence!

Simon Chiaroni:
Thanks, Angela, I find that really heartening! Also, high (but realistic) expectations from teachers are such an important component in students' self-esteem and achievement.

Tracey McLennan:
Good point here Simon. I'm a firm believer that sometimes students and teachers need to step up to the mark and view material that may well be beyond them at that present moment in time. Not to totally freak them out, but to be used as a tool to show extension opportunities, ‘where to from here’. In this respect the progression of clips are really useful I think.

Topic: Wider uses of exemplars

Tracey McLennan:

I agree with you Tracey. When I was travelling around the country last year to some of our more rural schools, some teachers were daunted by the standards on the resource videos. Topic: Managing arts resources across the school

Sarah Wilson:
Can we help out with an answer to this? Sue wasn't able to make it on but has emailed me the following question: if you feedback I will contact her tomorrow and make sure she can log in to read the answers

“We had one question here which was, if all the school is doing the same art strand, how can resources be managed effectively so they can be utilised by all? Short of spending money, of course.

Tracey McLennan:
It wouldn't be the ideal, I don't think, to have all the school working in this way. At the same time, is it totally unavoidable? Even if the progression was slightly out, i.e. one group ahead of the other, that would certainly work better for resource sharing, as well as the excellent peer-feedback that could also occur after lessons. We have much to learn from each other, and it would be a shame if this could not be shared and then feedback utilised to make shifts/changes.

Carol Langton:
This is an excellent moment for the exemplars to walk in, stage right. For planning purposes, there are no better or more accessible resource along with the MOE resource packs for each discipline.

As far as physical resources go, well, that is why a good overview is necessary when planning for the year...stretching resources (everyone's fair share) is vital to peace of mind for the staff. I always used to start my music units with the making of percussive and stringed instruments and then added a few really good instruments to the final ensemble.

Innovation is the name of the game in a school where the resources are thin on the ground.

Simon Chiaroni:
First of all, what a fantastic problem to have, so many users that the arts resources are stretched!!

I'm not sure of your schools' circumstances, but one possibility might be team teaching, where each teacher prepares a module/activity based on a particular resource and then works with different classes at different times to teach it. Takes a bit of co-ordination, but the students get to experience different teachers' approaches and a range of activities/units.
