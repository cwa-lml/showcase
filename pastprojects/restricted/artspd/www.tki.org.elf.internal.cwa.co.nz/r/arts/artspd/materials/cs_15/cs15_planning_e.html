Printmaking – Makarewa School, Invercargill

Introduction | Organisation/Planning | Content | Issues | Evaluation/Reflection

Organisation

In preparation for this unit, Dianne and the students discussed their motivational focus: the historical significance and visual impact of Pegg's Cottage in their community.

Down the road from the school, there is an old 1900s dilapidated cottage that used to be the home of an Australian Aboriginal called Bert Pegg. The battle to preserve the old place is ongoing, but the teacher, Dianne Cowan, and the students decided they would preserve it forever by visiting the site, making observational drawings of the cottage, and then creating ink monoprints of their drawings.

Pegg's Cottage

Together they researched the history of Pegg's cottage using a text from their own school library. The story of Pegg falling asleep on his daily return home from work, while sitting on his wagon and holding the reins of his horse caught the attention and imagination of the children. The students then visited the site where the cottage was in the last stages of collapse. At the site the students were asked to make observational drawings of the cottage and it's surrounds in their art journal. These drawings were the reference point for their printmaking.

For her artist's model, Dianne used the work of artist Jo Oglier from the Ministry of Education resource, Exploring the Visual Arts in Years 1–6: Printmaking. Dianne and the class discussed how Jo used observational drawing in her work. They also studied her printmaking style.

Integrated curriculum links:

  • Social Studies strand: Culture and Heritage
  • Science – botanical, observational drawings

Dianne made sure that the printmaking materials were in workable condition and there was equipment for each student in the class. Time was spent teaching the class good printmaking standards: how to organise their work space and how to look after the equipment (for example, putting inks away, cleaning brayers (rollers), wearing art shirts) and how to share drying space.

The printmaking tools included:

  • printing inks
  • particle board
  • lino cutters
  • brayers
  • newspaper

Planning

Printmaking Pegg's Cottage was taught through a series of sequential lessons. Dianne followed the resource Exploring the visual arts– years 1–6 Printmaking:(pp 6–13).
Her specific learning intentions were for the students to:

Develop ideas (DI)
  • research the story of Pegg and his life
  • work from observation (flowers/bush and cottage/ cabbage tree)
  • discuss with the teacher the ways that their drawings and prints could evoke a feeling.
Develop practical knowledge, skills and drawing techniques (PK)
  • render observational drawings
  • develop monoprints of flowers/trees
  • develop monoprints of Pegg's Cottage
  • frame the images
  • use contrasting shapes for emphasis
  • balance with small amounts of negative space
  • repeat shapes to create movement in the image (page 12).
Communicate and interpret their ideas as they proceed (CI)
  • understand the process (drawing - print block - printmaking - print)
  • modify their work according to feedback and self- evaluation
  • discuss the works of a New Zealand artist (Jo Olgier) and the context within which they were created.
Develop an understanding that art often reflects what is important to the artist and to the local community, such as the preservation of a historical site (UC)
  • study the Jo Ogier work in the Ministry of Education resource, Exploring the Visual Arts –Years 1–6: Printmaking
  • to feel proud, to shine, to value their work.

The school exhibition
The aim was for each student to have two pieces of work in the exhibition. At the least, every student to display one piece of work.

  • to participate, to feel confident
  • to learn how to be an audience (as it could be the first time some of the students have seen an art exhibition).
