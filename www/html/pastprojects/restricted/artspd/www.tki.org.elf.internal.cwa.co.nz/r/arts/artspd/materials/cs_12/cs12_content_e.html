Painting living creatures – one Bay of Plenty teacher inspires another

Introduction | Organisation/Planning | Content | Issues | Evaluation/Reflection

Content

The sequence below helped the students meet the achievement objectives.

Students:

  • gathered and investigated images of animals and insects from a range of sources to generate ideas;


  • developed these ideas into sketches, using observational drawing techniques;


  • modified their sketches by simplifying or exaggerating animal or insect features (that is, simple wing shapes, twisting shark shapes), to create a motif style;


  • composed the painting by planning background and foreground images;


  • decided where to place repeat images in relation to background size, direction, and rotation;


  • transferred motifs onto A3 cartridge paper, using chalk;


  • mixed colours and blocked in areas of paint to fill the A3 page;


  • painted the whole composition, adding finer detail;


  • repeated the process for foreground objects;


  • revisited their work several times to adapt and refine it by adding details and patterns;


  • added three-dimensional images that protruded from the work;


  • added other materials, such as harakeke/flax and glued polystyrene shapes, to add depth to the work;


  • framed the paintings (students were also encouraged to think about a patterned border drawing on Māori art symbols);


  • discussed their own and others' work.
"I wanted the students to pay attention to detail and show their fish in action, not looking still and lifeless.

"I also used the technique of looking at what is at the centre of attention and how we could focus on this through overlapping, repetition, and rotation."

Andy Dunseath

Assessment

To assess learning outcomes, teachers considered finished work in terms of how well students:

  • made simplified motifs from their sketches;


  • developed their ideas in imaginative ways;


  • showed more understanding of painting techniques;


  • used overlapping and perspective to show movement in the work;


  • used Māori design elements to decorate their frames;


  • discussed their own and others' work.


Assessment strategies

Teachers:

  • made observations and responded orally to each child;


  • wrote evaluations for each child on a range of learning outcomes.


For example, Learning Outcome 4 specifies that "students used overlapping and perspective to show movement in the work". To assess this learning outcome, teachers had to:

  • know about, and teach, overlapping and perspective;


  • observe how students used these techniques;


  • discuss students' ideas and techniques with them as their work progressed and was modified.

Questions teachers posed to get students discussing their work

It is important to spend time with students discussing, communicating, and interpreting the visual arts. Prompt them with questions such as:

  • What techniques are you using to show the difference between foreground and background?


  • Explain how your painting shows movement.


For help with questioning, go to "Questions and Discussion Points (CI)" on pages 9 and 20 of the Ministry of Education's visual arts curriculum guideline

Painting – exploring the visual arts in Years 1-6 (2001).
Wellington: Learning Media.

As part of a series of five books, this teacher guide was sent to all schools, and features more of Frances Patete and her Pongakawa students' artwork (pages 12 to 16).

Also check out the glossary and list of useful teaching and learning resources on pages 27 to 28.

For more ideas on visual arts assessment, go to The Arts Online http://www.tki.org.nz/r/assessment/exemplars/arts/visarts/d_2a_e.html
