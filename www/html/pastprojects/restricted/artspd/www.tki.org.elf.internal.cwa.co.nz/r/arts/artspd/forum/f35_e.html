Excerpts from forum discussion with Wayne Flanagan (Drama teacher and The Arts Online advisory team member)
4 November 2002

Val Duthie:
Kia ora, welcome to the final drama forum for this year with Wayne Flanagan (drama teacher and the Arts Online advisory team member). Wayne will be discussing drama planning for 2003.

Wayne writes, 'The best teaching occurs within a context. Instead of trying to teach what an 'element' is, let it evolve from an action you are actively involved with. You may be improvising in class whether a mining company should be allowed to operate in your town (e.g., Waihi). Students will become different characters – mayor, housewife, teacher, and miner. Now you can talk about their 'role,' 'action,' 'focus', etc. Note that any space may be used for such a drama so you need not be limited by classroom confines. Read the orange books, The Arts in the New Zealand curriculum and Drama in the classroom. Watch the video. Dare to trust yourself. It always works out. Break a leg!'

Wayne Flanagan:
Planning for drama will make sure that you and your students have a fun 2003. Have a look at your orange books to find out what you should be teaching. If you don't understand ask me any question you like. Think of a context to teach within. It might be a local issue to try process drama or it might be a movie like 'Harry Potter' or a book or whatever. Let's throw some ideas around and see where we end up.

Can you suggest how I can plan a unit with a personal stories class theme?

Tim:
I'm to be teaching a year 5 group next year and they will be older than the children I am working with this year. I thought we would use Paroa School's work as a basis for our planning (p.14, Drama in the Classroom). How do you suggest I plan for this? Can you suggest other ways that I can work with a personal stories theme with this class?

Wayne Flanagan:
Yes this would be a great way to plan a unit.

  1. Ask the students to bring in a photo from home
  2. In small groups, they can retell the circumstances of their photos
  3. Then create a storyboard of their stories focussing on the beginning, middle and end (simple pictures are great with captions)
  4. Create a freeze frame of these 3 stages (demonstrate the focus of the freeze frame)
  5. Decide on roles/characters – Use 'role on the wall' convention to create depth of characters and 'hot seating', if you please
  6. Rehearse beginning and ending with 'freeze frames'
  7. Introduce 'speaking thoughts aloud'
  8. Perform to peers.

This is a great starter unit that can be integrated easily across the curriculum. Terrific for the class to get to know each other and the teacher (bring in your own photos).

Tim:
What other ways can I develop the 'personal stories' theme with this class?

Wayne Flanagan: Personal stories can be extended to include others' stories. Once the kids have re-enacted their own stories have them meld one story with another.

  1. Take a character from one story and place them in a scene with a character from a different story. Make sure they have physical characteristics. Let them use simple script from their original scenes. You'll end up with some fascinating results. Trust the process.
  2. Let other characters enter the fray one at a time
  3. Discuss the use of elements and techniques
  4. Use conventions to guide their discovery
  5. Have groups develop other 'simple' cultures, with language and customs
  6. Introduce cultures to each other and see if they can work out each other's custom
  7. Reflect upon what happened.

How can we integrate this starter plan into our drama plan for next year?

Sheryl:
Good to have this opportunity to speak about planning for next year. I read your reply to Tim and wondered how we could best integrate this starter unit with our drama plan for the year. Where would you suggest we go next?

Wayne Flanagan:
After the starter outlined above you could choose a multitude of directions. Firstly, check out the Arts Curriculum book p40–43 for their learning examples and link to 'Drama in the classroom' book pp6–11. These will form a basis for 'what' to teach. Either use the planning unit at the rear of the book or use the website in Jean's reply. If you want to create your own flow-on from my previous example you may try to extend the stories idea to include other people and other cultures. Integrate your unit so that the students' research others lives using the web, library, taping parents and friends. Let them discover problems that can be 'played out' during process drama. Using this method means you will be able to cover the four strands with relative ease and all children's learning styles will be accessed making it enjoyable. Trust your instincts. The unit could travel anywhere once it begins.

Jean:
I have been looking up the planning template on Arts Online. http://www.tki.org.nz/r/arts/artspd/word/Drama_planner.doc. It has spaces for achievement objectives, learning outcomes, summative and formative assessment, links with other essential learning areas, skill development and resource materials.

  1. Have you used this template? If so, how helpful did you find it?
  2. Can you tell us how you plan ahead for drama in your school?

Wayne Flanagan:
Yes, I do use the drama unit planner template. It allows you to consider all areas of your plan and to reflect upon what you've achieved. I plan school wide for our drama classes. Students come to me in half-class sizes for six one and a half-hour lessons. This allows me to develop a continual process covering the curriculum. However some teachers ask me to plan within their unit plan contexts and this works well too. I choose to teach elements, techniques and conventions within a context so that it's easy to understand, and part of a process. If you need more specifics let me know.

How many conventions should we be planning to introduce with a year 3 group?

Sheryl:
How many conventions should we be planning to introduce with a year 3 group?

Wayne Flanagan:
Drama in the classroom states, 'Conventions are ways of working or strategies that we use to structure and sequence (or frame) dramatic action' (p.8). It is not important how many conventions that are used at year 3 or any level. It is important that conventions are used to 'deepen understanding, or add impact in drama work'. On page 40 of the Arts curriculum, one learning example suggests the use of 'Freeze–Frame images to represent specific moments from the stories'. This is a great start as it will also create a focus for the students and it is an easy instructional method that will assure you have control and the students will be clear on what you expect them to do. In fact all of the conventions work in this manner. Try them, as you will be able to establish your own balance, being aware to use them for specific purposes (e.g., the use of 'role on the wall' to deepen the understanding of a character). Pages 8–10 of 'Drama in the Classroom' list a whole bunch of them. Watch the video for clues as well.

How are activities linked to a line of enquiry?

Phil:
In an earlier forum you were discussing aesthetic education and you illustrated your comments with the following: Take for example a drama unit on Harry Potter.
Open journal: What is a wizard? What frightens us?
Line of inquiry: How might Harry Potter help us to discover who we are?
Activities: Making wands (kinaesthetic); poems (linguistic); strategy games (logistics); maps (spatial); and songs (musical).
Thus, the subject area you focus on is up to you.

Wayne, can you explain how the activities above are linked to the line of inquiry? I can understand the open journal work, and the line of inquiry section. I can see the activities as useful extension work, but I'm not sure how it links together.

Wayne Flanagan:
Thank you for mentioning aesthetic education as a method of planning. This would be a great way to prepare for 2003. Briefly, Aesthetic Education is a way to engage students with art forms. The teacher plans a unit around a work of art (Harry Potter for example, or a dance piece, visual art, play, music work...). A line of inquiry is established (how can Harry Potter help us to find the magic in our lives?). From the line of inquiry activities are created that the students engage in. At the close of the unit students view (or listen to) the art piece. The intent is that students will now be actively engaged with the artwork because they have attempted aspects of it themselves and thus they may be more open to an 'aesthetic experience'. So, Phil, the activities are supposed to be related to the artwork and the line of inquiry. In the example I used previously wands represent magic and are a great kinaesthetic beginning (remember I was also explaining the importance of using as many of the multiple intelligences as possible as a way of engaging students as well. Poetry, strategic games, maps and songs relate in the same way. In Harry Potter these activities are all used in the film). The line of inquiry is a guide in the creation of learning activities, the students never know that the question exists. Let me know if this clears it up for you.

Phil:
I'm still a bit confused. "Wands represent magic and are a great kinaesthetic beginning"? I still find this a difficult concept. If, for example, the student makes a wand (that has a notion of magic attached to it, but is in fact a 3D object that has no particular 'power' in itself), I still can't see how that increases their understanding so that they can better understand the line of inquiry. Is it that by holding a wand, children can imagine the power of the magic? I can see how the other activities can help children understand better some aspects of the inquiry.

Wayne Flanagan:
I think you are thinking a little too deeply, Phil. The wand doesn't have to help the student understand the line of inquiry. It is just a 'nice' way of introducing this unit and relates to the Harry Potter story because there are wands in the story. Certainly they will 'endow' the wand with magical qualities as children do with all of their play. One use for the wand that I use is to make it out of a pencil or pen. When you write with it, it can only write the truth. Get the children to use it when writing in their private journals. They will be 'hooked' into the unit because they are enjoying themselves.

Drama and Learning: A resource for years 9 to 11. Published by: Learning Media, Wellington 1990 for the Ministry of Education. This resource kit offers guidelines for approaching drama in the classroom, in particular how to enhance students' enjoyment and understanding of literature, and how to extend their use of language.

The videotapes illustrates, within classroom situations, some of the approaches and teaching strategies detailed in the teachers' resource book.

  1. Tell me a Story (32 min.) Using drama as a pre-reading activity, this illustrates a number of approaches discussed in the text. The starting point is Patricia Grace's short story, "It Used to be Green Once".
  2. Working in Role (40 min.) This involves extended drama activities based on Ruth Dallas's poem, "Pioneer Woman with Ferrets".

BOOKS

  • Taylor, Philip. The Drama Classroom: Action, Reflection, Transformation. New York: Routledge, 2000.
    This text summarises the most recent developments and thinking in educational drama. It's suitable for teachers wishing to explore the theory behind drama teaching.
  • Neelands, Jonathan. Beginning Drama 11–14. London: David Fulton Publishers, 1998.
    This is an easy–to–read text that was written for the National Curriculum in England and focuses on year 7–10. It can easily be adapted to a range of levels and age groups. It explains what teachers need to be able to do to teach drama.
  • Cusworth, Robyn and Jennifer Simons. Beyond the Script. Drama in the Classroom. Australia: Primary English Teaching Association, 1997.
    This book is aimed at the primary level and takes a useful, classroom–based, practical approach to drama. It looks at the elements and techniques of drama and explores some of the starting points and planning strategies for using teacher in role.
  • Burton, Bruce. Creating Drama. Melbourne: Addison Wesley Longman, Australia Pty Ltd, 1996.
    This Australian text is designed for middle secondary school programmes but contains useful material for any teacher wanting to learn more about movement, voice, characterisation, improvisation, focus, and ritual.
  • O'Neill, Cecily. Drama Worlds: A Framework for Process Drama. Portsmouth, NH: Heinemann, 1995.
    This text for primary and secondary teachers of drama defines the forms of process drama. It draws on social studies, history, and literature to provide units of work and analysis and discussion of the experience of drama. It contains a useful explanation of the range of drama forms available to teacher and student.
  • Peter, Melanie. Drama for All: Developing Drama in the Curriculum with Pupils with Special Educational Needs. Great Britain: David Fulton Publishers, 1994. This is an inspirational and accessible text that explains how to structure drama to meet the needs of students with special needs. Peter offers practical guidelines about incorporating drama into and across the curriculum.

As far as multi-level teaching is concerned drama is a great medium to integrate the whole class/school. Plan an activity as usual with clear learning objectives. This would allow all students to be actively engaged at their own level.
