Annotated Bibliography for Teacher Professional Development - Dance

Gilbert, Anne Green. (1992).Creative Dance for all ages. VA, USA:The American Alliance for Health, Physical Education, Recreation and Dance.

An excellent very readable text especially for those new to dance. This informative book is full of practical ideas and centres its framework on the elements of dance. Part one deals with organisation and implications for planning. Part two explores creative approaches to the elements of dance. However the author clearly argues that creative dance is not just about teaching steps - the study of dance concepts is essential. To this end her philosophy and pedagogy are interwoven throughout.

Jonas, Gerald. (1992). Dancing. New York: Harry N. Adams, Inc. Publishers.

From the standpoint of a broad overview of world dance this excellent book is packed full of splendid illustrations. These images underpin much of the narrative deepening the reader's appreciation. The book is founded on the premise that dance is found in all human societies and nearly everyone is involved as watcher or performer. The chapter headings provide a useful guide signalling that the book explores the functions of dance in a human society - cultural identity / fusion / mores, religious expression, social order and power, classical art, individual expression and who we are

Hanna, Judith Lynne. (1999). Partnering Dance and Education. Champaign, IL: Human Kinetics.

Although from an American perspective, the author argues for dance to be central to each student's education. Part One explores understanding dance covering the following: what dance knowledge consists of, who should teach it and the power and survival of dance. Part Two contains some pertinent chapters for teachers that deal with issues in dance education such as gender, cultural and national identity and discusses the benefits of dance education for at-risk youth. This book provides an excellent insight into the issues involved in dance education.

Lavender, Larry. (1996). Dancers Talking Dance. Champaign, IL: Human Kinetics.

This book is very useful in developing skills in appreciation/evaluation and to some extent the choreographic process (particularly related to dance strands DI & CI.) All teachers will recognise the process of observation, reflection, discussion, evaluation and recommendations, which the author applies to dance. The author outlines this critical approach, which can be applied from a variety of standpoints: viewer, maker, performer and teacher. Throughout the book there are very useful summaries at the beginning of each chapter. Theory and practice are not separated but dovetailed with practical examples throughout.

Shapiro, Sherry.(Ed.) (1998). Dance, Power and Difference. Champaign, IL: Human Kinetics.

This book is a selection of writings by eight female dance educators from around the world including an article from a New Zealand perspective. Divided into three sections, each has its own focus.

Part 1

 Explores questions of curriculum pedagogy and research.
Part 2 Tells stories informing the reader of personal histories in specific contexts.

Within this part Jan Bolwell discusses issues in dance education, addressing debates which centre around concerns of generic verses interdisciplinary, and social and cultural challenges all located in the New Zealand context.

Part 3 Focuses on the role of dance within a socio/political climate.

Critical reflection in this theory book raises issues and questions to challenge ideas and promote debate.
