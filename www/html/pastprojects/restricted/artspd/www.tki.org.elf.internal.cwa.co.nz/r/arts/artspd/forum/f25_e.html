Excerpts from forum discussion with Merryn Dunmill (National Coordinator for the Arts – Music)
26 June 2002

Val Duthie:
Kia ora and welcome to the final forum for Term 2. Merryn Dunmill (National Coordinator – Music) is our guest online. The focus this week is on music resources. Merryn has a wealth of knowledge about music resources, so this is your opportunity to ask the expert. As you are aware, Into Music 1: Classroom Music for Years 1–3 was distributed to your school at the end of 2001. Music handbooks for years 4–6 and years 7–10, plus a planning and assessment guide for teachers are currently under development. There is also a resource for secondary school: Sweet: A Taste of New Zealand Music (an audio CD, an accompanying teacher resource book and a CD-ROM) under development. Finally there is a resource on all the main genres of New Zealand music being developed for secondary schools.

Win:
I teach in a secondary school in the North Island and am aware that the music teacher in our school uses Into Music 1 as a guide to plan and prepare her lessons for secondary students. I wonder how the music handbooks for years 4–6 and years 7–10 (mentioned above) will be different from Into Music 1? When will these new resources be sent to schools?

Merryn Dunmill:
Yes, it is interesting how many secondary schools want Into Music 1 which they are adapting by using different musical contexts, but covering many of the same skills and knowledge bases because of the wide range of abilities in any one class. This is, of course, a good thing to do as the curriculum is spiral learning, and revisiting and building on learning is the focus. Into Music 2 is in production at present and hopefully will be out end of the year while Into Music 3 is being written by a team and we meet once a month with the main writer. It is a lengthy and comprehensive process. This latter guideline will be out next year to all intermediate and secondary schools followed by a Planning and Assessing in Music Guideline (including Music Technology) for all levels, which will begin development next year for 2004 publication and production. The Ministry funds all of these books and CDs and Learning Media produce them with a team of advisers – lots of quality control. Each of the first three handbooks has chapter headings that are the same but the content, approaches, learning and teaching all grow from each other and shift in emphasis according to child development. They are each useful for the music teacher or interested school and should be seen as a progressive package. Primary or secondary, the kids need the opportunities and the teachers need to see the whole picture. Any non-free copies can be purchased from Learning Media of course.

Merryn Dunmill:
My first response is that each idea can lead you to a range of resources but to source some, how about checking out the Teaching and Learning Materials button on Unwrapping the Arts – Music learning examples, which is very large and up-to-date. A selection of websites is helpful with some that are interactive, midi-downloadable for backing tracks, and can give heaps of background information. Texts are varied and numerous. I will list some main publishers for music here in New Zealand as web addresses, too:

  • SOUNZ – Centre for NZ Music – www.sounz.org.nz

  • Piper's NZ for links to visual arts, music, Māori arts and culture webs – http://www.piperpat.com/DesktopDefault.aspx?tabid=53

  • Te Puna Web Directory – hosted by the National Library who have a well resourced music centre – http://webdirectory.natlib.govt.nz/index.htm

  • Timeframe – New Zealand's largest online database of heritage and history images – www.natlib.govt.nz

This will get you started but remember to check the references in Into Music 1 as these are specific to the activities detailed.

Hans:
Thanks Merryn. I think of resources as more than the booklets and tapes, like instruments? Are there schemes of sharing or loaning musical instruments between schools? Once we get past the more 'primitive' simple percussion, and the basic recorders, how do we get access to a broader range? Didn't schools once get issued with a piano each?

Merryn Dunmill:
The days of department-funded instrument resources are well gone and schools are now funded to resource themselves according to need, as we know. I recommend a piano of course and there may be some kind local who will donate theirs! KBB Music (fax 09 6302231) has a great catalogue as do Piano City, in major centres: they have heaps of good instruments for you to order. There is a general music catalogue that advertises the wares of about 30+ stores nationwide. Look at www.musicworks.co.nz. Actually you should have a catalogue in your schools because they send copies to all. Some locals make great instruments too, which are good quality. Check the sound and structure of anything if you can and ensure you get something decent to start with, as you save in the end. Instruments are taonga and should be treated with respect as with any other school equipment. I'm not aware of hire schemes between schools as most schools use their gear all the time (hopefully). You may be able to contact local schools and rationalise some more major purchases such as band or electronic gear for special performance purposes.

Ces:
We were talking about using music that has been out of publication for a while. Do we need copyright if it is? Do we need to pay APRA for very old music?

Merryn Dunmill:
I think we should have a whole session on this one! Anyway, you are best to get a school licence to cover yourselves. If you are copying old scores that are no longer obtainable there is no real issue except for the legal issues around any photocopying and your school has documentation on this. If you are using copied music for class learning purposes then limited copying is allowed. You only pay if you are performing. In reality, it is impossible for anyone to individually collect royalties so it is an insurance policy really! For class use be safe and keep a few copies only, collect them in and store them. Do not hand out photocopies galore of music even for rehearsal purposes. You are meant to buy multiple copies for performance purposes or hire from SOUNZ if they have what you want. National Library loans out music too. You can email APRA at nz@apra.com.au or hit the website www.apra.com.au for info. Secondary schools usually pay up to seven licences to cover all sorts of copyright based on a levy per student. This covers them and keeps things legitimate.
