eDance curriculum support material

The curriculum support material below (published 1995 onwards) has been collated from Ministry of Education publications, TKI curriculum support material lists, the DANZ newsletter, and other materials identified by the college of education facilitators and The Arts Online team.

Print, video, CD, and CD-ROM materials

Ministry of Education

Ministry of Education. (2002). Dancing the long white cloud [video]. Wellington: Learning Media.
This set of two dance videos includes one for teachers of years 1 to 6 and another for years 7 to 10, and an accompanying teachers' book. The videos are intended to help teachers "unpack" dance in the curriculum.
For further information see website: http://www.tki.org.nz/r/arts/artspd/events/new_e.php

Ministry of Education. (2002). Wall charts for classroom dance: Years 1–10 [posters]. Wellington: CWA New Media in association with Footnote Dance.
These two sets of large, attractive, and inspirational charts support the teaching of dance as described in The Arts in New Zealand Curriculum. The charts are aimed at levels 1 to 3 and levels 4 to 5 of the curriculum document. Each set has four separate charts representing one of the four strands of the dance curriculum.
For further information see website: http://www.tki.org.nz/r/arts/artspd/events/new_e.php

Other (New Zealand)

Bolwell, J., Cossey, J., & Oliver, L. (1998). Creative dance in New Zealand primary schools. Masterton: Wairarapa Educational Resource Centre.
This handbook written by New Zealand dance educationalists provides primary teachers with a useful text for teaching creative dance as part of the curriculum. It includes a series of sample lessons based around the elements of dance, and support materials for management and organisation.

Cossey, J.(Ed). (2001). New Zealand music for creative dance [CD]. Available from SOUNZ: Centre for New Zealand Music, Wellington.
Selected by an experienced dance teacher, this variety of New Zealand music is particularly useful for teaching creative dance.

Creative New Zealand. (2000). Creative explorer: An interactive creative experience (millenium ed.) [CD-ROM]. Wellington: Learning Media Ltd, in association with CWA New Media.
Creative Explorer is an interactive CD-ROM about the arts in New Zealand, aimed at year 7 and 8 students. It features well-known New Zealand artists across six media, including Neil Ieremia (dance). Teachers' guide online support material for the CD-ROM, includes a collection of unit plans, an updated online gallery of students' work using the CD-ROM, and a discovery kit that introduces Creative Explorer. The unit plans use the CD-ROM to implementing The Arts in the New Zealand Curriculum and integrate the arts across other curriculum areas.
Website: http://www.tki.org.nz/r/arts/curriculum/creativex/files/discovery/index_e.php

Drama and dance in the classroom: As long as it takes a jelly to set. [video]. (1999). Auckland: Auckland College of Education.
This video and the accompanying teachers' notes illustrate the work of six primary and intermediate teachers using drama and dance in their classrooms. Available from the Centre for the Arts, Auckland College of Education.

Footnote Dance in education pack: Fast forward [booklet and CD]. (1998). Available from footnote@paradise.net.nz
This resource kit has been prepared to support and assist teachers working with dance in the classroom. The kit is designed to be used in collaboration with Footnote Dance Company and its classroom programmes of dance in education. Suitable for levels 1 to 5 of the New Zealand curriculum.

Reed, F. (2001). Magic moments: A guide to creative dance teaching in the classroom. Invercargill: Essential Resources.
This book provides six detailed lesson plans for teaching creative dance for 5- to 8-year-olds. It is aimed at the general primary classroom teacher with specific learning outcomes suggested for each lesson plan.

Reed, F. (2002). Creative Souls: A guide to teaching creative dance in the upper primary school classes. Invercargill: Essential Resources.
This book in two parts provides detailed lesson planning with both sections focusing on the teaching of dance, the first uses the elements of dance while the second uses themes. It is aimed at upper primary/intermediatre students and is directly referenced to The Arts in the New Zealand Curriculum.

Royal New Zealand Ballet – Dance education resource [booklet and CD-ROM]. (2001).
This curriculum support material written by the Royal New Zealand Ballet Company is for primary, intermediate, and lower secondary school teachers and their students. It introduces the history and development of the Royal New Zealand Ballet and highlights some of its major artists and directors. It is available from the Royal New Zealand Ballet, phone 0800 432 623 or email inquiry@nzballet.org.nz

Other

Allen, A. & Coley, J. (1995). Dance for all 2. London: David Fulton.
This book has information for teachers around a variety of dance themes that relate directly to classroom topics. The authors suggest a structured approach to the dance lesson and provide ideas for further development. Each module contains six lessons around a common theme chosen to appeal to young people.

Ashley, L. (2002). Essential guide to dance. London: Hodder and Stoughton. This generously illustrated textbook integrates dance theory and practice in user-friendly language. It has a wealth of ideas, activities, and tasks for dance students, including explanations of social and cultural contexts, and the development of modern dance.

Evans, J. (1996). Inspirations for dance and movement. Leamington Spa: Scholastic.
This books focuses on popular classroom topics for primary schools. It has a thematic approach and it provides stimuli for children to create their own movement. Included are detailed lesson plans as well as guidelines for planning and assessment.

Gibson, B. (1998). Dance matters [resource pack]. London: Heinemann.
Ideal for years 6 to 10. The resource pack provides nine manageable dance projects divided into units that can be used as lesson plans. The pack includes a CD.

Gough, M. (1999). Knowing dance: A guide for creative teaching. London: Dance Books.
This book sets out a general structure for understanding the various aims, demands, and constraints of teaching dance, and presents many practical and creative solutions. After examining the interface between teaching and learning, the author looks at how to plan, deliver, assess, and evaluate dance teaching. The author is a dance teacher of many years experience.

Minton, S.C. (1997). Choreography: A basic approach using improvisation (2nd ed.). Champaign, IL: Human Kinetics.
Aimed at secondary to tertiary levels, this book focuses on discovering movement through improvisation. It outlines practical activities that explore the elements of dance and various choreographic devices, as well as presenting useful information on the choreographic process, from the initial ideas stage to organising a concert.

Peter, M. (1997). Making dance special. Developing dance in the curriculum with pupils with special education needs. London: Fulton.
This book has practical teaching information for developing dance in the curriculum with students with special educational needs. The advice is helpful to general as well as special education teachers.

Rickett-Young, L. (1997). Dance sense. Theory and practice for GCSE students. Plymouth: Northcote House.
Written in a lively style, this book is illustrated throughout and combines practical ideas with dance appreciation and history. Each chapter contains group or individual tasks relating to the content. Although written for GCSE students, much of the material is useful for secondary students in New Zealand.

Schrader, C. (1996). A sense of dance: Exploring your movement potential. Champaign, IL: Human Kinetics.
This book explores the elements of dance applied to daily life; the physical expression of dance; the dance elements of space, time, and energy; and the creative process. The author's belief that everyone has the capability to dance underpins her examples.The aim is to facilitate students to find their own voice. In addition, the book provides the opportunity for students to be able to appreciate, discuss, and write about dance.

Stinson, S. (1998). Dance for young children: Finding the magic in movement. Virginia: American Alliance for Health, Physical Education, Recreation and Dance.
This is a helpful guide to planning and teaching dance in early childhood education.


Online materials

Ministry of Education

Unwrapping the arts – dance
Website: http://www.tki.org.nz/e/arts/dance/dance_home.php
This website contains a selection of dance learning examples for levels 1 to 5 from The Arts in the New Zealand Curriculum. Teachers will find comments on planning and assessment, elements and terms, links and resources. This is a draft of the material, and teachers are invited to evaluate the material and return their feedback comments.

The dance exemplars and dance matrix (draft)
Website: http://www.tki.org.nz/r/assessment/exemplars/arts/dance/
The dance matrix sets out key aspects of dance learning progressions at five curriculum. The dance exemplars will show many, but not necessarily all, of these progressions. Although the matrix is set out as a table, which may suggest a concept of linear progression, this meaning is not intended. In practice, learning in dance is spiral in nature, incorporating earlier stages and building on itself. The draft matrix is an indication of possible signposts in the dance-learning journey. Students will "travel" in many different ways. This matrix will continue to evolve over the course of the exemplar project and a final version, incorporating feedback, will be published with the national curriculum exemplars in 2003.
