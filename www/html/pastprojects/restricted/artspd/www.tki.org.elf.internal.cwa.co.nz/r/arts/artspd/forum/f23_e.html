Nō reira e ngā reo, e ngā mana, e ngā hau e wha, tēnā koutou, tēnā koutou, tēnā koutou katoa. Just a quick hello to you all. I'm Rawiri Hindle, The National Coordinator for Ngā Toi .

Val Duthie:
It would be really useful, at the start of this forum discussion, if you could summarise for us the differences between Music in the Arts in the New Zealand Curriculum statement and Music in the Ngā Toi statement.

Rawiri Hindle:
Merryn, the National Coordinator for Music, would have a more in-depth knowledge of the Music in The Arts Curriculum. However, here goes for the Pūoro discipline in the Marautanga Ngā Toi. The elements are comparable to the Arts document. The Māori document places emphasis on Te Reo in each of the disciplines, especially around specific language that the children will need to learn/know related to each of the disciplines. The whenu (strands) are as follows: tūhura (explore); mahi (make/do); mōhiotanga (knowing); and maioha (appreciation). You are able to make connections from this to the strands in the Arts document. Another important aspect about the Ngā Toi document is that it comes from a strong whakapapa and tikanga base and is not a direct translation from the English document. Ask Merryn her opinion because she has a good grip on what she sees as different.

Carol Langton:
In The Arts in the New Zealand Curriculum, a culturally inclusive programme provides students with a safe environment in which to develop and express their identity, by encouraging positive attitudes towards cultural diversity. Also, it recognises the diversity of individual students within particular cultures. It goes on to state, "cultural practices are always changing and contemporary cultures are in a continual state of development" (p.104). Question: With the continually changing nature of cultural identity, how can teachers ensure that what they are teaching is relevant for the needs of students today?

Rawiri Toia:
Hoki koe ki te tīmatanga kōrero. We must maintain the tikanga of why we do things. From a Māori perspective we should not look to change or learn new processes first but instead, look to the beginning of who we are as Māori. In particular with puoro, the way nature has influenced Māori music. The musical instruments have a whakapapa that links back to the environment, so should our cultural identity.

Val Duthie:
I have a couple of questions from teachers who are keen to participate today but have to attend meetings after school. I'll post their questions for you, as I'm sure that everyone will benefit from your responses.

Question 1: As a teacher of mainly Māori kids in a mainstream English medium class, should I be emphasising the connections between dance and drama, using examples such as kapa haka, or should I be trying to demonstrate the difference between dance and drama?

Rawiri Hindle:
That was a big question. Kapa haka should support topics taught in the form of writing songs and music. It's use in dance and drama or Ngā Mahi a Te Rēhia should look at the process of dance and drama to highlight the words of the topic, for example, waiata a ringa action song, haka or as a choreographed dramatisation.

Val Duthie:
Question 2: Am I right in thinking that in Ngā Toi , dance and drama are combined in one discipline? For the class that I'm teaching (92% Māori), should I be teaching the arts as three disciplines or four?

Rawiri Hindle:
We must remember that Ngā Mahi a Te Rēhia is not just dance and drama. It looks at the performing arts as a whole. You're able to teach three or four disciplines depending on the contexts that you're using and the Achievement Objectives you're looking at.

I have a limited ability to play guitar. I often get words to Māori songs but don't have the chords. I do use Hei Whakakoakoa a wonderful resource. Do you have any other suggestions?

Rawiri Hindle:
Pureingia tonu o three chords e hoa. Can't help as far as known resources go but as for playing the guitar, don't restrict yourself in thinking that you have to play guitar or (any other instrument for that matter) to get Māori waiata into your classroom. Explore acapella styles, low-tech instruments, and in particular kohatu (stones) to find the manawataki (beat) and tauira ungeri (rhythm patterns).

Judy:
I work with Auckland College of Education and visit lots of schools. I find as I work with Māori teachers that they treasure the value of playing by ear but they are keen to have the children learn to read music, too. How will the document support that balance?

Rawiri Toia:
The document looks at providing a base for students. As you work through the levels (koeke), the Whāinga allow the growth and avenues to learn to read music.
