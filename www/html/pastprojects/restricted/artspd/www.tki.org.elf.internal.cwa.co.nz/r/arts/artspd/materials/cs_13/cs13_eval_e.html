One teacher's journey – Hamilton East Primary School

Introduction | Programme structure | Planning and content | Issues | Evaluation/Reflection

Evaluation/Reflection

Student comments

  • "I learned where the godwits fly to and how they use their bodies to fly there."
  • "I enjoyed the court case most. It felt like real life and I got annoyed."
  • "When we did our drama and the little godwit couldn't fly, I felt sad and tried to show everyone by using my face and body."

Teacher comments

How did this unit extend the children's learning?
"The children found out for themselves where the godwit flies to and why it travels the world. They displayed this knowledge in their court case argument posters and when they presented their arguments. I also had them fill in an evaluation of the bird flight adventure, asking them their likes and dislikes about what we did and what they learnt. It showed how much they enjoyed the experience, how successful it was, and believable, and that they did learn about bird migration whilst increasing their knowledge and understanding of drama."

How was this unit different to the first drama unit taught?
"Different, because it was using drama to explore a topic and not just using it to show their level of learning, as in the digestive systems case. It also involved the class as a whole unit working together to learn about and solve a series of problems and issues."

What worked well and why?
"The whole adventure concept and travelling to a new destination. Getting the children to realise the problems and issues, taking ownership and wanting to solve and discover the information so they could move on to the next part of the adventure. The teacher in role as a variety of characters to keep the class on their toes - just when they thought they were off another obstacle was put in front of them to solve and investigate."

Has your knowledge been extended?
"Most definitely. I have learnt how to use drama as the main focus of learning and how to incorporate it into curriculum areas. I have also learnt how to use drama as a tool for learning and discovery. I have actually experienced the different elements, conventions and techniques of drama and how to plan/teach using the correct terminology."

How has this process changed any future practice for you?
"I am not afraid to use drama as the basis for a unit, no matter what curriculum area. I can integrate drama into planning to ensure it is getting a focus each term.

How has the nature of the contract informed future practice for you and your school?
"For me personally I now feel confident in planning drama units and integrating drama into my class programme. I can see a place for drama in the weekly routine. I have become, on the whole, a more reflective practitioner. School-wide, I have led two workshops on drama showing how to use drama and teaching the conventions and techniques. In my team I try to ensure they are planning for drama, and put drama aspects into our shared planning. I would really like the rest of the school to have the same opportunity I have."

Facilitator comments

Linda is an example of a teacher who has been inspired by a series of positive practical experiences to take responsibility for her own learning in drama. Linda's progress/achievement is typical of the success of the professional development model which builds practical knowledge within a workshop setting and then focuses on the specific needs of each participant, through facilitator in school modelling and opportunities for discussion. The high level of success in Linda's case was due to her willingness to take responsibility for her own professional development and obvious commitment to developing drama within her own classroom. She was not resistant to change and worked collaboratively with the contract facilitator to ensure that all Hamilton East children were provided with the opportunity to experience the benefits of drama in education.
