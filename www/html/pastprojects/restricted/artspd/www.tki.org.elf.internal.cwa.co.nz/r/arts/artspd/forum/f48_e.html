Introductions

Sarah Wilson:
This week we are talking about Nga Mahi a te Rehia – Maori Performing Arts with Rawiri Hindle and Jojo Rangihaeata. Both guests have a wealth of experience in this field so look forward to some excellent discussion around this subject.

  • Rawiri Hindle is the Ministry of Education’s National Coordinator for Nga Toi and a lecturer at the Wellington College of Education. He is also the Maori Content Coordinator for Te Kete Ipurangi where he assists with developing materials for Maori language teachers in the general stream and for those working in the Maori immersion Kura Kaupapa schools.

  • Jojo Rangihaeata is a Maori Web Developer for Te Kete Ipurangi and a highly experienced kapa haka performer. Here she introduces herself:

Ko Pukehapopo te maunga
Ko Waiomoko te awa
Ko Ngati Konohi te iwi.
Ko Whangara-Mai-Tawhiti te marae
Ko Paikea te tipuna
Ko Jojo Rangihaeata toku ingoa

I am a part of a unique kapa haka roopu Whangara-Mai-Tawhiti, which hails from the mighty East Coast of the North Island. I am a descendant of Paikea from a long line of chiefs. I have been performing in kapa haka since I was a little girl in the Patea Maori Club, and since moving back to Gisborne and doing kapa haka all my life. I have competed at in regional kapa haka level for about 10 years, and have competed in the nationals for nine years. My life's passion is evolved around kapa haka and anything Maori. I live and breath anything Maori and especially my own native tongue and of course Nga mahi a te Rehia (performing arts).

No reira te whanau kei te mihi ki a koutou katoa kua tae mai ki konei ki te korerorero nga ahuatanga e pa ana ki Nga Toi.

Topic: Breadth of Nga Mahi a Te Rehia

Rawiri Hindle:
Nga Mahi a Te Rehia is a huge area – not just kapa haka. It covers areas such as whaikorero (speech making), karanga (the call in the context of powhiri), welcome ceremonies, mau rakau, stilt walking, puppetry, dance, stick games, string games, drama, performance...

Kapa haka components include waiata a ringa (action songs), poi, haka, waiata tawhito (chanting, entrance and exits on to stage).


Topic: Impact of waiata and kapa haka

Tom Evans:
Tena kotou. Good to be online again!

Friends at the Australian conference were very moved by a waiata from Taranaki – even on the lips of a Welshman like me, te reo calls to many cultures. We need more of it in our schools and reflected in our lives and our practice.

Rawiri Hindle:
Ae tika!! I agree totally – there's something very special about performing waiata. I'm constantly moved by the expression of kapa haka, and I think it's to do with the fact that everything is involved: voice, sound, movement, gesture. And then there’s the inward emotions being projected to the listening audience.

I also love the fact that more and more people/students are learning and performing waiata – for the fact that that the expression is rooted in the environment and connects to where we live in this great big world!

Topic: Getting started, keeping going

Tom Evans:
Hi Norma! Over the years teaching in an Intermediate school I have seen many attempts at teaching kapa kaka. Some have been very successful, and the children and school community have benefited greatly.

But the experts are in short supply and there is an immense call upon their time and energy. Nurturing and valuing these precious people and supporting them in what can be a foreign environment seems essential.

At the moment one of our teachers belonging to South Taranaki is doing a crash course to help the school and the children she obviously loves and cares for. I hope she does not burn out. It is great to see her practice poi any time she has a moment – her enthusiasm is infectious.

Rawiri Hindle:
Ki ora Tom!
Great to hear that your teachers is so onto it, and, yes, it is important to watch the burn out thing. Its very inspirational that she’s so into it and often the inspiration aspect is infectious – be great if it rubs off onto some of the other staff!
