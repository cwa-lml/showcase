  • The Arts in the New Zealand Curriculum, page 20

  • the exemplars from the National Exemplar trials on
    http://www.tki.org.nz/r/assessment/exemplars/arts/dance/edv_2a_e.html

In this exemplar the students actively explore the elements of: body awareness (with a focus on body shapes) and space (with a focus on floor pathways).

Sue Cheesman:
Body shapes is a concept that teachers find easy to relate to and there are so many examples of contrasting shapes that children and teachers can choose from to explore. Do you think that using a concrete image such as a long nail or a beach ball helps initially?

Tina Hong:
My answer would be "Yes". I would encourage the use of concrete images – particularly for earlier experiences when students (no matter what age!) are initially exploring body shapes (Element: Body Awareness). I tend to use: Pin (long/thin); Ball (round); Wall (wide); and Twisted (combination of previous) as images for student exploration. These cover the basic shapes and they can be done in any number of ways – different levels, size etcetera.

After some initial exploration it is also interesting to then have students explore and develop movement transitions from one shape to another, for example, "Can you make a ball shape and then make a jerky transition into a wall shape?"

Sue Cheesman:
Yes, that is great. Also, a fun activity is to vary the time they take between shapes. With younger ones I find that shells are interesting to use as stimulus for shape work. For older children how would you suggest we approach this work?

Ann N:
I find my kids are very keen on dancing around the classroom. I am keen to structure this but do not want to use the latest pop music. Do you have any suggestions of how I might do this in relation to the ideas in today's forum?

Tina Hong:
Kids love to dance! I'm not sure how old your kids are but I'd suggest you use their enthusiasm and as you say 'structure it'. On the first of the two videos coming out soon (that I mentioned in my reply to Phil) there's an example of how a teacher has used the hip-hop idea and contemporary music. He begins by having the kids look at some music video clips that have dance sequences in them and identifying the different types of jumps that are used. Five basics are: same foot to same foot; two feet to two feet; one foot to different foot; two feet to one foot; and one foot to two feet. Students then explored these, developed short sequences in pairs or trios and finally ended up creating larger group and finally a whole class dance in a la hip hop style to funky music.

What this does is work backwards by having the children examine not only the context (and there's a lot that could be done, such as researching hip-hop dance via the Internet) but also look at breaking down the movement patterns and techniques as in the attention to the jumps. Might this be an appropriate beginning for you?

Christine:
What does it mean to change the dynamic in dance? I know the term in music, not dance.

Tina Hong:
Dynamics in dance relates to the quality of the dance. In our New Zealand curriculum it is broken down into Time and Energy as the element. So, in short, Time plus Energy equals Dynamics.

Sue Cheesman:
Great to get a range of questions. My thoughts on chance methods are that they are a way of ordering the movements. For example, you may have six different shapes and label them one to six and throw a dice six times to see the order you would dance them in. This is one example I know of that works.

Tina Hong:
Aha – you've been reading the curriculum! Chance method (or aleatory method) is rather like a "lucky dip" approach to dance making. Literally, the choreography is structured purely by chance.

There are lots of ways but one way that is often used involves the casting of dice to determine the movement material or structure of the dance. For example, you might have already developed some simple movement material but how you then proceed to manipulate the material to make it into a longer sequence, or dance, happens according to the roll of the dice. You allocate a particular way of doing movement to each number ahead of time, for example: 1 = repetition; 2 = doing the movement backwards: 3 = changing the dynamic; 4 = changing directions etcetera. The process then begins by rolling the dice and incorporating the instruction into the dance work.

The American choreographer Merce Cunningham has worked a lot with chance or aleatory methods. It's a very interesting way of working, with often surprising and dynamic results. When I've worked in this way with students they have all really enjoyed it.
