Developing an Implementation Plan

Excerpts from forum discussion with Catherine Gibbs (Co-director The Arts Online) 7 May 2003.

Sarah Wilson:
The topic for this forum is Developing an Implementation Plan for the Arts, and helping Arts curriculum managers to lead the Arts PD for their team. One of the most frequently asked questions from teachers when any new curriculum is introduced is "How do we use this document to help us plan a school or class programme?"

During this session we will be discussing various approaches to this question with the intention of providing practical solutions. This will include stocktaking, creating the vision, and prioritising. The session will also highlight pathways for providing guidelines to Arts curriculum managers working with a school team to build understanding.

Catherine Gibbs is our online guest for the session. Catherine has a wealth of experience in providing advice and guidance to teachers and principals about implementing the Arts curriculum. She is co-director of The Arts Online programme 2003 and in previous roles has worked as an adviser, lecturer, writer, deputy head, teacher and Ministry of Education Curriculum Facilitator for the arts.

Cathy Gibbs:
I am looking forward to sharing our ideas today as we continue to practise using this new way of learning together. I am interested in what you are doing in school to make a start with getting your Arts programmes up and running, and happy to share my ideas with you. I'd like to focus on positive ways to help move things along.

Sharron Smith:
I am Arts manager and Visual Arts specialist teacher at Nelson Intermediate School. I am interested in how other schools, particularly other intermediate schools, timetable the four disciplines.

Within my school at the moment, Visual Art and Music receive 18 to 20 hours per student per year and 4 to 6 hours of Dance is scheduled for Terms 3 and 4 within teams to spread the professional development learning curve. At this stage, Drama will be taken across curricula while teachers gain confidence and build up skills and resources. Next year Dance and Drama will be timetabled more "formally", probably while seeing the demise of Electives.

I am really intrigued how other schools manage to include all four disciplines within an already crowded timetable.

Carol Langton:
We are looking at implementation today. It can be so very different [at a full small primary school] to a large high school or a small country school. But the question remains – as Sharron Smith asked – how do we fit it all in? Do you have any ideas?

Cathy Gibbs:
Yes, timetabling – especially at intermediates – is quite tricky, and every school seems to have a different answer to your question. A lot depends on size and spaces available, especially for the equipment-intensive disciplines such as Visual Art and Music. Most intermediates I know timetable a core component for each of the disciplines and then invite children to participate in options. This is a good way to introduce children to a more specialised pathway in preparation for Year 9. For a start you could read strategies tried by different schools to address the issue of meeting the requirements of all four disciplines. Check out the Arts Online button under Implementation titled "Issues".

Cathy Gibbs:
[To Caroline] Yes it's a balancing act alright. I agree that the way to go is a major minor focus in your situation. How about integrate with the minor selected discipline and focus intensively on teaching the new skills with the major one each term. Secondly, planning for the concert need not be considered as extra but an integral part of your planning cycle. Know your destination, but the process of teaching something new along the way is the important bit. Can you plan and reflect on what improvements the children have made to their knowledge and understanding in the particular thing you chose to do as learning outcomes? The concert items will "fall out" of your thriving weekly programme!

Cathy Gibbs:
[To Norma] Gosh there are so many ways to make a start. How about starting with a question to get everyone thinking about the impact of the Arts in their own lives, for example, "Tell us about your most recent Arts-related experience and how did you respond?" Or from where you are sitting right now can you see something that would stimulate a discussion about why we might teach the Arts at school? [You could] invite each teacher to describe their own journey in learning in one of the Arts disciplines ̵ going right back to childhood and the influences that have played a part in bringing them to where they are today. This could also be communicated using some of the disciplines, for example drawing a line and hanging pics at significant moments, acting a role, or recalling special songs or pieces of music that they will always remember because of the context they were in at the time. The idea is that you want them to engage in a meaningful exercise focussing on the deep and meaningful way the Arts pervade everyone's lives. You could also go the formal pathway and begin with the official journey by "opening up the book" ̵ all these steps are here for you under the Arts Online RH button called "Teacher PD materials".Have a look at all the help provided here ... all done for you to adapt!

Barbara Tait:

All children get to play at least three tuned percussion instruments (recorder, guitar and xylophone/ukulele). The dance and drama classes are taken in five-week blocks and the guitar etc. ten-week or term. This action culminates in our end of year production. We have very good community and parent support to help us with this each week – it's a big commitment to those "Helpers" who come in.
