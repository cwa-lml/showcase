With regard to singing how important is posture. I often hear people say sit up or stand up when they are teaching singing.

Merryn Dunmill:
Ok, let's sing. Check out page 30 for resources because there are so many. Check Learning Media in Wellington and www.sounz.org.nz for more resources online.

Now, environment, Val. You can, and should, sing any time of day. Acoustics are important. Try singing in different spaces, for example outside, in the hall, against a concrete wall, and talk about the differences with the children. Some bounce-back is good but too much confuses the ear. Alison talks about hello and echo songs: great! Sing end-of-day songs, travel safe, celebration songs etcetera. Build events in society into your singing programme so that singing is seen as part of everyday life. Wouldn't it be great to have our future adults travel the world able to sing a song from New Zealand, knowing the words and feeling a strong sense of pride and identity!

Sing between activities to change routine. Incorporate actions for kinaesthetic learners, and reluctant singers get hooked in as they concentrate on their actions rather than voice, if they are shy about it. Then, they sing naturally with others.

Always warm up the voice: see the exemplar of Exploring Pitch for ideas. Like any body part the voice needs warming up. Think of your own voice in the mornings when you wake up. It takes time to get it sounding "normal" especially after a bit of a night out!!

Sue:
Posture does impact on the quality of sound and on the ability to control breathing. If you sit scrunched up uncomfortably, the diaphragm that pumps the air can't operate properly and the children end up puffing out short bursts. This is okay for warm ups but when you want to get it sounding better – for example for a "performance", even it is just "let's stand up, move closer together so you can hear the person next to you" (this helps maintain in-tuneness) – you will notice an improvement. Play with dynamics (loud and soft, and variations). Play games of getting louder on one note and getting softer on one note (two things happening here are: maintaining pitch, Kirsty; and exploring sound quality (tone colours) of the voice). Work on levels that sound right for the song.

So, posture should be relaxed shoulders, feet a little apart, and hands at sides if you are performing without movement. With movement, all of that falls into place, for example, kapa haka. Open mouths and make sure the words come through clearly and all together. "S" is a tough sound and usually ends up hissing through: save the "s" until the last minute, that is, hold vowels in preference to consonants.

Mainly though, for young ones, choose songs in the right pitch or key so they can sing in tune. Choose stronger singers to lead and have others join in, as they feel confident. Always encourage listening to each other and use good tapes if you are unsure of yourself. Stick one finger in one ear and listen to yourself, too, and adjust if you are "out". That's why one-note exercises to small pitch range chants are good – not too much wobbling around in the early stages. Just like any sport really, one stroke at a time, build on the skills, evaluate and extend.

Carol:
One of the things we strive for, as a rural school in Northland, is to encourage our Kapa Haka group to sing in two-part harmony.

Phil:
I'm also interested in Carol's question about two-part harmony. Do you have any comments on that?

Merryn Dunmill:
You need to build up to singing in parts, although many students do this naturally as a part of their community and family life, which shows how important the environment is to learning to sing.

With students who have had little or no experience of singing outside of the bathroom or along with tapes in their bedrooms, lots of unison stuff first is the best plan: then rounds so they learn to hold a part against another, then to part-singing – holding a different part at a different pitch. This is great for seniors, especially, who like the challenge and the sound produced.

Again, all those Kiwi Kidsongs songbooks offer a range of songs for this. Beatles songs and 60's music in general have good part-singing in them: simple harmonies, predictable and fun to sing and play along with.

Some students will struggle with part-singing and it takes independent group practices to make it strong and confident. They simply have to learn to listen to the strongest person on their part and hone in on their pitch. Drawing pitch in the air can help. Have two parts stand in groups in different spaces in the room. Get them to make eye contact with each other and follow a confident and accurate leader's pitch drawing (by hand movements) in the air. Sometimes this tactile approach reaffirms the aural. Try one group soft and one loud and then swap. Try getting them to walk around the room randomly singing their part. This is hard and a real test of accuracy at the end.

Ann N:
I am very interested in the questions and answers so far, providing me with many varied ways to explore. My question is, when discussing singing how does rap fit? My children love to rap and are really interested in this. How do I help them?

Merryn Dunmill:
Rap is a distinct style of music with particular techniques and meanings. Often it is taken as anything chanted and often dealt with badly: for example, no syncopation or repeated words or no sense of what rap should really sound like. Kids take badly to teachers trying to teach rap and ending up doing a very "classical" chant. So, if the kids are into it, then they are likely to be the best ones to lead it.

You can help by playing good quality and okay (check lyrics) rap in class and talking about it: how it has been put together: rhythmic pattern: use of voice etcetera. It's a great art form and hip-hop, in particular, has grown out of the Rap movement and is a big music industry in New Zealand. It's quite unique, particularly the Pacific feel, for example, Nesian Mystic, Sheelahroc and so on.

Record the kids' work and they can DJ or scratch over the recording by using a turntable (called turn-tabling and a legitimate art form in its own right). Hip Hop has catchy hooks and choruses to sing along to and is a dance style, so moving to it is important, too. There are plenty of videos around and TV is a good source of inspiration too.

Chris:
Where is the best place to start developing a school wide implementation plan for music?

Merryn Dunmill:
Your question is enormous, and a long-term thing. School wide implementation has to come from shared goal setting and understanding of the curriculum. The four strands, the levels and the range of contexts are all critical across the school. Regular, ongoing music learning needs to be planned for throughout the (dare I say) week, if not each day.

In the early years it is so naturally part of all learning: language development through song; rote learning through song; mathematical understanding through rhythm work; discussion of contexts is social studies; creative work is technology and health.

All the essential skills are embodied in every music activity: social cooperative (working in cooperative learning groups totally uniquely in music, as all have to work together and listen and respond simultaneously in a distinct language beyond words for success to occur); problem-solving (for example, creating an original piece of music, working out how to play an instrument); numeracy (counting beats and rhythms, reading notation; literacy (reading text, reading notations); communication (through performance of songs and instrumental music and through discussing and analysing what is heard); study skills (through independent rehearsal and discipline etcetera). It is interesting that employers prefer to take on employees who have studied music, as they are advanced in all these skills compared with others.

Whatever, the plan needs to incorporate listening, singing, playing, creating, reading and representing, and analysing opportunities for a good holistic programme. You will note that music-strong schools have focused, high achieving students who want to be at school and to learn: sometimes that's all they come to school for.

That sounds passionate but there is loads of international and national research to back all of the above and I am interested in the number of Decile 1–3 primary, intermediate and secondary schools setting themselves up with music academies. Good vibrations indeed!

Christine:
I am interested in the resource called Beat Circle created by Max Stewart. Have you heard of it? I was wondering how valuable you considered this resource.

Merryn Dunmill:
The Beat Circle is an adaptation from the idea first used in Upbeat (an Australian music kit for each level of their curriculum and very good to adapt from). I have seen Max's version and think it is colourful and fun.

You can do this yourself by simply drawing different symbols on the board in a circle – 3 for three beats and 4 for four beats etcetera. Point to each beat in the circle and have an action (and one silence) for each symbol. Then put music on in the time/metre you have symbols for and point away in any order to keep everyone on the job. This is lots of fun and a student can come up and be the conductor pointing around the circle, back and forth, repeat one action over and over – lots of fun and guaranteed to have the whole class on task and alert!
