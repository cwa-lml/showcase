Learning in Music

Use this overview of literacy in music to help inform your teacher planning for music.

Students need to be taught the skills and knowledge bases of listening, creating, performing, and critically responding to sound. Students need to know in, through, and about music. Literacy in music involves the development of knowledge and skills relating to styles, genres, technologies, and musical structures. Through experiencing a wide range of music, students can use their developing practical knowledge to compose and perform and to enhance their understandings of the world in which they live. As sensitive listeners, they learn to make sense of sound, to read and write music, to interpret performances, and to make personal responses and express their ideas.

Through learning in the four interrelated strands of the music curriculum students become literate in the sound arts known as music.

Developing Practical Knowledge in Music

This is developed through focused listening, practical application such as playback, experimentation, use of music technologies, transcriptions of sounds heard.

  • layering sounds, thickness, thinness

  • reading and recording

  • creating

  • analysing

  • evaluating

Developing Ideas in Music

This is developed through experimentation, manipulatio, and representation of sound to improvise and compose music.

All of the above skills and knowledge bases are integrated, refined, and evaluated in the learning centred around this strand. Composing and improvising are based on the ability to listen with discrimination, to select and combine sounds, and to organise these to create a work of art. Representing music in the form of a score, and/or recording (notating and/or making a sound recording) original musical works of art assists in preserving the artwork for future performance and for evaluation purposes.

Communicating and Interpreting in Music

This is developed through rehearsal, preparation, performance, and reflection on the performance processes and practices – technique, accuracy, musicianship, performance presentation. Interpreting in Music also relates to the reading of music notation systems/responding to stylistic requirements of reproducing music.

Here the skills of playing/singing, reading, recording and evaluating music are the focus of the learning. The essential skills (social and co-operative, self-management and competitive, physical, problem-solving, communication, information, numeracy and work and study) all feature, and they often feature simultaneously. For example, group performance provides an incredibly unique context for co-operative learning. The success of the group's work relies on the simultaneous physical, intellectual and empathic rapport between each and every member within a time-based event i.e., a musical performance. Improvement relies on whole group active and reflective processes.

Understanding Music in Context

This is developed through experiencing, analysing and reflecting on music of past and present contexts, from a wide range of cultures and settings.

In this strand the skills of listening, analysing, reading and evaluating music are the focus of the learning. The essential skills of communication, information, numeracy, and work and study feature. All music learning relies on active and reflective practices that are experienced within a context. In this way, the Understanding in Context strand pervades all music learning. It provides the opportunity to study the purposes and meanings of music, and the value of it in society. Study in this strand is critical to developing a personal compass. It helps provide a way of knowing who one is, where one comes from and how one fits into our diverse society. It also provides a unique basis for sharing ideas, beliefs and feelings through the universal language of music.
