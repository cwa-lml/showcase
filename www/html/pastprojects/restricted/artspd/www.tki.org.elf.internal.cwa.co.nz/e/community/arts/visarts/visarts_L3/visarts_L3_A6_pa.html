Level 3: Poupou

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Throughout the unit or lesson, teachers should be conferencing both individuals and groups of students whilst encouraging them to respond to and reflect on their own work and to the work of others by:

  • reinforcing their knowledge of basic elements and principles;
  • focusing on specific principles using images and artists work for reference.

The following elements and principles will be the focus for discussion in this learning example.

Elements

Space – consider how the figure occupies the space (e.g., background, foreground). How deep is that space?

Shape – consider the shape of poupou, the body and its component parts. Look for repeated shapes or recognisable motifs used as surface decoration.

Form – consider the three-dimensional nature of the artwork.

Line – consider the ways line has been used.

Principles

Balance – consider the arrangement, placement, and shape of the figure and its components on the background. Is there symmetry?

Proportion/scale – consider the relative size and scale of the figure and its component parts.

Contrast – are some areas differently shaped or sized than others, or more detailed?

Possible starting points

Experience and learn about poupou. Approach local kaumatua to arrange a class visit to your local marae. Access local resources at museums and art galleries. Use books and videos. Visit Māori carvers.

Note: In this unit the focus is on pou or epa, the smaller wall posts of a wharenui (meeting house), and not the tall free-standing poupou that form the central support for the roof of the wharenui.

Class discussion

Look at pictures of pou and poupou. Select examples of poupou which clearly reveal the identity of the individual represented. Discuss the known stories to which the details on the poupou refer to. (see Teaching and Learning Material) Focus on particular pou and ask the following types of questions:

  • What materials and processes were used?
  • Who do you think this pou represents?
  • What are the main elements and features of the work?
  • What designs and symbols can you see on the pou?
  • Where would you find this carving?
  • What do you notice about the size of the head in relation to the body (scale)?
  • What do you notice about the way that parts of the figure (e.g., the shape of hands and feet) have been represented? What does this mean?
  • Describe the surface decorations, and any patterns that you see.
  • How does this pou make you feel?
  • What is the purpose of the pou?
  • What is its value to the Māori people who use the marae to which it belongs?

Possible learning approaches

Teacher task

  • Create charts with examples of the ways in which different tribes depict the human form in poupou. Focus on the body and its various parts, and on the decorative treatment of the surface (refer to Teaching and Learning Material).
  • Teach the children about the forms and significance of Māori design symbols.

Student task

  • Create a pou of yourself or of a special person, possibly an ancestor or hero.
  • Make practice drawings of this figure.
  • Consider how you will represent (through the symbolism of the pou) the special character of your figure (e.g., what makes the person special – sport, emotions, skills, personality, hobbies, interests, life events, mishaps, or adventures).
  • Prepare a firm rectangular base for your pou.
  • Cut out the body parts (e.g., head, torso, hands, legs, and feet, using the background shape of the pou as a guide) from paper. Ensure pieces are symmetrical, where that is intended. Consider proportion and scale, remembering that the figure must fill almost all the background space.
  • Arrange the body parts on the background to show what the figure is doing.
  • Create and attach a symbol that represents the special personalising characteristic of your figure (e.g., netball, heart, paintbrush, cake etc).
  • Use cutting, folding, and scoring techniques with paper or card to create a variety of textural details such as spiral designs for joints and facial features (eyes, eyebrows, nose, mouth, moko).
  • Use paint or dyes to work further on the figure and the remaining background space, using contrasting or harmonious colours.
  • Add drawn (e.g., pastel) detail on the pou to further define decorative elements or details.

Sharing their work

Encourage the children to describe and interpret their own and their classmates' pou, using visual art language. As a group, talk about the ways different pou convey individual identity. (CI)

Other ways of making poupou artwork could include:

  • Clay sculpture
  • Box sculpture
  • Totem style poupou (using large whiteware boxes mounted on cardboard tubes)
  • Large-scale paintings in small groups
  • Printmaking (monoprints, cardboard prints, collagraphs).

Materials

  • Selection of papers and card (provide a variety of colours and textures)
  • Drawing materials – pencils, chalk, crayons, felts, pastels
  • Painting materials – acrylic, brushes, sponges, card, paper
  • Glue, scissors, Kraft knives
  • Clay
  • Printmaking materials
  • Cardboard tubes and boxes
  • Twine, string, wool.

Assessment

Possible specific learning outcomes include:
Students will be able to:

  • Discuss purposes and contexts within which poupou were made and valued (UC).
  • Identify, in discussion, the features of particular poupou and the ideas they convey (CI).
  • Communicate identity, role and/or status through the use of appropriate imagery and materials in their own artworks (DI, PK, CI).
