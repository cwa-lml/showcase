Level 4: Other Echoes: Imitating Birdsong

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Key teaching and learning concepts

The elements of music:

Beat – the pulse in music.

Rhythm – duration of notes forming patterns.

Pitch – the degree of highness or lowness of a note.

Tempo – the speed of the beat in music.

Dynamics – the varying levels of loudness and softness in music; or the signs used to indicate such levels.

Tone colour – the specific tone or sound quality of a musical instrument, combination of instruments, or sound source (e.g., singers from different cultural backgrounds have unique ways of using their vocal apparatus and therefore produce distinctive sounds); also called timbre. Sound sources include found, environmental, vocal, instrumental, and electronic sounds.

Texture – a piece of music's "density" of sound, which may range from thin (e.g., a single strand or instrumental line) to thick (e.g., several strands or instrumental lines).

Ostinato – a repeated accompaniment pattern that can be rhythmic or melodic and that is maintained throughout a section or piece.

Structural devices – these are devices used in constructing a piece of music and include a distinguishing idea such as riff, phrase, hook, sequence, repetition, variation and cadence. The combination of elements also contributes to overall music structure (e.g., harmony, texture).

Prior learning

Students need to have:

  • listened to a wide range of music that uses nature as a source of motivation;

  • completed a range of focused listening activities in which they have identified key musical features including the use of texture and ostinato;

  • an understanding of elements and structural devices of music and how to use them to create a soundscape;

  • experience and skills in creating sound effects using a range of sources: body and voice percussion, tuned and untuned percussion, band and orchestral instruments and simple technology such as music recording and playing equipment, synthesisers and computers.

Possible specific learning outcomes

The students will:

  • identify musical elements and structural devices used in contemporary New Zealand works that have used bird sounds as a source of motivation (PK, UC);

  • explore and discuss the way the composer of "Other Echoes" has used native birdcalls as a motivation for the work and incorporated the use of texture and ostinati to create varying effects (PK, CI, UC);

  • experiment with a range of musical ideas and soundmakers to create a soundscape that uses bird sounds as a source of motivation (PK, DI);

  • perform and evaluate their soundscapes by sharing with others (PK, CI).

Ideas for activities

  • Listen to New Zealand works that have been inspired by nature (there are many examples in the Our Music resource). Offer a variety of styles and encourage students to discuss the similarities and differences, for example, "Aotearoa Overture" (orchestral overture) and "Soundscape with Lake and River" (electronic) both by Douglas Lilburn from Our Music.


  • Listen to a range of New Zealand music that has been inspired specifically by bird song, see for example:


    • Ears Wide Open Taringa Areare, New Zealand Music for Levels 1– 4, SOUNZ, www.sounz.org.nz


    • "Vocalise" from Awa Herea by Gillian Whitehead.


    • Hirini Melbourne's music, for example, Te Wao Nui a Tane [CD] and Toiapiapi [CD] available from SOUNZ, the Centre for New Zealand Music, www.sounz.org.nz.


    • Our Music by Elizabeth Kerr [Book/audio cassettes], Learning Media


    • Birdsong: Kakako and Birdsong: Kakapo


    • "Kea Country" by Ian MacDonald, on Our Music


    • "Duets and Choruses" by Jack Body, on Our Music


    • "For the Kakako, No1" by John Rimmer, on Our Music


    • Universal Children's Audio , www.ucamusic.com


    • Rest Time-Whakaka [CD], www.ucamusic.com


    • The Nature Tape [CD], www.ucamusic.com


    • Te Ku Te Whe by Hirini Melbourne and Richard Nunns, [CD], www.rattle.co.nz


    • "Poiawhiowhio", on Te Ku Te Whe [CD], www.rattle.co.nz


    • "Wai", on Te Ku Te Whe [CD], www.rattle.co.nz


  • Listen to recordings of native birds (possible resources: Our Music, Tape A, Tracks 8, 9, or Birds of New Zealand on tape or CD, available from Replay Radio, Radio New Zealand). Use voices, instruments or found sounds to imitate the sounds. Record the imitations.


  • Listen to an excerpt from "Other Echoes" by Eve de Castro Robinson from the resource, Ears Wide Open (Track 12) researched by Cheryl Camm and published by SOUNZ, the Centre for New Zealand Music. Identify the instruments that are playing the slow, repeated notes (tubular bells, cello, double bass, later joined by harp, viola) and the instruments that are playing the fast motifs (piccolo, oboe, cor anglais, violin, bassoon, trumpet) in the first section of the work.


  • Read the extract on page 12 of the Teacher Guide of Ears Wide Open that explains the purpose of the work. Discuss with your students what aspects of the twentieth century they might reflect in a composition if they were given the opportunity.


  • Listen to the piece again and discuss the changes in texture. (Thin layers of sounds at the beginning with just a few instruments, gradually increasing in density until all instruments are playing and then becoming thin in texture again at the end.) Look at two excerpts from the score found in the music score book of Ears Wide Open, for example, bars 1–10 and bars 34–38 to observe what these changes in texture look like visually.


  • Listen carefully to the tubular bells. Identify the gradual change in pitch as the chords get higher. Investigate what this looks like on the score. What effect does this create in the mood of the
    work?


  • Identify the castanet ostinato. Indicate with an action whenever you hear the castanet pattern. Advanced students may like to try notating the castanet part.


  • In groups, children create a soundscape that uses birdsong as its source of motivation. Each group can develop their own birdsong with its own distinct "song" or pitch patterns. Incorporate the idea of space (spatial use of instruments) and silence – the kind that can be experienced when listening to birds, trees, the wind etc in the bush. Students could use and manipulate their recordings if the equipment is available or create the sounds live using voice and found sounds. The soundscapes should include an ostinato and layering of rhythms and sounds to vary the textures. Reflect on the effects all these devices have in the compositions and rework as necessary.


  • Record the soundscapes or present live to the class. Discuss and evaluate the compositions of others and then provide an opportunity for the students to refine their work.


  • Experiment with ways to represent the sounds symbolically using pictorial, graphic and/or conventional notation. Create a key and share the score with others to see how accurately it can be interpreted.

Assessment approaches

Formative

There are many questions and investigations suggested in the Ideas for activities which will ensure the students' progress can be monitored in an ongoing way:

In creating the soundscape, ask the following questions:

  • How are you going to create the sound of birdsong? What pitch patterns and rhythmic patterns suit your scene? How often will these recur?

  • What other sounds of nature need to be included – where and when?

  • What instrument will you use for your ostinato?

  • Will your ostinato be rhythmic or melodic?

  • What instruments will you use as an accompaniment?

  • How will all your parts work together?

  • How will you combine the sounds to vary the texture?

  • Will you use recorded sounds, voice sounds or instruments?

Possible strategies for assessment

Peer reflection on the composition – how effective was it? How well have the musical ideas worked to reflect the soundscape of birds in the New Zealand bush?

Self-assessment: teacher and peer reflection using agreed criteria based on the use and effect of compositional devices studied.
