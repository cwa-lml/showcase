Level 2: Creating Rhythmic and Melodic Ostinati

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Key teaching and learning concepts

Untuned percussion - percussion instruments on which only sounds of indefinite pitch can be played e.g. the snare drum, wood block.

Tuned percussion - percussion instruments on which sounds of definite pitch can be played e.g. the timpani, xylophone

Musical phrases - segment of a melody ( similar to a phrase in language)

Ostinato - a repeated accompaniment pattern that can be rhythmic or melodic and that is maintained throughout a section of a musical piece or the whole musical piece.

Prior learning

Students will need to have:

  • Explored untuned instruments.

  • Some experiences playing tuned instruments e.g. glockenspiels, recorders, keyboards.

  • Be able to play in time.

Suggested learning outcomes

  • Play back rhythmic phrases on a range of untuned percussion instruments (PK)

  • Play back melodic phrases on tuned percussion instruments (PK)

  • Create a rhythmic ostinato (DI)

  • Create a melodic ostinato (DI)

Ideas for activities

1. Listen to and sing some simple musical phrases from a range of songs.

the phrase "Karanga Weka Weka" in the song Karanga Weka Weka Kiwi Kidsong

the phrase " yes I do" in the song 'Love Somebody' The Tinder Box.

Ask
Show me with your hands how the notes go up and down.

 

2.Play the phrase on a range of untuned percussion to create a rhythmic phrase.

Sing the song and play the rhythmic phrases as part of the song.

When the students feel confident, play the rhythmic phrase at the appropriate place without the support of singing.

Ask
What instrument sounds best playing your rhythmic phrase in this song?
Why?
Does your rhythmic phrase fit with other parts of the song?

 

3. Listen to and sing some simple musical phrases from a range of songs.

Play the phrase on a tuned instrument e.g. a glockenspiel, a recorder, a keyboard. The teacher could play the phrase first and then the children could echo it

e.g. in karanga Weka Weka, the children play
  1 2& 3& 4&
  asq ee ee ee
  B BB AA BB
  In Love Somebody, the children play
  1 2 3 4
  asq asq asgh  
  E F# G  

 

4. Sing the song and play the melodic phrase in the appropriate place. When the students feel confident, play the melodic phrase at the appropriate place without the support of singing.

Ask
What instrument sounds best playing your melodic phrase in this song?
Why?
Does your melodic phrase fit with other parts of the song?
Why/why not?

 

5. Sing the song again. Create a rhythmic ostinato or repeating pattern using body percussion or untuned percussion instruments that fit throughout the song. This ostinato might be derived from a word or phrase in the song.

Ask
What rhythmic ostinato sounds best with that particular song?
Why/why not?
What instrument sounds best playing your rhythmic ostinato in this song?
Why?

6. Turn one of the rhythmic ostinato into a melodic ostinato. Get the students to find 2 or 3 notes they can use. Make sure that these notes sound okay when the melodic ostinato is played with the musical phrase.

e.g. in karanga Weka Weka, the students could create a melodic ostinato using the following notes:

E D C or B
e.g. a repeating pattern of E and B

In Love Somebody, the students could create a melodic ostinato using the following notes:
A and E

Ask
Does your melodic ostinato sound right when you play it with the musical phrase?
Can you use different combinations of notes to make a more interesting melodic ostinato?
What¹s the best starting note?

7. When performing the song, combine the singing, the musical phrase, and the rhythmic and melodic ostinati in different ways to create layers. This could be as simple or as complex as you want.

e.g.

  • one group of students could sing the whole song,

  • one group could play the rhythmic ostinato on untuned percussion instruments throughout the whole song.

  • one group could play the musical phrase on tuned percussion instruments.

  • one group could play the melodic ostinato on contrasting tuned percussion instruments when the musical phrase is being sung or played.

Ask
What¹s the best way to combine singing, phrases and ostinati to make the song sound interesting?

 

Teaching Tips

  • Select musical phrases that are simple for the children to play back

    e.g
    in Love Somebody, the musical phrase moves up and down in steps
    in karanga Weka Weka , the musical phrase uses two notes only.

  • Provide ongoing opportunities for students to practice playing their musical phrases and rhythmic and melodic osinati.

 

Assessment Approaches

Focus learning Outcome 1

Key question – How well can the student imitate the rhythmic phrase using untuned percussion instruments?

Criteria for assessment could include:
ability to play back the rhythm of the musical phrase accurately

Focus Learning Outcome 2

Key question – How well can the student imitate the melody of a particular phrase?

Criteria for assessment could include:
ability to play back the melodic phrase accurately

Focus Learning Outcome 3

Create a rhythmic ostinato

Key question – How well can the student create a rhythmic ostinato that fits with the beat and rhythm of a particular song?

Criteria for assessment could include:

  • identification of what a rhythmic ostinato is
  • ability to create a rhythmic ostinato that fits with the whole song
  • ability to make decisions between rhythmic ostinato in terms of how they best enhance the song.

Focus Learning Outcome 4

Create a melodic ostinato.

Key question – How well can the student create a melodic ostinato that fits with the melody and rhythm of a particular song?

Criteria for assessment could include:

  • identificiation of what a melodic ostinato is
  • ability to create a melodic ostinato that fits with the musical phrase
  • ability to make decisions about what starting note sounds the best
  • ability to make decisions between melodic ostinato in terms of how they best enhance the song.
