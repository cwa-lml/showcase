Level 3: Presenting a Waiata or Haka

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Key teaching and learning concepts

This experience asks for a traditional waiata or haka. Ideally teachers would consult their local iwi for appropriate waiata and haka and recommendations of who should teach it, e.g., haka usually is taught by a male. Traditional waiata or haka will have traditional actions, or conventions of delivery, e.g., the tone is continued by some singers, while others take a breath.

Each school will ideally consult its local iwi for words, pronunciation, and actions that may be unique to the region. If that is not possible, reliable versions may be accessed on tape, CD, or video (see Teaching and Learning Materials).

  • Waiata means song. Haka is a postural dance. Many of the waiata and haka used in schools are contemporary compositions and accessible to all teachers.

  • Te Ihi is the mana of the material.

  • Te Wehi is the essential force of the performance.

  • Te Wana is the creative power made possible through the knowledge of the traditions.

  • Poi are the small balls on string used to maintain flexibility, dexterity, and strength of wrists.

  • Tira are the long wands or batons used in practise to promote strength and agility.

  • Titi torea, or ti rakau, are the "sticks" used in cooperative rhythm games and promoting coordination, lateral vision, and quick responses.

Prior learning

Students have sung and performed contemporary waiata and haka and have an ongoing te reo programme in the classroom.

Suggested learning outcomes

The students will:

  • listen to and describe features and purposes of traditional waiata and haka (PK, UC);
  • demonstrate the ability to follow the gestures and pronunciation of the leader through performance (CI).

Ideas for activities

As a starting point, work on the following aspects of waiata and haka performance:

  • Dance: rhythm work using whole-body coordination.

  • Kapahaka: warm-up ideas with teacher modeling, using takahia, welcome gestures, typical actions to a plain beat, or guitar, or Te Reo Kori tape.

  • Haka: Toia mai, Utaina.

  • Voice: vocal warm-ups with particular emphasis on breathing, projection and vowel sounds. Use well-known chants, e.g., Haere Mai, Kua Rongo Mai Koe, Ki Ana Au Pakia.

  • Poi, tira, and titi torea work can be used to increase the synchronisation of movements and the cooperation involved in group performance.

Listen to, or view examples, of Māori performance groups. Examples include:

  • St Stephens haka medley on New Zealanders Make Music

  • Powhiri as recorded on Sing Together

  • Maraeroa School.
  • Discuss notable features, e.g.:
    • long phrase-lengths

    • little pitch variety

    • unison voices, with "embellishment"

    • lack of instruments

    • use of feet and hands sounds, possibly poi

    • te reo

    • what the leader is doing

    • the context of the performance.

  • Learn a haka or waiata. While not necessarily "traditional" the following have elements of traditional repertoire and are accessible:
    • Toia Mai, Utaina, (haka)

    • Whakarongo Ake.

  • In te reo Māori, the words and their meaning are more important than the melody or rhythm structure so many teachers will begin with the words, explaining their meanings, and practising the pronunciation.

  • Other approaches are also possible, for example, if a recording is available, the teacher can teach the actions while listening to several repetitions of the music adding words later. Attend to elements of performance:
    • "entrance"

    • formation of lines

    • leadership

    • starting note

    • "exit" or finish.

    Investigate the appropriate occasion for the haka or waiata and try to find an opportunity to perform in this context.

[tip]

Assessment approaches

Formative

During the learning experiences, monitor progress by asking reflective questions:

  • How good is our te reo? What needs working on?
  • How coordinated are our gestures and singing? How can we improve the performance?

Assessment tasks and strategies could include:

  • Teacher, self, and peer assessment to the learning outcomes, with a description of the group performance, including qualities of good pronunciation and appropriate and confident gestures.
  • Teacher assessment of individual ability to talk about the appropriate context of performances.
