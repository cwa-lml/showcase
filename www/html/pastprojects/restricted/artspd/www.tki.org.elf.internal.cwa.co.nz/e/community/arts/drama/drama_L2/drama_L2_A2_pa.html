Level 2: Conversations

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Planning

Possible contexts include using the theme of "Making Friends" and including and excluding others. This could generate conversations about:

  • Making friends, losing friends

  • Shifting house

  • Blended families

  • Possessions, presents

  • Making arrangements for after school

  • A school incident about fairness or being picked on

See below for three individual and paired practise or warm-up activities that can create a broader understanding of conversation, non-verbal communication, and gestures in a variety of contexts.

Warm-ups

Conversation

  • Students work in pairs to consider what makes a conversation. Both students could talk and listen, maintaining eye contact. Students can practise with one looking away while the other tries to attract attention without using words.
  • Students may consider the reason for the communication. Is it to inform, to get support, to share problems, or to ask for help?

Non-verbal communication

  • Students work in pairs to explore and demonstrate use of facial expression and gesture to convey emotion. One student shows anger, hope, fear, joy, and the other reacts.
  • One student models another to show an emotion, for example, delight, anticipation, surprise, or worry.
  • Students may consider:

    • How people talk with their bodies without using words (using only eyes, hands, heads and shoulders, gestures, facial expressions)

    How would people stand and look to show anger, sadness, shock, disappointment?

At this point issues of cultural difference may be raised. For example: Is it appropriate to look at someone in authority or should you downwards when they are speaking to you? Explain to the students that different cultures may have different ways of communicating non-verbally or gesturing. For example, a sideways movement of the head may mean "no" in some cultures and "yes" in others.

Gestures

  • Students practise gestures, for example, come on, stop, turn right.
  • They then develop the gestures into short "gesture sentences", for example:

    • Quick, come here and see what I've got.

    Where did I put it? I left it here somewhere.

    I didn't do it – it's got nothing to do with me.

    Come over here in a hurry – there's been an accident!

    This cake is absolutely delicious. Can I have another piece?

    He's so stupid. I'm not going to talk to him.

    It's really odd – I wonder what it does?

  • Students may consider who uses the gesture, for example police directing traffic, people feeling very strongly about something – extremely happy – (winning Lotto), or very angry (the bad driver in front), sneaky people try to convey messages without others seeing (over the shoulder of someone who is not looking). People who may need to attract attention from a long way away. Try out all of these as movements.

Activities

These activities have provided warm-up and preparation for technique and can now be expanded upon.

  • Students take one of the above actions, or any other starter, and work in pairs to create a longer conversation that has more than one turn each. They may use only facial expression, gesture, and body movement.

Starters that involve the issue of friendship might be:

  • A new person arrives into the class. The following questions are considered:

    • What gestures show friendship?

    What would you talk about to your friends?

    What would you say to the new person?

  • In pairs – one is the new person, one is a member of class. The new person introduces him/herself with no words, using only the techniques already practised.


  • The following possibilities are explored using only movement, facial expression, and gesture:

    • In pairs, the new person watches then approaches a group playing and is excluded. Rerun the non-verbal communication and this time find how to include the person.

    In pairs, one person is sitting alone having lunch and the other approaches and starts to make friends, perhaps by showing and looking at each other's collections, toys, or doing an activity together.

    In pairs, one is teaching the other a skill or the rules for a game or demonstrating the sequence of a game.

Presentation and reflection

Some pairs in the class may wish to share their conversations. Others can watch and talk about the meaning of the conversation and how they conveyed their message through non-verbal communication. They may talk about the gestures used most commonly and what made some more easily understood than others.

Assessment

Assessment activities could include:

  • In the discussion, students will evaluate the effectiveness of the non-verbal communication. There may be the chance for refining work by watching the conversations. They can refine their own technique to create clarity and exactness. A response checklist showing student observation (peer assessment) of clarity and exactness may be a helpful way to document the findings of each student. Results from these sheets can be collated by the teacher and peer assessments and findings shared in a positive way.
  • Students will demonstrate aspects of practical knowledge of drama voice, or gesture, or facial expression during the exploration process. This may be commented on in a verbal feedback circle near the end of a lesson. Using this technique students sit in a circle and each person has a turn to speak and share their understanding of what they did in a particular exercise. For example: "I waved really hard and jumped up high to attract attention when there had been an accident."
  • Self and peer assessment can comment on development of group skills, for example, the ways in which the group sorted out problems
  • Students may engage in a drama convention such as writing in role for summative assessment.
