Level 4: Reflections and Textures

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Throughout the unit or lesson, teachers should be conferencing both individuals and groups of students whilst encouraging them to respond to and reflect on their own work and to the work of others by:

  • reinforcing their knowledge of basic elements and principles.
  • focusing on specific principles using images and artists work for reference.

The following elements and principles will be the focus for discussion in this learning example.

Elements

Texture – Consider the surface quality or appearance of an object, that is can you see and/or feel its texture (e.g., rough/ smooth, scaly/ furry, slippery/ rubbery, soft/ hard).

Tone – To what degree has the artist used tone in the artwork to create reflection or convey texture (e.g., light and dark).

Line – Consider the ways the artist has used line for emphasis, texture and detail (e.g., hatching, dotting, outline pattern. Is the markmaking controlled or loose?

Shape – Consider the outline of the object and how it appears in reflected/textured surfaces (e.g., circle/oval, square/rectangle, regular/irregular, extended/reduced).

Principles

Examine how the shapes and elements are arranged within an artwork e.g., unity, balance, rhythm.

Consider and compare the effect of contrast when opposites are used in close proximity in an artwork, e.g., light/dark tones, flat/textured surfaces, large/ small objects.

Possible starting points

Both reflective and textured surfaces are mentioned in this example. It would be better to concentrate on either one, or the other in any lesson, or series of lessons.

When using objects that have strong reflective, or textured surfaces it is important to consider the light source/ strength of the light:

  • use a spotlight or reading lamp to create a side light;
  • classroom lights (particularly fluorescent lights) often flatten objects;
  • natural lighting can change dramatically when the sky clouds over, or as time passes.
It is also useful to consider:
  • varying the viewpoint (e.g., looking down on, sideways at, or close-up on objects;
  • trying different backgrounds to contrast the reflective/ textured surfaces;
  • placing contrasting papers/ objects in front of the reflective objects to change how they look.

Possible Learning Approaches

Reflective surfaces

Collect a range of reflective objects and materials for a series of drawings. Suggested objects: shiny spoons, forks, stainless steel games, sculptures, ornaments, metal trays, metallic rods, drinking glasses, reading glasses, milk bottles, shiny plastic materials.

Select the three objects or materials that will most effectively demonstrate a reflective surface.

Discuss the different types of reflective surfaces in terms of the evidence of line, tone, shape, and distortion. Display findings on classroom walls and leave there as a resource for further discussion.

Using a soft pencil (e.g., 2B), prepare a grey scale of different tones (very light to very dark) on a sheet of white paper.

Encourage students to identify the tones and keep these sheets for reference during the following exercises:

Distortion

Revisit and practise principles of self portrait drawing using the visual arts elements and principles.

Observe their face reflected in the concave surface of a spoon, or fork. Observe their face reflected in the convex surface of a spoon or fork.

Discuss and explain the observed differences in shapes, lines, and tone.

Using a range of pencils (2B to 6B) looking at either the convex or concave reflection side of the spoon or fork observe and draw a self portrait.

Discuss the final drawings with a focus on the use of line, tone and shape.

Compare with examples from artists such as Escher, Jan van Eyck, Salvador Dali, Henry Moore (drawing).

Textured surfaces

Collect a range of textured objects and materials to use for a series of drawings. Suggested materials: fur, plastic, rubber, tree bark, sand, polystyrene, silk, sandpaper, thistles, metal, slime, clay, feathers, paste, wool.

Ask students to bring in old shoe-boxes, or empty wine boxes to make into texture boxes (a texture box is one with a hole, or slit in it which is big enough to let the student feel inside the box but not big enough for them to see what is inside).

Brainstorm words associated with each texture.

Develop a word bank associated with each texture box e.g., a slime box could have the following words: cold, slimy, wet, gooey, slippery, shivery, disgusting, revolting, horrible, sticky, dank, soft, playable, stretchy, squelchy, seeping, creepy.

Discuss and compare the use of texture in the work of selected artist models (refer to websites under Teaching and Learning Material).

Interpret textures using marks.

Create a series of marks to interpret the textures on paper, e.g., slime could be interpreted by using flowing lines whose edges crept and stretched to the edges of the paper. Select and represent five textures. Consider how these textures can be suggested using crayons, pencils, pens, inks, paints. Display results on classroom wall and use as reference material.

Choose an object with textures on it:

  • use a range of marks to describe the textures. Consider scale, tone, colour;
  • use wet media and a range of mark-making techniques to create a range of different textures.

Using wet and dry media

Both wet and dry media are mentioned in this example. A particular lesson, or series of lessons, could focus on the qualities of either wet or dry media. Each example of wet or dry media below could be the focus for one or more lessons.

Dry media

  • Soft pencils (2B to 6B) – keep them well sharpened, draw with the side as well as the point of the pencil.
  • Charcoal – try adding (drawing directly onto the paper) and subtracting (covering a section with charcoal then rubbing off to leave a textural effect).
  • Crayons/ pastels – try building up thick overlays of crayon, then scratching back with a sharp instrument or drawing into light coloured crayon layers with a pencil.

Wet media

  • Use wet paint onto dampened paper.
  • Apply paint with a knife and a brush.
  • Use a toothbrush/ stippling brush to paint textures.
  • Scrape back thickly applied paint – try this on differently coloured and textured papers.

Students could use these techniques and processes for a series of textural drawings based on their chosen objects.

Materials

  • A range of shiny and/ or textured objects/materials.
  • Pencils (2B to 6B), crayons, pens, inks, paints, dyes.
  • Paper – Consider a wide range of paper (e.g., sandpaper, wallpaper/glossy, matt/rough, smooth or use the techniques of collage, frottage or sgraffito to change the surface quality of paper.
  • Tools for applying media.

Assessment

Possible specific learning outcomes include:

Students will be able to:

  • convey the reflective/ textural qualities of objects in their own work, developing ideas from observation and a study of artists' works (DI/PK);
  • select and use wet/ dry media and artmaking conventions to convey reflective/ textural surfaces (PK).
