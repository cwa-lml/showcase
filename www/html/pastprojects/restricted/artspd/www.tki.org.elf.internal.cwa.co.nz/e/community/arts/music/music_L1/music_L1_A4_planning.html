Level 1: Creating Music for a Special Occasion

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Key teaching and learning concepts

Listening to music, singing songs and playing music intended for special occasions involves recognising the music's purposes and functions:

  • Music for special occasions is about our group identity.

  • Listening to and talking about special occasion music can include identification of the elements of music.

  • Sharing music making can include:
    • reflecting the spirit or feeling of the occasion;
    • demonstrating confidence in communicating to the audience;
    • being aware of the appropriateness of the music to the setting.

Creating own songs for similar purposes assimilates learning and provides a student-centred basis for self expression and musical skill development.

Prior learning

Students need to have participated in or viewed some or all of the following special occasions at home, in early childhood centers, on marae, on TV, at Christmas parades, New year, weddings, church festivals.

Suggested learning outcomes

The students will:
  • talk about special occasions they have participated in (UC);

  • identify the features that made it "special" including the sounds and/or the music (PK);

  • participate in a special occasion in the school community by singing, dancing, or playing in a performance appropriate for a special occasion (CI);

  • identify and describe music that can be used for a special occasion (PK, UC);

  • invent their own music that can be used to mark a special occasion (DI).

Possible learning experiences

  • Recall and sing Happy Birthday in different languages, or a known welcome or goodbye song.

  • Attend a school assembly or community gathering that marks a special occasion. Join in as fully as possible. In class recall and discuss the experience, and if appropriate learn a musical component to be better prepared for the next occasion.

  • Learn a welcome waiata to be sung when new students or visitors arrive in the class; e.g. Kua Rongo Mai Koe, Haere mai te Manuhiri, Toia mai, etc. Learn it following the accepted procedure (Titiro, Whakarongo, Korero) by listening, speaking the words, singing the tune, adding the actions. Do the same for a simple haka, for example: Poutini.

  • Experience a community festival or school assembly that includes music.
    Discuss the place of music at this special occasion, e.g. to create a mood, to march to, for group singing, to build school pride. Ask: What was that song about?

  • Observe through role-play in drama or on video different ways in which people celebrate (Olympic games, New Year, weddings etc).

  • Learn and perform a sasa to welcome/farewell visitors. Include greetings rituals in this sasa.

  • Identify different seasons/religious observances, public occasions, national holidays throughout a year. Observe and discuss how people mark the changes in a year.

  • Listen to, dance, sing, and play music that is special for particular times, for example, Christmas, Easter, Spring, Anzac day, Arbor day. Learn and sing the songs that belong to each special time.

  • Talk about the musical ideas heard and experienced. Choose some features and create a sound piece for a special class occasion.

The following are simple steps towards creating a sound piece for a special occasion, using words, and the musical idea of a fanfare.

With the class:
  • Listen to some fanfares: Fanfare for the Common Man by Copland, the fanfare at the beginning of The Children's march from Bizet's Carmen, Jack Body's Fanfares from Suara - also in his latest CD? - I need to check) Juliet Palmer's Secret Arnold and Dorothy Buchanan's Peace Gareth Farr's Te Papa (track 2).

  • Brainstorm some appropriate celebration words.

  • Arrange them into a chant using repetition and rhythm, and practice chanting them with expression. OR use them to provide the rhythm pattern of the instrumental music.

  • Select some sounds that provide an attention-getting flourish. Such sounds are likely to be drums, cymbals, and in the absence of trumpets you may like to make paper cones that can act as megaphones to enhance the student's voices.

  • Try out a sequence of sounds that could include: a drum roll, cymbal crash, and voice sequence. Try them repeated three times with increasing volume.

  • Consider using your fanfare as an introduction to a welcome song, danc, or sasa.

Assessment approaches

Formative

During the learning ask the following questions so that you can monitor understanding and skill development:

What songs, dances, and music have you heard at birthday parties, feasts, and celebrations? Do you know what they are called? Who sang or played them? How did they make you feel?

When the students are inventing their own:
Do you think special occasion music should be loud or soft? Long or short? How can we use music to tell other people that the occasion is special? How can we make our song more special sounding?

Key questions for assessment in relation to learning outcomes:

  • Are the students aware of the role of music to mark special occasions?

  • Are the students able to reflect on the music through guided discussion after participating in a special occasion?

  • How did the students feel when they performed their own music for others?

  • How well did the students create and perform their own special occasion music (song)?
Strategies and criteria:
  • Teacher comment on the contribution individual students made to the discussion on the special occasion music, e.g., was the student able to use musical language in discussing the sounds?

  • Self-assessment on participation in a welcome ceremony: Did they know the words and actions and carry out some of the welcoming such as: facing the audience, smiling and saying the words clearly?

  • Group assessment: did the group feel that their invented music added to the occasion?
