Level 2: Motifs and Symbols

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Throughout the unit or lesson teachers should encourage students to explore and identify the characteristics of Aboriginal art by:

  • stopping frequently to consider and discuss the meanings of symbols and motifs apparent in the art works;
  • focusing on selected elements that are typically used in traditional and contemporary Aboriginal art works.

Elements

Space – Consider whether the art works have a flat two-dimensional quality or a sense of depth and form.

Line – Consider how the artist has used line, e.g., spiral, hatching, dot, outline, pattern? Controlled or freehand?

Colour – Consider if there is a typical palette of colours identifiable in contemporary Aboriginal art, e.g., monochromatic, polychromatic, subdued, bright, contrasting, harmonising.

Principles

How is the art work organised or composed? Look for any patterns, a sense of rhythm. Consider the use of motifs.

Does it have balance or contrast? Is it symmetrical or asymmetrical, formal or informal.

Possible starting points

View and discuss selected art works

Select and display a range of contemporary Aboriginal art works. Have a few examples of traditional Aboriginal art available for comparison.

Show a range of commonly used Aboriginal symbols as a "key" for the students to use when discussing context of the artworks.

For a superb range of art examples, inform, and printable symbols visit

http://www/aboriginalartonline.com/culture/symbols.html

Investigate the social text of the art works. Consider the conventions used to reflect the Aboriginal culture, e.g., line, colour, symbols. Use the following questions to generate discussion.

  • What kinds of marks has the artist made? (dots, lines, geometric shapes, hatching, spirals).
  • What colours have been used? (possibly make links to the Australian environment).
  • What tools and materials did the artist use to make the painting (brushes, paint, dye, sticks, sponges, stencils etc).
  • Where do you think the artist got ideas for the art work? Refer to ancestry, Dreamtime, the animals, plants and landscape of Australia.
  • What is the painting telling us about? Can you find any signs or symbols that could help us translate the message? (Refer to traditional motifs and their meanings).

Suggestion

Photographs of Australian flora, fauna, and landforms could be used to compare colours and images depicted in the paintings

More information on art elements http://www.cyberschool.k12.or.us/~wallace

Possible learning approaches

Experiment with tools and markmaking

Create a page of patterns in Aboriginal style. Divide a piece of paper into sections. Experiment with pencils, sticks, straws to make different types of marks in each section. Refer to the art works for examples of thin and thick lines, dots, dashes, hatching, spirals, curves, outlines.

Tell a story without words

Read a Dreamtime legend to students. Select a feature of the legend to translate into an art work. Apply the elements of space, colour and line in the same way that Aboriginal artists use them. Encourage students to select relevant symbols to use within their composition so that their art work tells the story of the Dreamtime legend.

Method
  • Choose an object to be the focus of the composition.
  • Using chalk, draw a simple outline of the main forms. (Chalk can be easily covered with paint.)
  • Supply students with acrylic paint in a limited range of colours (black, white, yellow ochre, red ochre).
  • Apply a thin wash of base colour to the entire surface. Paint dots, lines and patterns in clearly contrasting colours to create a vibrant effect. Use each element to add meaning to the design.

Hint

Allow each layer to dry thoroughly before applying another colour.

Sgraffito could be used to achieve sharply contrasting lines. This means the top layer of paint needs to be wet when lines and marks are scratched into it to reveal the dry contrasting underlays.

Activity also effective using crayon and dye.

Tell a personal story

Using similar techniques to the previous activities, students can create their own set of visual symbols to represent themselves or aspects of their lives. Motivations could include family members, interests, the environment, favourite games/toys/food, special events.

Method
  • Develop a set of symbols to be used in the painting. Encourage students to apply the elements of space and line to organise a page using their symbols.
  • Arrange these symbols to create a composition that includes the elements and principles of the art works previously studied. Consider balance, pattern, colour, shape and use of space.
  • Select colours and tools to achieve the desired outcome.
  • Students could work on black or white paper.

Note

Although Aboriginal art has traditionally been painted on a limited range of surfaces, contemporary artists are working on more varied surfaces. This activity could be applied to bark, ceramic ware, paper plates, boxes, or any other appropriate surface.

Materials

Art works to study (see Teaching and Learning Materials)

Drawing – range of pencils (soft/hard), chalk, crayons, pastels, ink, paper.

Painting – acrylic paint, wide and narrow brushes, scratching implements, dye, cartridge or black paper, other surfaces on which to paint.

Assessment

Possible specific learning outcomes include:

Students will be able to:

  • identify and use elements and principles appropriately to create an artwork in Aboriginal style (PK, DI);
  • interpret the meaning of symbols and motifs used to tell a story (CI);
  • use the principles of pattern and rhythm in their own art work (PK).
