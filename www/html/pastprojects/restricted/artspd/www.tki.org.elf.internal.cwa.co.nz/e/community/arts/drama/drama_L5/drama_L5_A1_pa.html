Level 5: Freeze-frames – An Historical Event

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Planning

Possible contexts include:

  • the first moon landing

  • the breaking down of the Berlin Wall

  • votes for women

  • Gallipoli

  • Ballantyne's fire

  • 1951 Waterfront Strike

  • Springbok tour

  • depression riots

  • opening of Auckland Harbour Bridge

  • Napier earthquake

  • Tarawera eruption

  • Wahine disaster

This learning example could be based on knowledge or prior research – but historical background is essential as a knowledge base for the work.

The initial starter may be:

  • a photograph

  • a newspaper headline

  • an eye witness account

Creation and Analysis of Images
Use one photograph of the chosen event to demonstrate the processes of close reading and replication with the whole class.

Close Reading.
Class discusses the scene in the photo. They will initially focus on the differences between denoted images and connotated images, that is, what we see in the picture in terms of body language, facial expression, gesture and positioning and what we understand to be happening.

Careful questioning is the key to reading the photograph accurately, for example, "What do we see happening in the picture?" Literal description is required, for example, "I see a woman with her mouth open and her hand raised." (Denoted images) "What do we understand is happening?" for example, "She is angry with the policeman in front of her." "How do we know?" The expression on her face, the tension in her posture. (Connotated images) Where is the focus of this scene? How do we know? Where is the tenson? How can we tell? What is the mood of the scene?

Replication.
Ask some students to recreate the scene in the photo by modelling the people. They must take up the posture, facial expression, gesture and positioning of the people exactly. The class must be satisfied that the photo has been reproduced exactly before moving on.

Note: Replication of the initial photo with living characters will allow the class to understand more about the scene by being able to manipulate the dramatic elements within it. For example, role (the people) can be moved and given appropriate dialogue and thoughts. Space, focus, tension, and action can all be experimented with.

What might the people be thinking and or feeling in this scene? Teacher can invite other members of the class to stand behind a character and speak appropriate dialogue and thoughts. How are these lines spoken, for example, what is the tone and pitch of voice? How can we change the focus of the scene? or the tension?

The actors in the scene can be closely directed as to how they move and speak. Each character may move and speak once. Run this brief scene beginning and ending with a freeze frame. Discuss the key events that may have led up to the moment and those that might follow.

The class now works in groups. Each group is given a photograph related to the historical event. They follow the process outlined above with reference to their photo. They then list key events before and after the scene depicted in their photograph and create two other freeze frames, one depicting an event before and one an event after the initial scene. Each freeze-frame is given a caption. These freeze-frames are recorded either on video or by photography. This recorded work will form the basis for scriptwriting, preparation, rehearsal and performance.

Creating Scenes and Scriptwriting
From a bank of knowledge:

  • recorded freeze frames
  • thought tapping of characters
  • initial development of dialogue and action
  • discussion of focus, tension and and mood groups develop a dramatic scene from each of their freeze-frames.

  • Initially this process needs to be very focused; action and dialogue need to be tight. This can be achieved by working on a step-by-step process whereby each character moves, speaks, and interacts once and then the process is repeated three or four times to create a skeleton outline. Once an outline for each scene is established it can be fleshed out.

  • Groups need to focus on the shape of each scene and how they create tension and mood.

  • Link the three brief scenes into a short script.

Preparation and Rehearsal

  • Clarify roles

  • Consider and discuss action possibilities and develop the script, for example, gestures and movement

  • Consider what is motivating each character in the scenes and their emotions. Are these clear?

  • Rehearse and refine to ensure:
    • that the actions and the words accurately depict the mood
    • that the tension in each scene is clear

  • View the work and refine use of space. This will entail:
    • Being sure of point of focus for each scene
    • Checking the spatial relationships in the scene (for example, levels, facing direction)
    • Evaluating how gestures are made most effectively

  • Refine and reshape the work as necessary for presentation.

Presentation

  • Presentation of the pieces of scripted drama.

Reflection

In reflecting on their work students should consider which elements, techniques and conventions they have used to create their freeze frames and scripted scenes and why they have used them.

Specific focus questions could include:

  • What are the elements that contstitute the dramatic moments captured in your freeze-frames? Give specific details for each one.

  • How is it possible to change the focus or tension of a moment by manipulating the other elements such as, role, space, action, and time? Make specific reference to your freeze-frames.

Assessment

Through peer, self, and teacher review and assessment the drama will be refined. This will be done by students viewing and critiquing their own work within the group, by sharing and giving feedback on other groups' work, and by formative assessment by the teacher. In this case, the first recorded still images become a point of formative assessment and a resource for the development of the next stage of the activity.

The scripted dramas could be assessed for their:

  • clear and believable story line, that is, plot development is logical and consistent

  • believable and effective characters

  • use of focus, mood and tenson In performance individuals could be assessed on their ability to use and sustain the techniques of facial expression, body stance, and gesture to portray characters and maintain the storyline.

Specific Learning Objectives may include:

  • Students will select and use dramatic elements, techniques and conventions to create freeze-frames and scripted scenes.

  • Students will devise scripted scenes from freeze-frames and rehearse and refine them for presentation.

  • Students will present scripted scenes and respond to the ways elements, techniques and conventions have been use in their own and others' scenes.
