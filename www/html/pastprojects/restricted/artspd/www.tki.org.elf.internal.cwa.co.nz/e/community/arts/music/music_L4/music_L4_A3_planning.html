Level 4: Preparing and Presenting Music to an Audience

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Key teaching and learning concepts

  • Solo performance skills, including instrument technique, accuracy of the performance, interpretation of the music, preparation, presentation, and appropriateness of programme content.

  • Group perfomance skills, including cooperation and negotiation, balance between instruments/equalisation, intonation/in-tuneness, technical fluency, sense of musical style and preparation, presentation, appropriateness of programme content.

  • Establishing a rehearsal schedule.

  • Use of appropriate warm-ups prior to rehearsal.

Prior learning

Students will have played or sung from memory; played or sung by reading music; and/or heard or viewed and discussed the following performances:

  • solo performances by their peers/ others;

  • group performances of a variety of styles and cultural settings, live and recorded (e.g., video/television, cultural festivals, Rockquest);

  • selection of assorted solo/ group performances structured into a public performance (eg. in school assembly, school show, community).

Possible specific learning outcomes

The students will demonstrate the ability to:

  • select and structure musical works for group/ solo performance in the form of a suitable programme for an audience (CI);

  • prepare and rehearse for public performance (CI);

  • perform to an audience demonstrating skills of performance (CI);

  • review audience feedback and identify changes for future performances (CI).

Ideas for activities

  • Practise sight-reading and playing back phrases from memory on instruments, e.g., glockenspiels, keyboards, recorder, guitar, voice. Discuss memory verses music notation use – what will suit group/individual or style of music being performed?

  • View some solo/group performances. Listen and evaluate these in terms of the concepts identified in "key teaching and learning concepts".

  • Listen to a range of pieces and discuss repertoire options. Discuss group vs solo works – do the students consider a mixture of both or should they work to their own strengths? What is appropriate to the style/genre of music considered for performance?

  • Discuss and try out different group combinations and roles in groups via a practical class session – e.g., a jam session on an easy blues chord framework such as 12 bar blues or using the example on Creative Explorer.

  • Discuss best practice for solo and group rehearsals. Provide students with a range of warm-ups appropriate for different instruments/voices involved.

  • Encourage students to keep a rehearsal schedule. Create an example for students to complete and keep updated.

  • Get students to maintain a reflective journal, noting strengths and areas to be developed. Get students to video record themselves and self and peer review noting strengths and areas for improvement.

  • Discuss introductions and presentation techniques for performance. Provide some public rehearsal of these prior to performance e.g., in front of class, record and review through the use of video.

  • To assist feedback process, get students to create three questions each to be answered by a random selection of audience members (for example, Was the programme interesting? Was it well rehearsed? Could you hear each instrument clearly? Did the players seem to enjoy performing?). Bring these to the group prior to performance and discuss which ones will get the feedback required.

  • Make backing tapes using Band in a Box to assist in performance especially where skills, or actual students/instruments are not available.

[tip]

Assessment approaches

Formative:

Teachers and peers should offer ongoing feedback during rehearsal.

During learning ask the students the following so that their progress can be monitored in an ongoing way:

  • How well are you listening to each other and working together to rehearse a group piece?

  • Is there a practice routine in place?

  • What is going well already during rehearsal and what improvements could be made? (suitable speed, level of accuracy, level of musicality displayed, presentation ideas).

  • How will you present yourself to an audience? – positioning, "dress", entry, and exit onto the stage?

  • When you watch yourself on video what key points can you identify for improvement? What is coming across well?

Key question:

To what extent has the student considered feedback and suggested possible changes for further performances?

Criteria for assessment could include:

  • identification of a range of feedback noting strengths and areas for development;

  • identification of a range of possible changes for future performances in the form of future goal setting.

Possible strategies for assessment:

  • teacher assessment and student self-assessment through use of audience surveys, a reflective journal and video review;

  • peer assessment. Complete a questionnaire about other class members, a tick-box idea with quirky titles such as "Top Muso", "Great organiser", "Improving Player", and "Helpful in Group" and so on.
