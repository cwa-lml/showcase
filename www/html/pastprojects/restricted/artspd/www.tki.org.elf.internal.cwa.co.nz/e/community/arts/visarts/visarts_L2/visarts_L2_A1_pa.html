Level 2: Foreground and Background

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

During class discussions and while students are drawing, continually use the art vocabulary being applied to this unit by:

  • stopping frequently to consider and discuss the element of space in both the environment and in the students' work;
  • focusing on selected elements using images and artists' work for reference;
  • encouraging students to respond verbally to their own and others' work. The following elements and principles will be the focus for discussion in this learning example.

Note: These elements and principles should be introduced gradually over several lessons.

Elements

Space – Look at the distance or area between shapes in an art work (e.g., in front/ behind, close/ distant, background/ foreground).

Principles

Foreground and background

The foreground is where parts of the picture appear to be closer to the viewer and the background is where they appear to be further away which creates an illusion of depth/space on a two-dimensional surface. Students may be encouraged to try the following techniques on separate occasions to further their understanding of how perspective can be achieved by:

drawing foreground shapes larger than background shapes (e.g., a person may be half the height of another person in the picture, showing the larger person is closer. This is called change of scale);

positioning foreground shapes lower in the picture (e.g., the feet of the person closer will be positioned lower in the composition than the feet of someone more distant);

making foreground shapes more in focus (sharper, clearer edges, stronger colours, more contrasting) than shapes in the background (e.g., we can see things that are closer in more detail. The effect of distance makes things appear less distinct);

overlapping foreground shapes to cover parts of background shapes.

Possible starting points

Local art gallery/ museum

A visit to the local/ regional art gallery, or museum could be a useful focus for this exercise. If original art works are not available, look for large reproductions in art books, or on the World Wide Web (see Teaching and Learning Materials).

Classroom observation

The classroom itself can be a useful scene. Ask the students to look at the person next to them, then at the students sitting behind them and then at the classroom chairs/walls/windows behind them. Consider and discuss the element of space using art vocabulary i.e., foreground, background, overlapping, proportion, change of scale.

Outdoor observation

Encourage students to look for changes in clarity between distant and close objects. Consider colour as an indicator of distance e.g., atmospheric changes causes distant mountains to appear blue and colour generally seems more dull with distance.

Handy hint

To measure the relative size of objects, hold a pencil in your outstretched hand, position the top of the pencil at the top of the object you want to measure and slide your thumb down the pencil to ascertain the height of the object. Keeping your arm outstretched, compare that height with the height of another object further away. This will give you a comparison between the two objects that you can then transfer to your drawing/painting.

Group discussion

Each group has two pictures to study and they have to identify at least two ways in which a feeling of depth has been achieved.

  • What looks closest to you in this picture?
  • Why do you think it looks closer?
  • Can you find other methods the artist has used to create the feeling of being up close or far away?

Drawing

This exercise could be accomplished using only lines. Lines describing closer objects could be darker or stronger than lines describing objects further away.

Montage

Construct a scene through cutting and pasting. Use cut shapes to show foreground and background by selectively sizing, placing, and overlapping them.

Painting

A painting could be developed in a similar way to the montage by painting foreground and background objects separately. Textural rubbings and the use of different paint techniques could effectively convey the illusion of space. (Use softer blurry effects for far objects, sharper, brighter effects for near objects.)

Materials

Art work to study – see Teaching and Learning Material
Drawing materials – pencils, chalk, crayons, felts, ink etc
Painting materials – acrylic, brushes, sponges, card, paper, towels, cartridge, manila paper
Montage materials – magazines, coloured papers, textured papers, paste, scissors, cartridge paper.

Assessment

Possible specific learning outcomes include:

Students will be able to:
  • identify and talk about two ways that foreground and background can be created in an art work (CI);
  • apply some of the pictorial devices to achieve a feeling of depth in their own artwork eg scale, overlapping, perspective, tone (PK);
  • develop visual ideas from observation (DI).
