Level 4: Hotseating – A Significant Moment

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Planning

Suitable contexts or starters for this learning example include strong characters within literature (short stories, poems, novels).

Characters to explore

From the text source, teacher and students select a character to explore. The character may have:

  • Many choices to make
  • A past with many possibilities
  • Intentions, or motivations for actions likely to challenge students' thinking.

A possible character might be Jan in The Silver Sword by Ian Serrallier. The drama work could occur as the class is working on the novel to encourage and develop deeper understanding of characters.

Role-on-the-wall

This may be used to focus students' thinking about a particular character, for example, Jan.

Activities:

  • Working collectively, students draw the outline of a figure on a large piece of paper.
  • On the outside of the drawn figure students record physical aspects of character, such as clothing and appearance.
  • On the inside of the figure students record feelings or what the character is like inside, for example, unsure, sad, scared, hungry.

This work may be displayed as a resource to be added to as work progresses and as a source for other writing, for example, poetry, journal writing, letters.

Freeze-frame images and speaking thoughts aloud [tip]

After reading early chapters of the book students select several key moments/ scenes related to the main character.

In small groups students develop a freeze-frame image showing the characters involved in that event.

Teacher encourages each group member to think of the words that their character might say with reference to the main character.

Teacher steps into freeze-frame and taps each participant on the shoulder and gets each to speak aloud their feelings, thoughts, in a word, or phrase.

After hearing from each participant, students should reflect on what they have learned about the central character and his relationship with other characters. They may note findings and develop a new wallchart showing this information.

An alternative method of creating freeze-frames and spoken thoughts works as follows:

  • After reading the early chapters of the book students divide into groups of five or six and create a sequence of five key moments as freeze frames. Their task is to convey the particular moment to the audience and for them to guess what is happening.
  • Firstly students display their sequence to the rest of the class as an "open/close" exercise in which the audience opens and closes their eyes so that the participants in the freeze frame can reposition themselves. The effect is cinematic and creates a sequence film/type frame.
  • Secondly students should run through their freeze frames again with the purpose of having the class explain what is happening from the story.

Hotseating [tip]

Hotseating is an opportunity to interview a character from the story and to explore ideas and attitudes about the role. It may be done as a whole class or in smaller groups.

Teacher may be in the hot seat to model the process first - then students follow.

Preparation of questions is important. In pairs students can prepare the questions for the characters. Questions could be open-ended, related to the story, or prompt a creative or imaginative shift, for example, encouraging students to think beyond the bounds of the story – the moment before, the moment after, ten years ago, ten years into the future. Questions may be recorded on the whiteboard as prompts. Phrases like "Can you tell us more about? How did you feel when...? Can you describe... in more detail? Are there are important reasons for? Can you discuss the process of...?" may produce more conceptual answers.

Students could interview each other in pairs as preparation.

A volunteer is chosen to model the process in front of the class.

Class members question the character in role. The teacher's role is to protect the student taking the role in case of inappropriate questions. The teacher may also act as prompt.

It is important that the teacher sets the boundaries for hotseating. Guidelines should ensure:

  • That only questions are asked it is not a conversation nor the opportunity to set someone up.
  • That questions are addressed to the character and to the role and not to the person playing the role. For example, "Jan, when you....?

  • That if there is more than one character being hotseated at the same time, the characters should not interact with each other.

De-roling

It is important to de-role at the end of a hotseating session. In role, students may speak intense feelings from a perspective other than their own. This involves imaginative identification and the need to be protected when stepping back into reality.

There are a number of ways of doing this:

  • making a physical move, for example standing up, stretching, turning around, and then returning to where we are here in the classroom;
  • closing eyes, directing a silent thought to the role, and then moving away and back into the groups;
  • removing the sign, for example taking off a jersey, or clothing, that marks the boundary between the role and reality;
  • shaking out, or brushing off, or taking a big breath as students come out of the role before they return to the class.

Selecting props

The information gained from this work can now be used to select props or clothing to develop the character further and to work towards developing a 90 second – 2 minute polished scene. Ideas include:

  • Discuss and touch significant symbols, including clothes, or props in a person's life. In Jan's case these include the box, shoes, the sword, an animal, food. These (except the animal) could be brought in an old suitcase and laid out for students to examine. A feather could work as a substitute sign for the chicken. A preparatory game in which students sit in a circle and the objects are passed behind their backs to allow them to examine the texture of the items and to try and guess what they are, will add tactile and thus imaginative involvement.
  • Discuss key scenes in the person's life. A timeline could be drawn up. It is important to physicalise this and thus useful to roll out a long sheet of newsprint and have students record the beginning of the character's journey and the end, then divide up the paper by first seeking an important middle event for students to relate to.
  • Groups decide on on which scenes they will create and which props could be significant. They need only imagine the props during rehearsal and as they are able to share the suitcase when presenting.
  • Students seek dialogue in the text that will help them to create their scene.
  • Students may add to this dialogue by improvising several more lines.
  • Students create a 90 second – 2 minute scene. A spread of scenes that will convey points in the character's life.
  • Students present the scenes.
  • Students video, watch and discuss their scenes.

Presentation

Presenting the scene provides options. Teachers need to choose a structure for presentation and guide students clearly through it. It may be a brief scene in which characters move and speak that finishes with another freeze-frame. This gives the students parameters within which to work.

Other ideas from the other conventions could be incorporated into a presentation. Speaking thoughts aloud might be used as the basis for a scene where the central character speaks thoughts and others use props to mime action.

The scene could be framed within the character's thoughts, The main character might be speaking his/her thoughts aloud, while others give focus by showing a series of freeze-frame images, for example "I remember the night the soldiers came." In this way students could develop an understanding of the convention of split stage.

The scene could be framed within a character's dream with students indicating the elements of rising tension through movement or increasing focus on the main character by moving in on them in slow motion. In this way students could develop an understanding of the convention of dream sequence.

Assessment

  • Students will select an appropriate prop or piece of clothing for a role and write or speak about aspects of the role it suggests. These may include for example social status, age, occupation, gender and attitude. They will also talk about how this is carried into their portrayal of a character through use of voice, body and space and discovery of the power of new or combined conventions.
  • Students will prepare a reflection discussing their selection of convention and illustrating how the convention explored the character. This could be written or oral. While most students will begin to explore conventions in new ways, excellent responses will develop entirely new conventions.
