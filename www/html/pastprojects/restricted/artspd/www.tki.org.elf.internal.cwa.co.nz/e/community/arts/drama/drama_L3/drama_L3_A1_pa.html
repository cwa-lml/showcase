Level 3: Mime – Conflict, Departure, or Anticipation

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Planning

Contexts

Possible contexts include:

Conflict between individuals in groups

    Possible situations:

  • A confrontation between two social groups – computer students and a streetgang

  • A conservation issue, for example greens versus loggers, or a civic group wanting to save a building versus developers wanting to demolish it for local kindergarten.

  • A town planning issue, for example, the siting of a dump, a prison, a halfway house for recovering addicts, closing of a factory, siting of a telecommunications facility.

Some of these examples might develop as follows:

Departure of refugees, sports or cultural teams, the parting moments after a chance encounter between the gangs.

Situations

  • Leaving for overseas – it is important to try a range of situations, for example:
    • being forced to flee in a hurry
    • leaving a refugee detention centre

Students may explore newspaper articles to allow them to find information about refugees in different situations around the world. Such articles may provide valuable insight into situations involving departure. This may include details of where and when the situation is occurs, which family members are separated as a result, what items refugees can bring to New Zealand.

This may also stimulate interest in finding out about what happens to refugees when they arrive in New Zealand. The Refugee and Migrant Service may be of assistance.

  • Choosing to leave and farewell relatives
  • Wishing the teams luck
  • Insulting one another as different gang members leave after their chance encounter

Anticipation of the arrival of a family or the return of sports team

Situations

  • Attending a sporting event, or selection, for example, Olympics or national team

  • Waiting for news or results, for example, a lottery, a letter, exam results, medical results

  • Waiting and preparing for an event, for example, a disco
  • Awaiting the arrival of another members' family from overseas.

This sequence of events could be woven together as a series of interrelated scenes.

Learning Example

  • Students will show a body sculpture for an emotion using techniques of drama.
  • Students will write about working in mime, recording reaction, and using drama terms (gesture, facial expression, tension, movement, contrast).

[Self and peer assessment could address the links between personal experience, the techniques used in drama, and the messages interpreted.]

  • Introductory discussion about tension. "What are physical signs of tension (butterflies in tummy, clenched hands, sweaty palms, biting fingernails)? What are the feelings involved (feeling uptight, anxious, sick)?" This leads into doing, for example, "Show a time when you felt tense!"
  • Talk from personal experience about situations that might trigger feelings of tension, for example, working towards gathering situations of conflict, departure, or anticipation. Teacher may then encourage situations outside students' own experiences, for example, those seen on the news, or in the newspaper
  • In preparation for devising a scene, the class might practise some techniques in pairs:

Body sculpting

Paired Sculpture

Teacher supplies the name of one of the three emotions being explored and then provides a relevant situation for students to sculpt.

Possible situations include fear of a hidden enemy, surprise at seeing so many friends and family waiting at the airport, confusion following natural disaster, conflict over a lost item, impending shipwreck, hunger as a result of famine, victory over hardship on arrival in a new land.

The sculpture proceeds as follows:

"Tension shown through fear of a hidden enemy":

  • Work in pairs (one, the sculptor, the other, being sculpted).
  • First, the hands are sculpted to show fear.
  • Secondly, the upper body is included.
  • Then, step by step, the whole body sculpture is created, allowing the sculptor to stand back and refine the work and make the image stronger.

Sculpting for intensity

  • In groups of four, one sculptor works to make three sculptures showing three degrees of intensity of the same emotion, for example:

  • iritated, seething with anger, in a rage

  • curious, expectant, ecstatic

  • disappointed, miserable, distraught.

Reflection

After this exercise students may write a journal entry recording their responses:

  • What happened in your body when you felt the first emotion? Describe your hands feet, face, stomach, shoulders, breathing, and muscles.
  • Were there any changes to this when you felt the second emotion?
  • What happened when you felt the last and most intense emotion?

    • This could be a written summary table for the class, including a brief title for the situation, what worked, what didn't, and what might be changed to make the sculpture more effective next time.

Group body sculpting

Work in groups or as a class with half observers/half sculptor clay.

Half the class is lying on the floor with eyes shut. The other half acts as observers.

  • Teacher selects a number of students to be the sculptors.
  • The students' task is to mould the others,who keep their eyes shut, into the given situation displaying a given state named and thus to convey tension through the whole group. For example, the state may be "anxiety" or "surprise".

Reflection

When the tableau is sculpted eyes are opened and the observers provide response.

Groups swap over and repeat the process to gain a shared response.

Sculpting progresses towards movement, mimed action and then a line, word or sound effect from each student. A cannon of movements and accompanying music could link into a sequence creating a short devised mime/movement drama.

The teacher may work to direct or develop a particular linking role such as a single character who is moving countries, a famous sports person who is returning after having lost or won an important international event. Other alternatives include creating a chorus of voices offstage to counterpoint the mime, developing a final scene, creates interesting entrances and exits. However, in particular, it is important to emphasise the most important moment/s of tension which may or may not be resolved and might become the final scene of a thought-provoking drama.

Assessment

Assessment activities could include:

  • Visiting assessor/s or advisers to actively note student involvement in movement drama.
  • Students to keep and submit their own journal log of process to be submitted as their record of work at the end of the performance. This may be visual/oral or written or a combination of all three.
  • Teacher to observe appropriate and active student involvement in sculpting exercises.
