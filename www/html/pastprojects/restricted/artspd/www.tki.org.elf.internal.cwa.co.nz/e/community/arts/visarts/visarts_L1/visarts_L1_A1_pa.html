Level 1: Living Creatures – Clay Models

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

During the processes of drawing and modelling, students can develop the knowledge and vocabulary to talk about art. Teachers should encourage students to use the art language associated with this learning example by:

  • stopping frequently to consider and compare the creature, the students' sketchesand sculptures;
  • focusing on selected elements using images and artists' work for reference;
  • encouraging students to respond verbally to qualities of their own and others work.
Note: Elements and principles should be introduced gradually over several lessons

Elements

  • Line – Consider how you might use line to create detail, patterning or texture on a clay model (e.g., straight/curved, short/long/jagged, smooth).
  • Texture – Examine the surface quality or appearance of an object. Can you both see and feel texture? (e.g., rough/ smooth, scaly/ furry, slippery/ rubbery, soft/ hard).
  • shape – Consider the outline of the object. What two-dimensional shapes make up the object? (e.g., circle, square/triangle, open/closed, regular irregular, organic/geometric).
  • form – What three-dimensional shapes make up the object? (e.g., cone, sphere, cylinder).

Possible starting points

Focusing on the Topic

The sketching and modelling activity may be preceded by dance, drama, or music activities, or discussion, focusing on the topic.

Class discussion could be focused around the following questions:

  • Discuss the characteristics of the creature.
  • What are the main parts you can see?
  • Where do they join together?
  • What different textures can be seen on the animal?
  • How do these shapes/ textures change over different parts of its body?
  • What happens to the shapes/ textures around the eyes?
  • Does the animal have feet?

Possible learning approaches

Explore shape and texture through drawing

Students should begin with a series of observation drawings in which they explore the shape and texture of their creature (what makes a cat look like a cat rather than like a person?).

The following sources could be useful:

  • Inform gathered by the students about the creature through reading and science investigation (e.g., junior readers, picture books and inform texts designed for young students).
  • Video resources (to find out about creature's shapes, features, habitats, movements, patterning, etc).
  • A classroom display of 3-dimensional models and 2-dimensional images (photocopies, books, paintings, photographs and posters).

Using the living creature as a model, make a drawing to record its shape, form, and texture.

While drawing students need to consider:

  • treating living things with respect and sensitivity (noise, movement, gently touching);
  • drawing quickly to capture movement if the creature is moving – alternatively the movement sequence may be photographed with a digital camera;
  • sitting close to the creature so that they can see it clearly and refer to it often.

Explore properties of plastic materials such as clay, or dough

Before beginning to make their sculpture the students should experiment with ways of manipulating clay, such as:

  • pulling and pushing the clay with their hands to make the shape;
  • adding (joining) new pieces of rolled out or shaped clay by smearing them onto the model;
  • cutting away (using wooden tools/ knives) to carve shapes from the model;
  • marking (indenting, or scratching lines) using nails/ matches to make dots, scores, textures;
  • impressing the clay with textures that would be appropriate for their creature (e.g., corrugated card, bubble wrap, coarse cloth, old rubber).

Handy Hints

  • Keep the clay sealed in plastic until ready to use. Clay dries quickly so keep it out of direct sun and advise students to work with it on the bench rather than holding it in their hands for too long. Models can be worked on again if stored in their own plastic bags.
  • If you apply a little water to the part being joined it helps the new piece to stick. Take care not to add too much water as the surface of clay can become unmanageable.
  • To make an effective join: The joining points need to be roughened by scratching (using parallel lines) and joined using slip (slip is a mixture of clay and water roughly the consistency of pouring cream). The join may need support whilst drying.
  • When clay dries it can be painted or varnished. If dried slowly (under loose plastic) it is less likely to crack. If painting, take care not to obliterate any carefully applied textures.
  • To "frame" finished models, glue them onto a wooden block.
  • If a clay model is to be fired it needs to be only up to 1.5 cms thick to prevent explosion in the kiln.
  • Similarly, forms created around a ball of paper will need to be pierced to permit hot air to escape during firing.

Create clay model based on sketches

Pin student drawings onto the classroom wall near the display of 3-dimensional models so students can refer to them (or teachers can draw attention to them) while they are modelling.

Create an animal using the techniques of pulling and pushing, adding, cutting away, joining, marking, and impressing the clay to add shape, texture, and detail that relates to the observation drawings.

Discussion during and after

Group discussion may be based on the following questions:

  • Which elements make this model look like the living creature, e.g., line, texture, shape, and form?
  • What techniques had been used, e.g., pulling and pushing, adding, cutting away, marking, joining, and impressing.
Individual discussion could focus on:
  • How well does the model's shape describe the living creature?
  • How effectively are the joining techniques holding?
  • How effectively have textures been used on the model?

Materials

  • Living creature
  • Drawing paper
  • Dough/ clay, water, newspaper, plastic bags, wood for bases, paint, or varnish small bowls for slip, toothpicks or skewers for hatching/joining
  • Textured materials (i.e., corrugated card, bubble wrap, coarse cloth, old rubber, old combs)
  • Biro pens, pencils, wooden tools, knives, old matches

Assessment

Since the sketches were more of a means to an end rather than an end in itself (for this exercise), the assessment/ discussion could focus more on the finished models. The teacher may draw attention to characteristics developed in the sketches that could have been followed through in the models.

Possible specific learning outcomes for assessment:

Students will be able to:

  • develop the main shapes/ parts of the animal effectively using clay as the medium (PK);
  • apply the details (textures and features) inventively using clay tools and techniques (PK, DI);
  • use the characteristics of clay with control and confidence (PK);
  • create a three dimensional animal in response to observation and imagination (the sculpture relates to the original model (DI);
  • begin to recognise and talk about qualities in their work and how they can improve it (CI).
