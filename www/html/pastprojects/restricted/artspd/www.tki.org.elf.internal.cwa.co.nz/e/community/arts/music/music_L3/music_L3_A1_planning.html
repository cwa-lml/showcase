Level 3: Experimenting with Sound Contrasts

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Key teaching and learning concepts

Using the elements of music through a range of tone colours and sound production devices to explore sound contrasts:

  • Pitch – high to low, combinations of pitches making melodies or tunes

  • Dynamics – soft to loud

  • Tempo – fast to slow

  • Rhythm – short to long durations, patterning of sounds

  • Silence – absence of sound

  • Texture – a piece of music' "density" of sound, which may range from thin (e.g., a single strand or instrumental line) to thick (e.g., several strands or instrumental lines)

  • Manipulation – thoughtful use of sound contrasts

  • Contrast – differences within and between the elements of music and tone production techniques

  • Tone colour – the specific tone or sound quality of a musical instrument, combination of instruments, or sound source (e.g., a saxophone and a trumpet playing the same note each has its own distinctive sound); also called timbre. Sound sources include found, environmental, vocal, instrumental and electronic sounds)

Vocal sounds
For example, tongue clicking, onomatopoeic effects, yelling, chanting, singing.

Found sounds
For example, classroom furniture, home pots and pans, jungle gym.

Environmental
For example, sticks, flax, stones, shells.

Instrumental
For example, koauau, piano, guitar, violin, trumpet, drums.

Electronic
For example, computer, synthesiser, drum machine, electric guitar/ keyboard.

Prior learning

Students need to have:

  • experienced and identified the elements of music through focussed listening;

  • explored, experimented with, and identified a range of sound sources;

  • listened to a range of musical.

Suggested learning outcomes

The students will:

  • identify, describe and respond to contrast within and between the elements of music through a range of music examples and sound sources (PK);

  • analyse and explain how own and others' work demonstrates making, altering and combining sounds (PK).

Ideas for activities

  • Experiment with sound. Begin with a sound source and share ideas on how to manipulate and alter sounds. Predict and discuss the effects, for example:
    • Change the sound of a guitar string by muting with some pegs.
    • Change the sound of a piano by weaving pieces of paper around the strings.
    • Change the sound of a recorder by blocking some holes.
    • Change the sound of a metal instrument by playing then touching it.
    • Change the sound of the vowels by altering the shape.
    • Use different fillings inside shakers.
    • Use the bass and treble effects on an amplifier to pan (shift sound) across speakers.
    • Use a microphone to amplify and distort the voice.
    • Manipulate the controls on an electronic keyboard to produce a range of programmed sounds.
    • Produce different sounds by scratching, rubbing, striking, blowing et al using a range of metal, wood, skin, other sound sources.


  • Devise a system for classifying sounds, for example:
    • for sound materials (wood, metal etc);
    • for method of sound production (blow, shake etc);
    • for the various elements of music (pitch, dynamics etc).


  • Devise a system of notation to represent degrees of contrast and sound production techniques, for example
    • graphic
    • conventional


  • Listen to and discuss the use of contrast in composed music, e.g., film music especially where effects are used to heighten the mood created, programme music such as The Hall of the Mountain King, Hoedown by Aaron Copeland, Other Echoes by Eve de Castro Robinson, Deep Sea Gongs by Gareth Farr.

  • Encourage students to find their own examples of contrasts in music form within their home collections of recorded music, or from the radio/television.

  • Investigate music contrast through studying music encountered by students in their community.

  • Students share with others their individual exploration of a range of sound sources and selection of contrast made. Encourage students to discuss differences in the music they have made, the ways the elements have been made, altered or combined, and why they made the choices they did.

  • Take a chant, such as a sports or protest chant, and experiment with contrasting ways of performing it. Share performances and analyse methods used.

[tip]

Assessment approaches

Formative

During the learning experiences ask the students the following so that their progress can be monitored in an ongoing way:

  • Which elements of music are being contrasted and how?
  • How could you change your sounds?
  • How could you combine any of your sounds?
  • How did you make your musical contrasts?
  • What would this sound like on a different instrument? Played using a different technique? Faster? Slower? Longer? Shorter? Higher? Lower? Louder? Softer?
  • Which worked well and why?
Key Question: How well has the student responded to, analysed and described contrasts in the music heard and experimented with?

Assessment tasks could include:

  • Identification and description of a range of uses of elements – worksheet based on listening activity.
  • Identification and description of combinations of elements used to create contrasts – group research task.
  • Application of learning through a practical task – eg create a sound contrast piece to reflect a storm, one minute in length, and perform it to the class.
  • Interpretation of the effectiveness of contrasts within a range of musical contexts – reflection activity on own and others' work.

Possible strategies for assessment include:

  • Observation, discussion, reflective questioning, conferencing (teacher/student, student/student, performance and verbal summary of work carried out in practice as audio, visual, physical, written response/s.
