Level 5: Plaiting and Weaving

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Elements and terms

canon a choreographic device or structure in which movements introduced by one dancer are repeated exactly by subsequent dancers in turn
choreographic device a specific way of manipulating movement to develop dance choreography, e.g., repetition, reversal, accumulation
choreographic structure the way in which moment is organised and shaped to create a dance, e.g., ABA, theme and variation
improvisation spontaneous movement in response to stimulus

Possible starting points

Visit a marae and look at the tukutuku panels and other woven objects.

Visit your local museum and view baskets, mats, and cloaks from Māori and Pacific Islands cultures.

Bring in a guest speaker who is a weaver or artist to talk about the creative process of making baskets, mats, cloaks etc.

Learn traditional Māori, Samoan, or Cook Island dances, focusing on the different formations in the dances.

Learn songs, e.g., Tui Tuia, that take weaving as a theme.

Contact the National Library and get videos of traditional Māori and Pacific Island dances and singing.

Possible learning approaches

Introduce students to both traditional and contemporary ideas of weaving in Māori and Pacific cultures. Discuss woven objects that have both functional and symbolic associations. Stress the importance of researching an idea thoroughly before physically creating a dance or dance drama.

Research

  • Research the making of mats (whariki), baskets, and tukutuku panels.

  • What are the names and symbolism associated with the different weaving patterns?

  • Research weaving traditions in Pacific Islands cultures.

  • What are the names and symbolism associated with their weaving patterns?

Small group activity

  • Read or create a story about the weaving process. Divide students into small groups. Direct each group to discuss a situation, e.g., gathering flax, preparing flax for weaving, making, weaving, plaiting, and then using the finished product.

  • Each group tells their story through mime and freeze frames. Tell the same story through dance using different floor patterns and three different choreographic devices, e.g. unison and contrast, canon.

  • Listen to He Rourou Ma Koutou (Hei Waiata Hei Whakakoakoa, Learning Media Ltd). Discuss the words, rhythm, tempo etc. Improvise to the music to explore the meaning of the words in movement. Learn the words of the song. Some students dance while the others sing.

  • Listen to Takapau Horanui (Te Ku Te Whe, Hirini Melbourne and Richard Nunns). Discuss the symbolism of the birth mat. Talk about the use of ritual and create a dance drama to this piece of music.

  • Using ropes as a prop create a woven pattern in small groups – over, under around, through between weave, mingle converge.

Assessment

Possible specific learning outcomes include:

  • Students can research a topic and use the information in the presentation of a dance or dance drama.

  • Students can use a range of choreographic strategies to tell a story through dance.

  • Students can demonstrate an understanding of traditional and contemporary weaving traditions of Māori and Pacific Island cultures.
