Level 4: Poster Design

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Teachers should be conferencing both individuals and groups of students whilst encouraging them to respond to and reflect on their own work and to the work of others by:

  • reinforcing their knowledge of basic elements and principles;
  • focusing on specific principles relevant to the design process and using images and artists' work for reference.

The following elements and principles will be the focus in this learning example.

Elements

Line, tone, colour, and shape.

Principles

Composition – consider how different components are selected and arranged in posters (e.g., is there unity, balance, rhythm).

Possible starting points

Talk about posters, their purposes, their components

Share any recent experiences of posters seen in the school, community or city, billboards etc

Consider a variety of typefaces

Collect old newspapers and magazines from home – each student will require one newspaper/ magazine and a pair of scissors.

Students should go through the magazine/ newspaper and cut out examples of a range of typefaces – fun, serious, heavy, light, colourful, dull, thin, fat, old fashioned, modern, spaced out, crowded, easy to read, difficult to read. These should be collated by category and glued onto sheets and displayed as a class reference.

Engage students in a class discussion about the typeface examples collected. Consider:

  • What makes some look like fun rather than serious, old fashioned rather than modern?
  • What would the big type faces be most useful for?
  • What sorts of typeface would be useful for a poster design? Why?
  • Which typefaces are easiest to read at a distance?
  • What makes them more readable?
  • Which typefaces would appeal most to a student audience? Why?

Ask students to point out examples of typefaces that could be used to advertise the following: a jazz ballet performance, a rugby match, play stations, the Maths and Science Fair, a pop concert, or a skate boarding competition. Encourage the students to give reasons for their suggestions.

Select and display posters on various themes for discussion.

As the poster must communicate in a direct way in order to attract attention, the impact of the poster must be established at first glance.

Discussion of selected posters

Focus points for discussion:

  • Would you look at this poster twice? Why?
  • What message does the poster communicate (consider every aspect of the inform presented and isolate the single most important element which conveys the message).
  • Does the image convey the message clearly? If not, how could it be improved? If so, why?
  • Does the text convey the message clearly? If not, how could it be improved? If so, why?
  • How many different types of lettering have been used on the poster?
  • What size, colour, font/character, spacing has been used and why?
  • Consider what media and techniques such as painted images, prints, computer generated images have been used
  • Is the use of colour, line, tone, shape or form important in this poster?

Possible learning approaches

Design a poster

Design a poster to convey ideas related to the theme of one of the posters discussed.

Note: when producing a poster, the range of materials and techniques which can be used is extremely wide. The availability of materials, the amount of time you have to produce it, and the situation in which the poster will be displayed must all be considered.

  • Brainstorm possible concepts for the text and the image and how to combine them in a layout.
  • Develop ideas in a small scale format and work on these small scale sketches until satisfied with one of them.
  • Select an approach. Refine it by redrawing the image and considering its placement in relation to the selected text wording. (Students could use collage or draw, paint, or print images.)
  • Consider possible typefaces. Select one and place and size for maximum effectiveness. (Students could trace or draw their own letters, use a stencil, or computer to generate text.)
  • If using computer software, try out a range of compositions, colours, type faces before deciding on a final design.

Final poster design

Complete the final poster ensuring that:

  • design conventions (elements and principles) have been used effectively
  • Images and text clearly relate to the poster theme
  • Key ideas have been communicated clearly.

Class appraisal

At the conclusion of the exercise, discuss the posters using the following key questions:

  • Which poster most effectively conveys its main idea?
  • Which type face is most easily read at a distance?
  • Which type face best relates to the theme?
  • Which images are most effective? What makes them effective (scale, tone, placing, colour)?

Materials

  • Posters for discussion
  • Newspapers and magazines for cutting-up
  • Scissors
  • Paper
  • Paste
  • Pencils and other drawing and painting media
  • Access to technology such as photocopiers, computers, scanners

Assessment

Possible specific learning outcomes include:

Students will be able to:

  • apply their knowledge of elements and principles and the conventions of the design process in order to create an effective poster (PK);
  • use research imagination and observation in developing ideas in response to a brief (DI);
  • interpret and describe how different approaches to the use of media convey different ideas in their own and others' posters (CI).
