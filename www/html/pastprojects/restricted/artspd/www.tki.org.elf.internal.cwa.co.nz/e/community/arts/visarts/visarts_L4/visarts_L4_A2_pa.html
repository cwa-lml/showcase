Level 4: Photography – Pinhole Camera

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Throughout the unit or lesson, teachers should be conferencing both individuals and groups of students whilst encouraging them to respond to and reflect on their own work and to the work of others by:

  • reinforcing their knowledge of basic elements and principles
  • focusing on specific principles using images and artists work for reference.

The following elements and principles will be the focus for discussion in this learning example.

Elements

Focus on texture, tone, and shape.

Principles

Composition – Consider how the shapes and elements are arranged in the photographic image (e.g., unity, balance, rhythm)

Contrast – Examine the effect when opposites are used in close proximity (e.g., light/dark tones, warm/cold colours, large/small shapes)

Movement – Consider the effects created by changing the position of the camera (e.g., fast, slow/stationary, jerky/smooth, towards/away from). Consider the effects created when photographing a moving image.

Possible starting points

Research

Study the basic scientific principles of light and its effects in photographic recording processes to discover how technology and scientific principles can be used as a tool for developing ideas and images

Study the camera obscura, the early history of photography (eg the 16th Century camera obscura) and the work of contemporary artists who use pinhole camera photography for expression and image making (e.g. Ruth Thorne-Thompson, Garcia

Discuss the use of this art making tool and the associated technology for the production of work with immediate and mysterious effects.

Construct a pinhole camera

Construct a light excluding pinhole camera by selecting a box which can easily be opened and resealed with tape for loading and unloading light sensitive paper. Paint the interior with black paint. Cut a 10 mm square out of the surface of the box. Cover the hole with tin foil using a sealing tape. Pierce the centre of the piece of foil with a pin to create an aperture. Use a piece of light excluding tape to seal the aperture on the outside.

View the following New Zealand website for simple instructions on building a pinhole camera http://www.qdesign.co.nz/pinhole.html

Possible learning approaches

The school community may contain people with the necessary expertise to assist with setting-up the following simple camera operations.

Photographing the images

Open the camera in a darkroom with the red light on. Attach a sheet of photosensitive paper to the back of the camera with small amounts of double sided tape. Be sure the light sensitive surface faces out, towards the aperture, on the front of the box.

Close the box and seal any edges with light excluding tape.

Select a subject which is one metre from the fixed camera in sunny conditions.

Take the camera to the selected subject and tape it to a fixed surface which will function as a tripod e.g., a stool or fence.

Expose the film by removing the tape from the aperture for 20 seconds. Replace the tape carefully.

Also consider:

  • experimenting with light sources
  • capturing the effects of movement through changing the exposure times
  • exploring the qualities of texture within an image.

Developing the photographs

IMPORTANT Attention must be given to safety issues relating to avoiding contact with photographic chemicals.

Produce a paper negative:

  • On your return to the red, lit darkroom undo the tape sealing the camera.
  • Carefully remove the photosensitive paper which will be put through a process of chemical development and then into a stop bath of water.
  • Fix and wash the paper negative in running water to remove any chemicals before drying.

Produce a paper positive:

  • Under red light conditions place a fresh piece of photosensitive paper emulsion side up on a the flat surface under your enlarger, or improvised light source (e.g., torch/lamp).
  • Place the paper negative image side down on top of the emulsion of the positive paper and cover with a sheet of glass.
  • Expose the papers to light from the enlarger for the recommended exposure time.

Reflect and discuss

  • Analyse, reconsider, and determine the exposure times of the paper negative and the positive image.
  • Analyse the images produced by the group, discuss and suggest new subjects and light conditions for capturing images.
  • Encourage students to make a series of images which explore this technology and its ability to generate art works.

Reprint images

  • Re-photograph and reprint a short series of photographs trying out different effects and arrangements based on the analysis.

Materials

The following items and materials will need to be organised and considered in preparation for this activity:

  • A photographic darkroom, or improvised darkroom space, with a sink and cold running water, chemicals and containers for developing and fixing images, photosensitive papers, enlarger, or focused light source and a red light to avoid exposing the paper during the processes of producing an image in the darkroom.
  • A supply of rigid cardboard boxes with lids or a suitable pre-fabricated container to make the camera (i.e., a large milo/coffee tin).
  • Scissors, cutting objects, light proof tapes, tin foil, rulers, black paint and brushes for painting out the interior of the camera, drying rack or line, pegs.
  • An accessible subject close to the darkroom and illuminated by a good supply of light.

Assessment

Possible specific learning outcomes include:

Students will be able to:

  • apply appropriate skills in handling photosensitive paper and chemicals in the darkroom (PK);
  • use the processes involved in pinhole photography to produce photographic images (PK);
  • identify photographs from different historical contexts and the purposes for which they were made (UC).
