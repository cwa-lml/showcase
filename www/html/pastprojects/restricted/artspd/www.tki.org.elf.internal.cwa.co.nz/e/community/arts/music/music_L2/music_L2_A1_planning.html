Level 2: Responding to Musical Elements

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Key teaching and learning concepts

  • Tone colour – The specific tone or sound quality of a musical instrument, combination of instruments, or sound source (e.g., a saxophone and a trumpet playing the same note each has its own distinctive sound); also called timbre.

  • Metre – the groupings of beats, usually in twos, threes, or fours; metre is usually indicated by a time signature (a fraction-like figure placed at the beginning of a piece of music).

  • Tempo – the speed of the beat in music.

  • Contrast – changes within and between musical elements, for example, contrasts can be found between loud and soft passages, fast and slow tempos, and high and low pitches; in texture between a solo instrument and many; in timbre, between different instruments, e.g., strings sounds compared with brass sounds.

Prior learning

Students need to have:

  • explored a range of sound sources;

  • moved to metrical music of different metres eg., 3 and 4 time;

  • experienced and identified the elements of music through focused listening.

Suggested learning outcomes

The students will:

  • identify contrast within and between tone colour, tempo, and metre (PK);

  • move in response to different tone colours, tempos, and metres (PK);

  • identify and use words and symbols that describe different tone colours, tempos and metres (CI);

  • Use identified words and symbols to compare different tone colours, tempos and metres.(CI)

Ideas for activities

  • Play the students some short musical extracts that have interesting contrasting features. Discuss the changes and contrasts.

Tone colour: For example, Tokelau by Te Vaka: gentle Pacific Islands music with Tokelauan language, opening track of beach sounds, waves, birds gives way to guitar, drums, voices, male lead singer, voices.

Tempo: For example, Brahms Hungarian Rhapsody 5 (two clearly contrasting themes, the first being broad and sweeping, with slower tempo, "proud walking" and the second, light textured and quick "twisting, flicking"); For example, Samoan Words by Henrietta Hunkin (Fatuga Fa'asamoa I Aotearoa ) which speeds up the second time through.

Metres: For example, Invercargill March compared with Lavendar's Blue (Ears Wide Open).

  • Do some movement warm-ups with the students using locomotor and non-locomotor movements that explore contrasting elements, for example, stretching high/bending low, creeping/ leaping, undulating/ jerking, flitting, plodding, stomping angrily, standing proudly.

  • Get students to experiment with different instrumental sounds and devise appropriate movements to represent the different sounds of the instruments, for example, brushing on a drum – low sweeping movements, "rush" of fine shingle in a rainmaker – a rush forward and back.

Ask: How can we make a movement that goes with this sound?

  • Play a game where instruments (already experienced and moved to) are played behind a screen. Students respond by moving to the sounds according to the agreed ideas already developed showing they can identify the differences between the sounds.

  • Move to one of the suggested pieces of music focusing on contrast that the students hear, for example, students in pairs could agree on 2 or 3 different movements to reflect the contrasts they hear in the music and share these with others.

Ask
Why do particular movements go with particular parts of the music?

  • Play instruments exploring tempos and metres. Play with accents in three time and four time. Set up a steady beat by patting hands on knees. Clap on the first of three or four counts, i.e. Clap pat pat or Clap pat pat pat. Keep repeating the pattern in the metre chosen. Play along to recorded music using the correct pattern that fits the metre of the music.

  • Try making the accent with voice (say "Hoo" on the first of four, "Hee" on the first of three). Use the whole body, keeping the beat with the feet and then try it with instruments. Drums could simply make a louder beat on the accent. Try at different speeds or tempos.

  • Sing and play along to experience keeping the beat in songs with different metres: e.g., Samoan Words (in 4 metre, with faster tempo second time through), E Papa Waiari (in 3 metre).

  • Listen to excerpts of music with interesting contrasts.
      For example:
      Lilburn, Soundscape with Lake and River
      From Scratch, Pacific 321 Zero
      Greig, Hall of the Mountain King
      Psathas, Fanfare for the Millennium
      Paki, Soul Flower off CD 'Tribal Stomp"


  • Devise graphic notation to represent the music.

Ask
Why does that particular symbol go with that sound?
What differences can you see in the "pictures" you've drawn?
Why are they different? Do they match the sounds?

[tip]

Assessment approaches

Formative

Refer to "ask" questions included throughout. Use these as a means to monitor and provide feedback to students on their progress.

Focus learning outcome 1

Move in response to different tempos, timbres and metres.

Key question – How well has the student used movement to show an understanding of different tempos, timbres, and metres?

Criteria for assessment could include:

  • identification of different tone colours, tempos, and metres in music;

  • matching movements to particular colours, tempos, and metres in music;

  • describing to others why a particular movement fits with a particular tone colour, tempo, or metre.

Focus learning outcome 2

Identify words and symbols to describe timbres, tempi and metres.

Key question – How well is the student able to use words and/or symbols to describe different tone colours, tempos and metres?

Criteria for assessment could include:

  • ability to identify particular words and/or symbols to match different tone colours, tempos and metres;

  • ability to describe the relationship between words/symbols and particular tone colours, tempos, and metres;

  • ability to interpret others' symbols/words that represent tone colour, tempo, and metre.

Possible strategies for assessment

Observation, discussion reflective questioning, conferencing, (teacher/student, student/student).
