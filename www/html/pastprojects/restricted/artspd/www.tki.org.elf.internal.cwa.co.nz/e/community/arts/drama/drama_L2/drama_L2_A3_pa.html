Level 2: Process Drama – A Community from Another Time and Place

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Planning

This learning example will extend over a series of lessons, as a unit.

There will probably be three major parts:

  • Learning about what it is to be a community.

  • Creating and building belief in the community the class develops in the drama.

  • Exploring the way the community reacts to the problem.

The three parts will interact and although the community will need to be created early on, details can be added as the process progresses.

A process drama is a form of drama where the purpose is to participate in the process of learning, rather than presenting it to an audience. This learning example involves a sustained creation of a belief in role on a big scale. The process will create a fictional world, and the participants will be involved in taking on roles in that time and place, with action being sustained through the tension of the problem and resolve the tension.

It will be more meaningful if the students can draw on the material they know from a social studies context so that the community is authentically based and provides a firm basis for building belief. This will draw on prior learning and will reinforce and extend learning through drama.

The strategies below relate to each of the three major parts:

  • What is a community?

  • Building the community

  • Defining the community problem

What is a community?

Establish through group discussion the features of community (with reference to social studies). The features which are likely to be important to the drama (and associated questions) could be:

  • It is a shared place (Where is it?).

  • It can have a common language (Which language?).

  • People interact and have a sense of shared purpose (Why do they?).

  • The community has its own identity (What makes it different?).

  • There are different roles and responsibilities within the community (What are they?).

The aim is to create in the drama a sense of togetherness, shared belief, and order in the community, so that, in the drama, the problem brings the students together in a shared sense of belonging and ownership and motivates them to work together to find solutions.

Building the community

The aim of this part is to create the community in the students' minds and in the classroom. Based on the work the students have been doing, their knowledge might be organised in relation to the following questions:

  • Who was there?

  • What did the place look like?

  • What language was spoken?

  • What work did people do?

  • What was special about the people?

  • Did they have any special costume, any sign that was common?

  • What was special about the place, the environment?

  • Was it mountainous or bush covered?

  • What building materials did they use?

  • What was their staple diet?

  • Where was their source of water?

This could be done as a class or group discussion. This moves from a factual base towards the fictional drama world.

Activities

  • Collective mapping

    • Working together with teacher guidance to create a bird's eye view of the community (including hills, mountains, rivers, paths, forests, lakes, buildings). This could be on paper, or 3D (clay, or sand on polythene).

    • Students may create the inhabitants as puppets made out of wire, brown paper and newspaper. They may place them in the world observe the world and share their understanding as one of the characters they or others have developed.

    • Once the map is partially drawn, the students might be asked, and decide on, the place where people gather to talk, or the forbidden area where people do not go. This builds belief in the community and gives it a history. Formal language might be used – "This is the place where..."

    Following this collective decision-making, they could choose a name for the community.

  • Recreate the map in the physical space of the classroom

    • Make the river, draw the street names, use masking tape to show roads, use cardboard boxes or stackable wooden drama boxes to show houses, use symbols and coloured tape to represent important places, mark the common meeting place, or use old fabrics to create the place. Allow the students to create different levels by creating their own signs for example an old ladder covered with green fabric represents the bush outside the town. The room may be totally remodeled so it is important to provide plenty of resources and to be prepared for change.

  • Roles

    In this community what were the roles people had? (leaders, workers, cooks, hunters, keepers of order, entertainers, young/old, tellers of stories, food-gatherers, protectors/ guards, lawmakers). What were the three major rules?

    • An element of mystery may be introduced to foreshadow a problem and build tension for example:

      • Who were the ones who didn't fit in?

      • Who were the ones who were different?

      • Who were the ones who had broken the rules?

    Students choose the role they want to play and build commitment and belief by creating in their groups a chant and/ or movement, to symbolise their group, their role, and their contribution to the community.

    Teacher in role

    Teacher takes role of one who needs to find out about the community. The teacher's role might be the person who is gathering and recording information about the community before the ancient traditions are lost. The role could be of a visiting outsider, or the one who brings news. This supplies the reason for the teacher in role to be seeking information and thus sustains belief. Possibilities for the teacher in role to move story on:

    • Circulate, observe, acknowledge, and comment on students in role groups.

    • In role as an outsider, listen to stories from each group to gather the collective history of the community. For example the story of a time they were brave, the story of a time they saved the community.

    • Convene a meeting or gathering to give each group the opportunity to introduce themselves by performing their chant, or talking about their role.

    This heightens the importance of the situation and gives the students reason to speak with integrity skill and the purpose of impressing listeners.

    It is important to note that the teacher may adopt a lower status in order to encourage student discovery.

Defining the community problem

A community meeting is called. Teacher may now be in role as the person who will present the problem (the bearer of bad news). It is useful to adopt a role at this point that is distanced to some degree from the problem and to be able to withhold or remain ignorant of some details, yet give enough hint to encourage students to work together on solving the issue. Problems may be:

  • A threat from inside for example rumor, gossip, crime, challenge to established authority. This brings about internal conflict that calls for possible solutions.

  • A threat from outside for examplemonster, dragon, conqueror, invader. This brings about external conflict that calls for possible solutions.

  • A natural disaster for exampleplague, flood, earthquake. This brings about conflict in the greater forces of nature that calls for possible solutions.

The problem is likely to have emerged from the detailed history developed for the community and may have historical connections.

Teacher in Role explains the problem. The group discusses the problem and finds ways to address it.

The shared understandings established up till now will be a basis for working with authenticity and commitment.

Some of the work on the problem and the solution will be done out of role. Conventions for entering and leaving role need to be established. These could include removing a scarf or hat which has acted as a sign of role, gathering round in a different part of the room where out-of-role discussion occurs or an out-of-role circle. Some scenes will be played in role. The drama will be slower, at this point, to engage thinking with the issue.

Consequences need to be explored and there should be no quick fixes. The scenes in role might be at another time in the story, for example if the community had to shift because of a natural disaster and the people moved away, the role play might move forward in time to remember the old place.

Assessment

Assessment activities could include:

  • Students to discuss what they have learned about themselves and othersout-of-role at the end of the experience. Teacher to record their contribution with their name beside it. Take all responses.
  • Students to create a visual diary as a social text of the process of their learning after the event. There may be three groups presenting their work at an agreed time.
  • Teacher to observe the students contributing to a mural and commenting on their work as they develop it to explain their role in the process drama. The mural may later be displayed in the classroom but it will be the comments that form the basis of assessment. The teacher may ask two simple questions such as "What was your role in the process drama?" "What was our most important discovery in the process drama?"
