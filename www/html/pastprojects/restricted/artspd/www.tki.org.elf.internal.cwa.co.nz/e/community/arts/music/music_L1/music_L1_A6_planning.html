Level 1: Using Voices to Explore Contrasts

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Key teaching and learning concepts

  • The voice is the first and most versatile instrument

  • Voice sounds can be used to create sound pictures based on the students' world of experience – what they see and know around them.

  • Exploration will be based around dynamics (loud and soft), pitch (high and low), duration/rhythm (long and short), tempo (fast and slow), and gradations of these as required to represent selected environmental features of the local area.

Prior learning

Students will have:

  • explored sounds with voices, body percussion, found sounds and instruments;

  • listened to recorded music in their early childhood settings and in their school activities;

  • routinely used their voices in chants and reading activities.

Suggested learning outcomes

Students will:

  • explore dynamics, pitch, duration and tempo contrasts with the voice (PK);

  • as a member of a group, use the voice to create a short sound impression representative of a feature of the environment (DI);

  • represent sound impressions by devising graphic notation (DI).

Ideas for activities

  1. Use pitch shapes to explore the range of the voice.

    For example, choose a student's name.
    Ask the students to say the name while you "conduct" with gestures:
    • "normally"
    • very low, very high
    • swooping from low to high
    • swooping from high to low

    Draw shapes in the air to indicate pitch e.g.,

    Take a shape and change it – try it:
    • jerkily, smoothly
    • fast and slow (what is the difference?)
    • loudly, less loud, softly but voiced, whispered, mouthed. (Ending with this one creates a quiet moment for the next [whispered] instruction.)

    Get students to draw patterns in the air and on the board to represent these contrasts.

    Ask:
      How else can you say the name/word?
      Can you show me how you would like us all to say the name/word?
      What other ways can you change the sound of your voice?
      What happens if you change the shape of your mouth making it as long and thin as you can, as wide as you can while you vocalise?
      What happens if you "wobble" your finger between your lips while you vocalise?
      What is the difference between your singing voice and your speaking voice?


  2. Use a chant as a basis for exploring voice sounds.

    Take the sounds associated with the chants and explore contrasts. Sometimes mixing up the wrong sound to idea can be fun too and students enjoy planning this. This can be achieved by putting sound ideas on cards (see 4. below) and holding them up at the appropriate times in the chant (eg the wrong card at the right time):
    • Going on a Bear Hunt in HMYC.
    • One Summery Saturday Morning by Margaret Mah.
    • Copycat rap from Kiwi Kidsongs 4.
    • Boom Chick-a Boom – traditional rhyme.
    • Kei Hea te Tuna? Tall Shops, and I am a Dragon by Margaret Hennephof.
    • Who Has the Courage? from Kiwi Kidsongs 7.
    • Weather Song from Tinderbox which has the children using a hum to make the sun, patting knees to make the rain and blowing for the wind.
    • Playground chants (be careful of propriety in words) skipping rhythms, clapping chants can be performed with changes of elements as above.
    • There are many enjoyable finger plays and action songs that could be developed into satisfying chants performed by the class.


  3. Create sound pictures with your voices

    Identify environmental features (natural or manufactured). Have pictures of some or draw some

    Decide whether you want to use words such as the name of the feature e.g., our stream or awa is Waipahihi, our mountain or maunga is Wharangi.

    Describe your chosen environmental feature and look for ways in which it can be represented in voice sounds. E.g. our stream bends and curves and has swiftly flowing water and a small weir.

    Ask: How can we represent that in our sounds?

    Our mountain rises out of the valley in a long ridge then there are steep steps to the very top. It is covered with bush and gorse. From the top there is a wonderful view.

    Ask: Can your voices show all these things?

  4. Represent selected sound impresssions as graphic notation and present in performance

    • Graphically notate the sound impressions by making up pitch cards to represent specific features or sound ideas. For example, a mountain scene or experience a student may have had such as climbing slowly up the mountain, reach the top, scramble through gorse, look at the view, feel the hot sun, run down the other side, stagger exhausted at the bottom, quietly catch your breath and stop for a rest, jump into a lake, splash in the water, drip dry, sleep in the grass etc.
    • Small groups each take one feature/card to represent in voice sounds, and combine them in performance to create a panorama! Swop cards and see if students can read and perform each others' cards.

    Ask: Do the performances sound different? Why? Are the sound picture cards easy to perform? How can we make them better? What have have we created if we change the order we put these cards in? eg starting at the top of the mountain, changing the shape of the river's course. What happens if we try them upside down? (For example, jumping backwards into the lake).

  5. Listen to real performers explore contrast with their voice

    • Listen to Bobby McFerrin describing with his voice some of the features of the land in How the Camel got his Hump.
    • Listen to and discuss a range of voice types and techniques on available recordings, e.g., throat singing, haka and chants, music from a range of countries such as in the Latcho Drom CD and video which demonstrates gypsy music of the world's peoples over time.

[tip]

Assessment approaches

Formative

Give instant verbal feedback to the children on their explorations and discoveries, noting use of elements and naming them to the children.

Use the "ask" questions throughout the learning experiences.

Relating to learning outcomes:

Key question: How confident is the student in using the voice imaginatively?
Criteria for assessment could include the use of dynamics, pitch, rhythm, and tempo contrast.

Key question: How well can the student interpret an environmental feature into sounds?
Criteria for assessment could include creation of a clear contrast between significantly different features of the environment e.g., the change between level areas and rises and falls in the landscape is matched with changes of pitch.

Key question: How well can the student interpret an environmental feature into graphic notation?
Criteria for assessment could include creation of clear symbols and pictorial representations of sounds created which can be re-performed by others.
