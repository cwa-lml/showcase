Level 5: Approaches to Sculpture

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Throughout the unit or lesson, teachers should be conferencing both individuals and groups of students whilst encouraging them to respond to and reflect on their own work and to the work of others by:

  • reinforcing their knowledge of basic elements and principles;
  • focusing on specific principles, using images and artists' works for reference.

The following elements and principles will be the focus for discussion in this learning example. Their use should be studied in artists' works first.

Elements

  • Form (mass, volume, weight)
  • Line (edge)
  • Shape (plane)
  • Colour

Principles

  • Proportion/scale
  • Movement
  • Balance,
  • Unity, tension
  • Contrast and variety
  • Emphasis and dominance

Possible starting points

Discuss sculpture as an art form.

Invite students to think of as many sculptural works as they can in their local environment. Collect photos, postcards, photocopies etc. of sculptures.

Take students on a sculpture trail. Take photographs of sculptures. Give them a sculpture worksheet to record their responses.

Provide visual resources to stimulate interest – posters on the walls, books, slides etc.

Lead a discussion on the nature of sculpture. Include such questions as: consider what is sculpture; what are sculptural issues; list the ways in which sculpture is different to painting.

Consider which qualities sculpture and painting have in common?

Sculptural issues

Get students to brainstorm a list of sculptural issues or aspects central to sculpture. The responses should include weight, scale, form, mass, volume, plane, line, contour, edge, shape, structure, materials etc. Work with students to enable them to define these terms in their words.

Possible learning approaches

How to analyse sculpture

Introduce students to the concept of different sculptural approaches, where certain sculptural issues are more dominant in a work than others.

Looking with them at a range of sculptures, ask them to categorise the works under the following headings:
[LINEAR    PLANAR    MASS/FORM    MATERIALS BASED (where the materials used are a dominant feature and can add to or change the meaning of a piece)].

The following may be a useful list of artists to start with:

Linear: Neil Dawson, Jacqueline Fraser, Greer Twiss.

Planar: Naum Gabo, Richard Reddaway, Isamu Noguchi, Richard Serra.

Mass/form: Henry Moore, Brancusi, Brett Graham, Charlotte Fisher, Anish Kapoor, Isamu Noguchi.

Materials/process based: Judy Darragh, Marie Shannon, Meret Oppenheim, Eva Hesse.

Analysis task

  • Get students to draw or paste photocopies of at least one work for each different sculptural approach, and make brief notes about each work in response to the following questions, (some art teaching books also have excellent analysis sheets for this purpose).

  • What are the dominant elements or features of the work?

  • Was the work designed for a specific site or not?

  • If so, how does the work relate to the site?

  • What materials and processes were used to make the work?

  • How does the title relate to the form or shape of the work?

  • Analyse works using the list of sculptural terms (scale, form, edge etc)

  • What contrasts can you see in the work?

  • Is colour a feature? If so, to what effect?

  • What do you think the artist's intentions were?

  • Is there an obvious meaning/theme in the work?

  • How would you describe the work to someone who couldn't see it?

  • Give your own personal response to the work.

Practical activities for students

Sculptural drawings

Possible approaches could include:

Set up a simple classroom still life using basic forms e.g., a bottle, cup and piece of fruit on a cloth. Students could make three sets of drawings from the still life, which interpret and explore three different sculptural approaches, using a range of pencils and charcoal.

Line
A series of drawings of the still life, using line to represent the volume or mass as well as the outside shape of the objects.

Plane
A series of drawings of the still life using planes to represent the volume and form as well as the outside shape of the objects. Students could try some drawings, which combine plane and line. The size and thickness of the planes could vary. (Analytic and Synthetic cubist drawings and collages may be useful here).

Mass/form
A series of drawings of the still life that exaggerate and change some proportions to emphasise the object's form and mass. Look at the drawings of Henry Moore (his use of contour line to show form) and Jim Dine (his use of negative space to show form).

Exploring materials

Students choose one drawing, from each of the three series of drawings, from which to construct a model. They will make three models in total, each exploring a different sculptural approach. Provide a range of materials, soft and hard. Students choose materials appropriate to the sculptural approach.

Make sure the model reflects the sculptural approach both in its material and form.

Making a series of works

Students could now decide which of the three approaches they would like to follow. They would need to identify a starting point, using their artists' works studies for ideas here. The teacher might also want to establish some starting points. Students could: use an underlying story (Jacqueline Fraser); begin with an object from the natural world and simplify it to its essence (Constantin Brancusi); begin with an object from the natural world and simplify and distort it to reach an abstract end point (Henry Moore).

Observational drawings

Students should make drawings of their chosen subject matter or starting point, from photographs as well as from observation.

From these observational drawings, they could make a series of sculptural drawings to plan sculptural models. Using the approaches learned in the earlier drawing series (line, plane, mass) ideas could be developed by a process of simplifying or changing the drawings as they progress.

Maquettes

Students could explore their selected sculptural approach using appropriate materials, making a series of three to four maquettes (about 10cm in largest dimension). This work should be based on the developmental drawings.

Contrast in scale, direction, a range of surface textures, and colour, should be explored as appropriate. Artist models studies made earlier should be referred to for ideas and reminders of ways of engaging with sculptural issues.

The students should analyse and evaluate their models, recording successful features and possible areas for further development and resolution.

Further drawings could then be made to plan a final sculpture. This might combine different models, or rework unresolved issues.

Final model

Make a larger final piece that shows a development of sculptural ideas from the earlier work.

Materials

The following materials are useful in this activity:

  • wire, string, card, glue, polystyrene, clay, plaster, wood, chicken wire, paint.

Equipment can include:

  • wire cutters, cutting boards, craft knives, rulers, handsaws.

Assessment

Possible specific learning outcomes include:

Students will be able to:

  • use appropriate sculptural drawing conventions to make a series of planning drawings based on a chosen sculptural approach (PK)

  • use sculptural processes and materials effectively in a series of works (PK)

  • generate, develop and refine a series of drawings and models based on their study of sculptural approaches (DI)

  • describe and evaluate how different ideas and processes are used to communicate meaning in sculptural works (their own and artists' works studied) (CI).
