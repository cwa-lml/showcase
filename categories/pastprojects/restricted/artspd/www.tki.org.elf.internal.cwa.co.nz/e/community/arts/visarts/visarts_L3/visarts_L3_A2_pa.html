Level 3: Self-portrait – Identity

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Throughout the unit or lesson, teachers should be discussing and exploring the different media, materials, and processes that students are choosing to use in their own work. Teachers should be working with both individuals and groups of students whilst encouraging them to respond to and reflect on their own work and the work of others by:

  • reinforcing their knowledge of basic elements and principles;
  • focusing on specific principles using images and artists' work for reference.

The following elements, terms and principles will be the focus for discussion in this learning example.

Elements

Line – consider how the artist has used line for emphasis or detail (e.g., spiral, hatching/dot, outline/pattern, controlled/freehand).

Space – consider whether the artworks have a flat two-dimensional quality or sense of depth and form. Look at the distance or area between shapes in an artwork (e.g., in front/behind, close/distant, background/foreground).

Colour – consider how the artist has used colour to emphasise mood, form and space. Examine if a particular palette of colours has been used in the artwork (e.g., monochromatic/polychromatic, subdued/clear, contrasting/harmonising).

Form – examine whether the artist has used contrasting colour or tonal gradation to create the illusion of three dimensional shapes such as cubes/cones, cylinders/spheres or markmaking (hatching, dotting etc)

Principles

How is the art work composed – Consider how the component parts are arranged within an artwork. Is there unity, balance, rhythm?

Unity - Is there consistency in the use of shapes or colours?

Contrast - Are there contrasting shapes, forms or colours?

Balance - Do parts of the picture balance one another, when similar or even when dissimilar, eg colours, shapes, sizes?

Rhythm - Is there a recurring shape, line or placement of some components that creates linkage or harmony?

Possible starting points

Select a range of portrait, still life paintings, or photographs to discuss and compare. Discussion points:

  • What do you see in the background?
  • What is the artwork called?
  • What is/are the subject(s) of the picture wearing?
  • Where does the subject of the picture come from and how can you tell this from the picture?
  • What objects are shown in the picture?
  • Do you think that these objects have special meaning?
  • Is the picture set in the past, present, or future?
  • What does this artwork make you think about?

NOTE
Students could look at a selected work of a different artist each day (original work, reproduction in book, photocopy, website) and by comparing these artworks could be encouraged to focus on how artists have expressed their identity and arranged selected components to create a composition. Through this analysis they can start to consider the approaches they could use for their own work.

Possible learning approaches

Create a diary

A possible range of themes to explore with the students could include:

  • My favourite things
  • My family
  • My pets
  • Sports
  • Music
  • Where I live
  • My school

Students could select one theme to develop or could try out a range of ideas for a composition about their own identity.

As part of a self-identity unit and over a period of a couple of weeks, students should be collecting and creating personal images, drawings and writings (in diary form) gathering ideas to be used in an art work. They could explore physical features, symbols with personal meaning (cultural, ancestral, family, pets, hobbies, interests, clothing/adornment, favourite things, character traits, landscapes/home surroundings).

Creating a portrait

Discuss what the students might create. Look at artist models (Rita Angus, Tony Fomison, Jean Michel Basquiat, Frida Kahlo, Vincent Van Gogh)

  • What objects would you include in your work and for what reason?
  • Will you show your present/ past/ future or a combination of these in your picture?
  • What might you choose to be wearing?
  • What colours might you use to convey something about yourself and your surroundings?
  • What things or effects would you put in your background if you were painting a self-portrait and why?

Materials

Management Issues
All materials should be accessible to students and there should be clearly established routines for selection, use of, and storage of materials.
  • Work on large cardboard, or extra strong paper (old blinds, wallpaper samples could also be used).
  • Chalk for outlining basic composition based on draft.
  • Papers – tissue, coloured, different weights, cellophane, mylar, wallpaper.
  • Paint – acrylic, watercolour, tempera, oil sticks (can be used with young students with appropriate management strategies).
  • Dyes/ glazes – shellac / tea
  • Found images – photos, photocopied images, drawings, photocopied objects, text as image.
  • Objects – buttons, jewellery, feathers, labels (indicators of identity).

The following processes could be used:

  • Copying/ reproducing – using photographs, photocopies, computer printouts.
  • Scratching through thick layers of paint/ crayon (sgrafitto), sand papering over a dry paint surface, brushing/ washing off while paint is still wet.
  • Layering, smudging, rubbings with pastel/ oil stick/ pencils.
  • Overworking with transparent media (thinned down acrylic, dyes, shellac varnish – works well over photocopied images).
  • Collage gluing real objects or photocopies of objects (boxes, hair, brooches, lace, cloth).

Assessment

Possible specific learning outcomes include: Students will be able to:

  • generate and develop ideas through drawings and collecting images and objects;
  • explain their choice and development of images and use of media (PK/CI);
  • talk about the work of other artists or peers (CI);
  • create a layered and textured surface choosing a variety of media to express personal identity and self-concept (DI).
