Level 4: Scenes on Current Issues

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Planning

Possible contexts include: [tip]

  • A conservation issue, for example, West Coast logging.

  • A local issue, for example, local land water use or resource development.

  • A health issue, for example, smoking, food additives.

  • A sport issue, for example, drug use or money for stars especially through branding and advertisement.

  • A technological / scientific issue.

Learning Example

Students will make a series of three scenes about an issue and use elements and techniques to focus the meaning of the events.

Students will watch presentations, identify elements and techniques, and evaluate their effectiveness.

Select starter resource (newspaper article, photo, TV clip, radio broadcast).

Discuss the events and issues to identify:

  • People involved
  • The situations
  • The tensions generated
  • The attitudes people have to the issue

Additional material may need to be researched through a visit to the library, investigation on the Internet, or use of periodicals.

Identify the main chain of events and key points of focus with potential for presentation. Create a flow chart of these and display all relevant research findings around the walls of the classroom so that information is readily available for students to read and work from.

In groups, select and sequence scenes.

Practise techniques, practise exploring a range of facial expression showing emotions (suspicion, doubt, elation).

  • Model possible ways to the conventions. Groups may select either Mimed Activity as completing a task in secret, making an item, gathering a group and seeking an outcome, performing an action, struggling to achieve a physical task. or Overheard Conversations – telephone conversations, gossip, dialogue via letters, argument between two small groups or two individuals, a secret meeting, opposing chorus or sequenced cannon of ideas.

Make decisions in groups about:

  • Division of six-minute time slot for the three scenes. The scenes may be of varied length but should fit within the given time frame of six minutes.
  • What will be overheard? (people making plans, expressing views)
  • Stages of sequence likely to be best shown through mimed activity and those best shown through overheard conversation.
  • The order for presentation of the three scenes and the running order for groups within the class.
  • Points of tension where "freeze-frame" may be used to end the scene and so focus attention.

Prepare and rehearse the scenes.

Present the scenes.

Assessment

Assessment activities may include:

  • Students will keep a record of decisions made to make their drama more effective, for example, the spacing, how the overheard conversation moved the story on or introduced tension.
  • Teacher may spend a period as a travelling observer studying the group decision making process in rehearsal.
  • Students may fill in an evaluation sheet detailing which contributions were best and why and nominating those who worked well or could have worked better in their group. In this way they will peer assess work. They may also suggest aspects of the work they would like to see changed and list the names of key conventions they have learned.
  • Students may be hotseated to see which side of the argument they supported as the character and why.
  • Visiting assessor/s or advisers to actively note student involvement in presentation of the scene.

Reflection

During or after the mimed activity the teacher may ask some or all of the following questions to assist students to reflect on the work they have just completed.

  • If you had to present your mimed activity in a larger space what differences might you have to make to gesture, facial expression or movement?

  • Try out the same mime but limit your space in half. What effect does this have on gesture, facial expression or movement?

  • Try out different timings and fluidity of movement. How does this affect the meaning of the mimed activity?

  • If you were to add sound to one sequence of the mimed activity show how it might add or detract from the mood established by the original piece?

  • What does this tell you about how mime works?
