Level 4: Contemporary Painters

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Throughout the unit or lesson, teachers should be conferencing both individuals and groups of students whilst encouraging them to respond and reflect on their own work and the work of others by:

  • reinforcing their knowledge of basic elements and principles;
  • focusing on specific principles using images and artists' work for reference.

The following elements and principles will be the focus for discussion in this learning example.

Elements

Line – consider how the artist has used line for emphasis or detail (e.g., hatching, dotting, outline, pattern, controlled, spontaneous)

Space – consider whether the artworks have a flat two-dimensional quality or sense of depth and form. Look at the distance or area between shapes in an artwork (e.g., in front, behind, beside, close, distant, background, foreground).

Colour – consider how the artist has used colour to emphasise mood, form and space; if a particular palette of colours has been used in the artwork, (e.g., monochromatic / polychromatic subdued, bright, contrasting, harmonising).

Form – examine whether the artist has used tonal gradation to create the illusion of three dimensions. Look for cubes, cones, cylinders, spheres. Are the shapes or forms organic or geometric?

Principles

Composition – consider how the shapes and elements are arranged within an artwork, is there unity, balance, rhythm?

Balance – is the artwork symmetrical or asymmetrical, is balance due to colour, shape, or line?

Contrast – consider how contrast is used in an artwork (e.g., black/white, inside/outside, rough/smooth, small scale objects/areas, large scale objects/areas).

Possible starting points

Research

Select from the following contemporary New Zealand painters:

  • Tony Fomison
  • Fatu Feu'u
  • John Pule
  • Michel Tuffery
  • Robin Kahukiwa
  • Nigel Brown
  • Dick Frizzel
  • Kura Te Waru Rewiri
  • Phillipa Blair
  • Shane Cotton
  • Lily Laita

Research their work on appropriate websites. See Teaching and Learning Material. Describe and discuss the ideas communicated in a selected piece of work.

Class Discussion

A selection of the following questions would be useful as initial discussion points:

  • What do you notice first?
  • What is in the background?
  • What do you think the artist is saying?
  • What objects/symbols/motifs are included in the artwork?
  • What is the significance of these components, for the artist, in the given context?
  • What time is the painting set in?
  • What does this painting make you think about?
  • How has the artist arranged the composition?
  • What pictorial devices has the artist used?
  • What medium has been used in this painting?
Note:
Through this discussion students can start to consider the content and approaches they could use for their own individual or collaborative work, using appropriate painting techniques and conventions.

Possible learning approaches

Ideas and images could be recorded in a visual diary.

Brainstorm a possible range of themes to explore. These could include:

  • traditional Māori art: pou, tukutuku, kowhaiwhai, taniko, moko, korowai;
  • Māori/Pacific Island myths and legends;
  • the environment – fish, insects, plants, landscapes, animals, reptiles, birds, trees;
  • social and political issues e.g., the effects of colonisation on the landscape and indigenous people;
  • personal cultural origins;

Select a theme to develop.

Research and select possible objects, images, ideas for inclusion in the work.

Make some preliminary drawings exploring the selected theme.

Using the pictorial devices of the chosen artist model, plan a collaborative or individual composition considering appropriate techniques and media to be used.

Throughout the process students are to be encouraged to analyse, clarify and regenerate their ideas, using processes and techniques.

Follow-up discussion

Discuss and compare the final works with the artist model's work.

Materials

The following items and materials will need to be organised and considered in preparation for this activity.

Dry media

  • Pencils, crayons, pastels
  • Paper, card, pohotocopies
  • Diaries/scrapbooks for recording ideas/drawings
  • Scissors

Wet media

  • Dye, paint, ink, brushes
  • Glue

Assessment

Possible specific learning outcomes include:

Students will be able to:

  • generate and develop ideas through drawing and collecting images and objects and the study of artists' work (DI);
  • talk about the effect of media in conveying ideas in their own and others' work (CI);
  • create a painting using the techniques and pictorial devices of an artist model (PK).
