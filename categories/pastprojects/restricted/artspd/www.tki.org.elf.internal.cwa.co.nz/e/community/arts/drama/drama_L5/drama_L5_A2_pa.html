Level 5: Creating a Character's Life

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Planning

Possible contexts include people whose environments and backgrounds are rich in detail:

  • People and the objects which are significant in their lives.

  • A tramp and and the contents of his swag, for example, food.

  • A bag lady and her supermarket bags containing clothes.

  • A person seeking political asylum (the suitcase).

  • A recluse and the interior of the hut.

  • A person who spends his/her life hunting, or trapping, or whitebaiting.

  • Someone who has escaped into isolation and has been out of contact with people for a long time, for example, someone who was unaware the war had finished.

Learning example

  • Students individually will create one to three possessions for a character.

  • Students may write in role from the point of view of the character discussing the significance of the object/ possession, or may prepare a monologue spoken as the character.

  • Students will take the hot seat in the role of a character, sustaining role and answering questions from class members.

  • Students in a group will use body shape and appropriate technique in a still image.

  • Students will speak aloud thoughts of a character developed from group work.

Discussion

Class discussion about objects that reveal significant aspects about people's lives using focused questions such as:

  • What do people save and treasure?

  • Why are those things important?

  • What possessions do people most treasure and value?

Teacher generates talk about people the students know and the things that are valued. Students bring an object that is significant to them. They share this object with a partner and demonstrate how they came by the object and why it is significant to them, for example, if it was a prize they create the moment of presentation. *Class discussion focuses on the different types of objects that are significant in people's lives. Use focusing questions such as:

  • What types of objects do people save and treasure?
  • Why are these objects important?

Developing the character

One group could work on:

  • discussing the character they will create;
  • determining the life of the character (for example, the basics of age, where he/ she lives, detail about the history of the person, checking dates, times, historical references);
  • deciding on some significant events in the person's life and the mementos that went with it (for example, a letter, a pen, a certificate, a medal, a good watch, a brooch);
  • find and assemble the objects (what can't be found can be created, for example, certificates and letters can be written);
  • creating, making, and assembling the objects (what can't be found can be made);
  • record all the details of the characters life so that they might form the basis for a brief presentation later;
  • displaying the objects.

Investigating the character

Another group of students might now be working in role as detectives, as though they are building an identikit picture of a missing person by linking together pieces of available evidence. Students might use clipboards or notebooks to add to their belief in their role.

The original group has created their mystery person and the detective group works to uncover the mystery and get as close to "the truth" as possible.

As they examine the objects they speculate about events that might have occurred in the role's life. They record these ideas.

Creating and presenting the scenes

Each group might now create a scene that they think might have occurred in the life of the role. They might use conventions (speaking thoughts aloud, still image, hotseating, thought tracking) for the purpose of capturing a moment in the history of the person.

A few criteria may be set:

  • The person is caught in a moment of using one of the objects.

  • One object must be in a pocket or on the person.

The scene may be developed:

  • into hotseating, with other students able to ask questions;

  • into speaking thoughts aloud where the rest of the group may offer the thoughts as a background or as the walls of the room.

* The group devises a presentation that will show their version of the life of the character. This presentation must include the scene that they have devised. They must provide a context for this work, for example, they might be detectives presenting information about a missing person or a group of friends giving a eulogy at a funeral.

* Refine and rehearse this work for presentation to the class.

Each group now retrieves the work on the character they originally created and rehearses it for a brief presentation.

Presenting the work
Each group now presents the results of their investigations in a context as rehearsed above. After each presentation the group gives a brief presentation on their ideas.

Note
It is important to stress that there are no right or wrong interpretations. This is not a contest to see which group gets the closest to the original character. It is an exercise in comparison that demonstrates the different views we form about people depending on how well we know them and the amount of information available.

[Tip]

Reflection

Students need to understand how different interpretations of material can occur depending on point of view and the fullness of the information available. For example, an actor tries to develop a full profile of a character they are playing but an audience must deduce much of the character from the brief snapshots of life that they see on stage. Specific questions could include:

  • Why did your group select the particular objects in yor initial display?
  • What information did you hope to convey with these objects?
  • What aspects of your character's life did the investigative group deduce correctly?
  • What were the differences between their version and yours?
  • Why do you think these differences might have occured?
