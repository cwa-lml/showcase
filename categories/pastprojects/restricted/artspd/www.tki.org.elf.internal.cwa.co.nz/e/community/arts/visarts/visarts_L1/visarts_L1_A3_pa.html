Level 1: Printmaking and Collage

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

The elements and principles should be introduced gradually over several lessons.

Elements

  • Line – Consider and compare the types of lines you can find on leaves (e.g., thick/ thin, long/ short, zigzag/ curvy, sharp/ blurry.
  • Texture – Consider and compare the surface qualities of different leaves (e.g., rough/ smooth, wet/ dry, hard/soft, shiny/dull, slippery/sticky).
  • Colour – Compare the variety of leaf colours (primary/ secondary, warm/ cold, bright/ dull).
  • Shape – Examine the varying outline shapes of leaves. Do they look like any two-dimensional shapes? (circle/square, rectangle, regular/irregular).

Principles

  • Pattern – Consider and compare the patterns that occur on leaves and those you can create by arranging leaves (e.g., repeat/rotate, reverse/symmetry).

Possible starting points

Take students on an observational walk focusing on autumn leaves and trees. Select a walk where there are piles of fallen autumn leaves and ask the students to:

  • walk through the leaves and shuffle them with their feet;
  • bundle the leaves up, throw them into the wind, and observe them falling to the ground;
  • express, through movement, how a leaf falls to the ground;
  • find a smooth, rough, and a slippery leaf to take back to the classroom;
  • observe the colour of the leaves and find a variety to take back;
  • observe the shapes of leaves and find a variety to take back;
  • whilst walking stop to consider and compare line, shape and pattern. Using the digital camera, photograph larger objects for immediate use.

Discussion

Reflect on the experiences associated with the walk. Place all the leaves on the table.

  • Are the leaves all the same colour?
  • Can you describe the other colours in the leaves?
  • Which colours do you see most often in autumn?
  • Are all the leaves the same shape?
  • How would you describe the leaf edges?
  • Are the textures of the leaves the same?
  • Can you make a pattern from the leaves on the table?
  • How many different leaf shapes can you identify?
  • Can you see any patterns on the leaves?

Possible learning approaches

Four possible activities related to this learning example are described below. Each activity could be the focus for one or more lessons or units of work.

Teachers could adapt and apply them by using instructions appropriate to the level of their students.

Drawing

Use drawing to explore and develop visual ideas. Drawings can be in a continual state of re-drafting, revising, and editing. You may use the photographs and magnifying glasses to aid close observations.

  • Using a white crayon on black paper, draw two leaves from memory. Think about the different shapes and make marks on the leaves to show the stalk and the veins of the leaf.
  • Using coloured crayon on black paper and using the "found" leaves, observe them carefully and draw the leaves to show their shapes, colours, and textures.
  • Using these drawings share ideas about the elements – colour, line, shape. Also share ideas relating to texture.

Basic equipment: black paper, crayon

Frottage

A sheet of paper is placed over a rough surface and rubbed with pencil or crayon to record the image or texture of the surface.

Select a leaf, some bark or other found objects and make a rubbing.

Discuss the shapes and textures that have been created.

Basic equipment: thin paper (newsprint), crayons, or soft pencil (3B -5B).

Card block prints

These are made from shapes cut from plain or corrugated card or found objects such as leaves arranged and stuck onto a cardboard base.

  • Source a range of materials – natural materials (ie leaves, bark), tissue papers and thin card.
  • Cut/ tear shapes and arrange them on a cardboard base (the "cardblock").
  • When a satisfactory composition is developed, glue each layer down in turn (bottom layer first).
  • When the materials on the block are dry, roll water-based ink onto its surface. Turn the cardblock upside down onto the paper. Roll the back of the cardblock firmly with a dry roller. (Alternatively you can place the paper on top of the block and use a dry roller on the top surface).
  • Gently pull the cardblock off the paper to reveal the print.
  • Repeat prints can be obtained depending on the colours used, thickness of the ink and the colour or type of paper used.
  • The used cardblock can often become an interesting art work in itself.

Basic equipment: paste, paper, scissors, cardboard and "found" materials, water-based inks, rollers. Avoid materials that are too thick, they will prevent lower adjacent textures from being printed.

collage

This technique involves assembling and gluing down on a flat surface assorted materials that have been selected for their pictorial meaning or textural qualities.

  • Source different materials, e.g., natural materials (leaves, bark), printed colour magazine photographs and illustrations or coloured/ textured papers
  • Cut/ tear shapes
  • Encourage students to "draw" with the scissors to create shapes that echo the natural shapes
  • Arrange and rearrange shapes to explore composition (moving/ overlapping/ layering shapes). Basic equipment : paste, paper, cardboard and "found" materials

Materials

  • Fallen leaves and natural materials (bark, plant material)
  • Printed, colour magazine photographs, illustrations, coloured/ textured papers
  • Black paper, thin paper (newsprint), corrugated card, thin card, tissue papers, sugar paper, rope brown.
  • Crayons, coloured pencils 3B -5B, ink, paint, glue
  • Magnifying glasses, dry roller, brush, spoons, scissors.
  • Digital camera for photographs of larger objects

Assessment

Possible specific learning outcomes include:

  • students can select, develop and complete their print, or collage using the materials they collected to create an autumn effect (PK, DI);
  • students can identify colours, shapes, and explore textures, or patterns (PK);
  • students can start to talk about the elements of their own work and that of others in their group (CI).
