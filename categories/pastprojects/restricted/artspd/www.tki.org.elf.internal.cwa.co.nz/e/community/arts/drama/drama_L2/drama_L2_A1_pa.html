Level 2: Interpreting Taonga Kitea (A Found Object)

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

Planning

This learning example could be used to create a fictitious character or could be related to a unit topic.

Possible contexts include a problem revealed, a journey, a taonga shared, a relationship between people.

  • A briefcase containing a diary, a parking ticket, a cheque book, an envelope with an address on it, a screwed-up note containing a revelation about an accident, for example, "I scratched your car", a shopping receipt "The shoes I bought used up all our food budget!"

  • A suitcase with an airline ticket, a holiday brochure for Samoa, a pair of sunglasses, and pack of cards.

  • A family heirloom, a taonga, such as a woven piece of clothing, a mere, a tool, or implement kept as family treasure.

  • A purse, a movie ticket, a shopping list, make-up, a diary page containing appointments to meet people.

A number of objects together would allow the group to discuss the clues collectively and record their findings as roles on the wall.

The teacher might ask key questions to give a framework:

  • What does this person like?

  • Where have they been?

  • Are they busy?

  • How old is this person?

  • What can you tell about their friends or family, or the community where they live from these clues?

  • What important events has this character experienced?

  • When did it take place?

  • How do the object/s relate to this event?

  • Is one object more important than the others?

  • Why?

Finding the object [tip]

  • The objects may be wrapped and carefully concealed about the room so that tension is developed through the use of treasure hunt.
  • The object/s will need to be carefully uncovered, or explored, for example, the contents of a bag may need to be examined closely.
  • The objects may need to be transported carefully to the place where they will be gathered and discussed.

Relating the object to the character

Questioning and wondering about the significance of the objects can generate paired discussion and questions, so that students seek clues, or information from each other and then together share ideas through the teacher who organises the details around a body on a wall chart. Placement of the ideas on the chart will be important. For example, if the object has strong sentimental significance the teacher may write this detail near the heart. If it was important as a tool it may be written near the hand. For example:

  • Who owns this object?
  • Has this person been on holiday recently?
  • Did they have the object with them?
  • Where did the person stay?
  • What was the most important thing about what they did while they were away?
  • Can we find out how they checked in at the airport? In pairs action play a one minute check-in scene with person A as Airport Check-in and B as the passenger.
  • Did anything difficult happen while they were away?
  • Find a volunteer to show their scene.

Once students have decided on the person who might have owned the object and have agreed about some key points in his/her life, more contextual ideas can be created, for example, social status, wealth, age, likes and dislikes, occupation.

Selecting and modelling the moment

After the class has decided on the person whose life is to be explored, model creating a scene with a small group by:

  • Selecting one important moment, for example the person packing their suitcase in preparation for a holiday
  • Students suggest who else is in the scene, place where it could be, what they would be thinking. They must also nominate specifically where the object is in the scene even if it is offstage at that particular moment. For example: in Hone's pocket, on the kitchen table, in Auntie's top drawer offstage.
  • Place several students in a freeze frame of the scene and animate them by asking the others to suggest one important thing each character might say at that time. Sequence the statements to create a scene.

Making the scene

Once the whole class has seen the process, the students can work in groups to make their own scenes.

  • Groups could talk further about the life of the person who may have owned the important object.
  • Groups develop a scene to present. This scene could include the object.
  • To provide structure, groups might prepare, first, the still image for the beginning of the scene and the still image for the end. This gives a manageable frame within which to work. They can then develop the action which will link the two images.
  • Alternatively, the group may select and create a series of three still images, each conveying an important moment in the person's life. They might be encouraged to provide the title for each one (The Day When She...).
  • Each group could refine their work by considering what position of focus the object itself should occupy in each scene or whether it is yet to be discovered.

Reflecting on the process

When scenes have been presented, discussion could explore the similarities and differences in interpretations:

  • How important was the object in each scene?
  • What was the significance of the object for the owner at each point?
  • Is the object linked to an important event or moment in the person's life?
  • How did the character acquire the object?

Assessment

Assessment activities could include student suggestions from "role on the wall" discussion can be annotated with their initials and form a valuable formative assessment.

Appropriateness of individual statements in the animated freezeframe may be reorded.

Student scene can be viewed as summative assessment to check ability to maintain role, use of hand and facial gesture.
