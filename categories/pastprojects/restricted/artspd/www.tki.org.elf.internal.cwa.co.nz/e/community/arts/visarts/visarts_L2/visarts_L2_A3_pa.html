Level 2: Close-up Plant Study

PLANNING AND ASSESSMENT

During the processes of drawing students can develop the knowledge and vocabulary to talk about art. Teachers should encourage students to use the art vocabulary associated with this unit by:

  • stopping frequently to consider and compare the plants and their drawing;
  • focusing on selected elements using live materials, photographic images and artists' work for reference;
  • encouraging students to respond verbally to their own and others' work.

The following elements and principles will be the focus for discussion in this learning example.

They should be introduced over several lessons.

Elements

Line – Consider how line has been used in art works to describe detail (e.g., thick/ thin, long/ short, zigzag/ curvy, sharp/ blurry).

Texture – Consider the surface qualities of real plants and plants in art works (e.g., rough/ smooth, wet/ dry, hard/ soft, shiny/ dull, slippery/ sticky). Consider how patterns have been used to describe textures.

Shape – Consider whether the shapes are open/ closed, regular/ irregular, organic/ geometric, i.e., circle, square.

Colour – Observe the colours of the plant and where they change, the effect of shadow.

Form – Consider what three-dimensional shapes the plants are made up of (e.g., cones, cylinders, spheres, pyramids).

Space – Notice which parts of the plant are larger and which parts are smaller.

Possible starting points

Discussion

Investigate a plant or flower. Consider the various parts and stages of growth of the plant, e.g., root, seed, shoot, stalk, leaf, vein, branch, stamen, pistil, petal.

Conduct a class discussion including the following questions:

  • What form has the plant? Is it shaped like a cone, a sphere, or some other shape?
  • Describe the shape of the leaves. Are the leaves pointed, rounded, or some other shape?
  • Are the leaves patterned or textured? What marks can you see?
  • How does the base of the leaf join the stalk of the plant?
  • Do the shapes/ colours/ textures change where the parts join?
Imagine you are an insect in the middle of the plant:
  • Are the lines/ shapes flowing, sharp, jagged?
  • What colours do you see - are they light nor are they dark?
  • What would it feel like inside the flower (sharp, soft, prickly, furry, slippery, sticky)?
  • If you were inside the plant, how many different parts of the flower would you see? Could you see the outside of the plant from there?
  • What would be above your head/below your feet/beside you?

Investigative line drawing of a longitudinal section

  • Find an interesting flowering plant. Carefully cut it vertically to create a longitudinal section or strip part of the leaves and petals from the plant so that only half of the plant remains and the structure of the plant is revealed.
  • Using a ballpoint pen, pencil, or ink pen make an observation drawing using line to show the construction of the plant, the stalk, petals, stamens, pistils, leaves etc.
  • Look carefully at the different sizes of parts of the plant. Show these in your picture.

Key discussion points

When the initial drawings are completed display them and engage the class in discussion about the range and quality of inform that they have identified and recorded.

  • What has come into view that you couldn't see before?
  • What textures, lines and details can you now see?

Possible learning approaches

Introductory activities

Introduce the concept of scale through discussion about well-known characters such as those in:

  • Alice in Wonderland
  • Gulliver's Travels
  • A Bug's Life
  • Honey I Shrunk the Kids!
  • Dinosaurs

Refer to any of the following artists and their works (see Teaching and Learning Materials for websites)

  • Georgia O'Keefe
  • Claus Oldenberg
  • Van Gogh
  • Monet
  • Polynesian art

Drawing the outside of the plant:

Experiment with changing scale by asking the students to observe the plant through a magnifying glass. Imagine they are an ant taking an imaginary journey around the plant. Teachers could facilitate this journey by verbally "leading" the students around the plant.

Right side of the brain drawing – students could imagine the tip of their pencil is the insect and they could take it on a walk around the flower, looking at the flower but not at their drawing. If they feel really lost they can quickly look at their drawing to relocate the pencil. In this exercise the pencil line is continuous, the pencil is not lifted, except, as explained, to relocate.

Key discussion points:

  • In what way is your second drawing different to your first one?
  • Compare the lines, the shapes and the sizes of parts of the drawings.
  • Which drawing do you like most?
  • Why?

Making large drawings

Using coloured crayons draw the plant again imagining that you are a tiny bug inside or on the plant. Change the scale of the various parts of the plant according to how close you are to them, so that you create a new and interesting set of forms with bold and exciting patterns and textures.

Handy hint

Use crayons, rather than pencil. This should encourage students to produce larger drawings

Materials

  • flowers or plants
  • newsprint, cartridge paper
  • ballpoint pens, drawing pencils (ie, 2B, 4B, 6B), ink pens, crayons, charcoal

Assessment

Possible specific learning outcomes include:

Students will be able to:

  • identify and convey different forms, patterns and textures in their art works (PK);
  • develop ideas from observation and imagination that express scale (DI);
  • use drawing materials to inventively create exciting patterns and textures (DI).
