Questioning and conferencing: Assessment strategies for the visual arts

By Helen Cooper, Regional coordinator for visual arts, 2003

Templates for recording assessment

The primary aims of assessment are to:

  • improve student learning
  • provide evidence of student learning so that a teacher can evaluate student needs and plan programmes accordingly.

Questioning and conferencing are two powerful formative assessment tools traditionally used by visual arts teachers. To be fully effective we need to ensure that such assessment tools are equitable – all students in the class should have equal access to feedback and feed-forward. Students who initiate contact can dominate questioning and conferencing, and quieter students may therefore have limited access.

The issue is how to record evidence of this process so teachers can:

  • know whether learning is taking place
  • know what progress individual students are making
  • ensure accessibility for all students
  • evaluate learning to inform planning
  • record evidence to report to students, parents, community.

Questioning – either through group or class discussions, or through conferencing with individuals – is a very effective method of assessment for the strands Understanding the Visual Arts in Context (UC) and Communicating and Interpreting in the Visual Arts (CI) from The Arts in the New Zealand Curriculum (Ministry of Education, 2000).

A means of recording assessment needs to be effective and manageable. Here are four templates that can be used to record assessment. Download either the word or PDF format.

Student conferencing records
Download this sheet and cut into slips of paper, ready for students each period to record notes from conferencing with their teacher. The record slips could be extended to provide a focus for self-assessment, once students are familiar with recording their goals.

Using the student conferencing records

The student conferencing records could be cut up, ready for students to use with a conferencing session with their teacher.

It is useful to have a supply of student conferencing records in clear file per class, with a named pocket for each member of the class. When a conference takes place with a student, the student completes the sheet, summarising what they understand they have to do as their next step. The student then files the strip in their pocket in the class clear file. At any stage the teacher can refer back to see what was discussed, and check to make sure all students are receiving feedback and feed-forward. Requiring the student to record their next step focuses discussion with the student on their next step or goal.

A central record of feedback allows the teacher at any stage to review the type of feed-forward being given, the effectiveness of conferencing, coverage of the class as well as maintaining a record that the student can return to.

If the system is first used with junior classes, then expanded at senior level, students will form the habit of taking responsibility for recording next steps, ensuring guidance and results of brainstorming are not lost. Some teachers use a pad and carbon paper for seniors to provide a copy for the student and a copy for a central filing system.

A central file and class record sheets provide evidence from conferencing and group or class discussions that can be used to inform report writing and parent/student teacher interviews.

Using the discussion record and assessment records

The discussion record and assessment record templates are for teachers to use. They could be kept easily accessible, for example in the roll book. After a class discussion or conferencing session with students, record the names of the students observed during that discussion or session, and record that they responded either by a tick or particular level of response

It may be that after a class discussion, contributions of only three or four students are recorded. To be effective the record needs to be maintained regularly, for instance, it is more effective to record a little, often, rather than trying to record evidence of a whole class but only managing it occasionally. In this way, evidence for all students is recorded over a few periods or weeks.

A subsidiary aim of the sheets is to provide a means for teachers to evaluate coverage of the strands Communicating and Interpreting in the Visual Arts (CI) and the Understanding the Visual Arts in Context (UC), recognising that these strands are integrated daily alongside the strands Developing Practical Knowledge in the Visual Arts (PK) and Developing Ideas in the Visual Arts (DI). In particular units there may be specific learning outcomes for CI or UC strands that may be assessed in other ways, but it is important to recognise that the knowledge and skill components of the CI and UC strands are integral to all art teaching.

The templates also provide opportunity for teachers to observe patterns of contribution. They can show which students in the class are not participating and which students the teacher is not conferencing with as much as others. In this way, a teacher can evaluate teaching and coverage, and target particular students to ensure that the needs of all students are met.

All sheets can be adapted to meet personal teaching requirements. Evidence of conferencing could be recorded with a different mark to contributions made in class discussions if the teacher wishes to identify how the evidence was gathered.

Other assessment strategies to consider

If a class has a visiting speaker, a teacher can use the sheets to focus their observations of students and their level of contribution, or if a colleague who knows the names of students is available, they could observe and record contributions for the whole class then provide feedback on coverage, questioning skills and strategies.
