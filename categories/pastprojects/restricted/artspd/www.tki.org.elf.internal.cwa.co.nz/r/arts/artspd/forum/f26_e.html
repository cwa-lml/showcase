Some people assume that Nga Toi is simply a translation of The Arts Curriculum document. What examples would you give to illustrate the fundamental differences between the two documents? For example, Kapa Haka versus Folk dancing? What aspects of the Nga Toi document make the content distinctly Maori?

Merryn Dunmill:
Kia ora koutou.
There are very real differences between the two documents apart from the fact that Nga Toi is not a translation of The Arts document. Nga Toi is based on ihi wehi, wairua and many Maori concepts and approaches. These are unique and special to Maori culture and are not transferable, although it is important to consider them in the pakeha document as well of course. The elements of music and Toi Puoro are similar but contextually different. There are no learning examples as such in the Nga Toi document and these are being developed for kura. The questions are so big that they deserve a forum on their own and there is no time to deal with them respectfully in the time available at the moment. I will pick up on this with Rawiri Toia at a later date if that is ok?

Hans:
Can you explain soundscapes for me? They are mentioned in the chapter referred to above and I'm not too sure about that. What would be a relevant activity for a theme such as "a trip to the zoo"?

Merryn Dunmill
Soundscapes are just as the title suggests... they are pictorial representations of sound, such as a timeline with graphics or symbols of actual sounds as they are intended to happen in the musical composition. So you could have a trip to the zoo starting at the bus. 10 seconds might be rhythmic bouncing of the engine starting up and accelerating, all shown by real notation, or symbolic ideas. Down the left side is a key which shows which sound sources or instruments play the lines or parts of the score, as in a traditional score. There is a graphic score which is not time based, and a free "events" type score for students to interpret with instruments. These can both be found on the last page of Into Music 1 (Appendix 4). Page 49 has clear instructions about what they are and how to interpret and/or write them.

Kids make up their own visuals but they must be clear enough and detailed enough for others to interpret back in the way the music was intended. Going to the zoo is a good one. You can visit cages or areas of different animals; have little themes and melodic ideas for each as in a characterisation. Between each "cage' you could have another little repeating idea such as a rhythmic "hook" to show that you are walking along...

Hans:
Thanks, that's great. I now have a clear idea of what that means. I liked your summary –representing is recording these ideas. That makes sense. Could you also explain 'tone colour' for me?

Merryn:
Tone colour is basically why one instrument sounds different to another...exactly as the term says I guess...each instrument or sound has its own unique sound or colour and identifying each on its own and in combination is a great skill. For example, the ability to name a violin playing in a group compared with a piano, or even the different drums in a drum kit, to the differences between a violin and a viola (trickier!). The way the instrument is played impacts on this description too. For example, the violin is plucked, while the guitar is electric and has a distortion effect.

Carol Langton:
Kia ora, Merryn. Welcome to another Music Forum. As this one is called "Creating and Representing", could you please define these terms for us?

Merryn Dunmill:
The creating is the ideas as suggested above – the sounds themselves and how they are selected and organised in relation to the concept. The representation is the written notation of that for future performance...to reproduce. Art is the child of imagination...creating music is composing and improvising, representing is recording those ideas.

The Bear Hunt.............ah Haa! Yes, I will bearware of copyright. That is actually a major issue...copyright. Can we play CDs in class (we can't play videos)? I know we can't copy CDs, but how about singing the songs off a CD for a school production?

Chris
If you are not charging a fee for the video viewing. You are not copying it. It is part of the learning experience. We would never use a video as a time filler after all would we...It can't be a breach of copyright to use the work of a credited artist. Surely...???

Merryn Dunmill
Yes, copyright is a big deal. Schools usually pay a sum to APRA for all rights and if no money is changing hands for performance of a song then all is well. There are several fees schools should pay to cover all sorts of copyright and it is worked out on a school capita ratio. If you are just listening in class and singing songs at school for general education purposes it's all fine. Audience-based concerts should detail whom the music is by and technically a fee should be paid. Primary schools generally do not have to worry except when doing musicals and festivals and royalties are incurred from such performances.
Relax in the classroom and don't copy any CDs as with videos. Excerpts of each are fine to use but whole videos, eg of Amadeus, are not supposed to be shown at school. Check your school policy and adherence to all copyright which includes photocopying of written materials of course.

Jean
I am working with 'identifying and exploring sound' with my class and we are making instruments to experiment with. We have already used material that is the same – such as all metal, all wood, all plastic. We are next going to combine a range of different materials to find out what sound they will make. Can you give me some guidance on how best to do this as I can see the sounds may be really complex and difficult to distinguish?

Merryn Dunmill
Great that you are making your own instruments and trying a range of sound sources. Excellent science and technology coverage too. Identifying the sounds is a good listening skill. I guess you've done all the "how many different sounds can we make with this instrument?" and "what makes this one sound different to this one?" stuff. Building the vocabulary and the focused listening will help students differentiate when they are all combined.

Try setting up the different materials in groups around the room, for example metal in one corner, wood in the middle and so on. Get a group of students to close their eyes and listen for the different instruments and say which is being played (metal or wood etc). Make this harder by putting students on a range of instruments, made of different materials, in the middle of the room with the rest of the class sitting around the outside of the them, but facing out so they can't see. Tap the shoulder of individual or combined students in the middle, indicating they play their instrument. Get the outside circle to say which instrument/s they heard.

When creating sound pieces, group the instruments in their sounds as independent parts or lines of the score. Have a visual that shows which is which and have students play a line on their instrument. Swap over and play a new line on a new instrument (eg from wood to metal to shakers). Keep the piece short and simple because they are learning to read and interpret sound through playing...quite a complex range of brain and physical things all at once!!!
Hope this makes sense.

Tamara
I think my first question has been answered; however I do have another one. Do you have any ways of encouraging a range of sound in terms of volume? I find that they all want to play loudly.

Chris
Ask them to play quietly. Two suggestions:

  1. Have the children sit in a circle, they play when you touch them on the shoulder, they stop when you touch them again.

  2. Use a ruler or pointer; have the children watch you. When the pointer is down low the volume is low, when you raise it up they play louder. Experiment with up and down to different degrees.

Something to start with anyway.

Robert
Kia ora Merryn. I have been a lurker on this forum for quite a while now. You have given me lots of stuff to work on over the weeks, so thanks for all your great ideas. I have really appreciated them. It would be great if you could give me some ideas about how to set up a positive, constructive feedback session with my class.

Merryn Dunmill
Invite the students to comment on what they thought worked well and why. How could they improve their soundscape if they did it again? What did they enjoy and why? What was their favourite moment in the piece? Etc. Get the ideas on a board for class evaluation. Use the language of music – talk about the dynamics, the tone colours that were particularly effective, how they were made and changed etc. Goal set for another creative adventure...what sounds/graphic ideas could we use in the next piece on...

Jean
When using a piece of music such as "The Hall of the Mountain King" as a model for an exciting ending do you have any advice on how best to use this technique?

Merryn Dunmill
Exciting ways to finish like "Hall of Mountain King"? I think that's what you are asking. Find other pieces that end the same way, in different ways and compare them – talk about what might be happening. Try to re-create endings of pieces to match ends of stories...happy, sad, mysterious, slow, fast etc. Try doing the opposite for a laugh eg a happy ending or all sad and slow with a little tune that tales off in the air....
