Ceramics with Niuean patterns – Brandon Intermediate School

Introduction | Organisation/Planning | Content | Issues | Evaluation/Reflection

Content

SESSION ONE – Drawing patterns

Students began by looking through a book of Niuean patterns. They discussed how Niuean tapa patterns reflect the patterns of the environment, such as:

  • fetau and tui tui trees
  • hibiscus flowers
  • the sea
  • the patterns of the land.

Each student chose several patterns and sketched them into their drawing book using lead and coloured pencils, and felt pens.

Their drawing books were made from A3 newsprint pages folded in half and stapled along the spine.

All Georgia's art students used their drawing book for sketching ideas and making notes about such things as colour, contrast, patterning, spacing, texture and composition.

Students chose several patterns from their drawings to use as decoration.

They had to stylise the pattern to the shape of the pot they intended to make.

Resource: Robertson, Ailsa. (1989). Patterns of Polynesia. (Other title: Niue). Auckland: Heinemann Education ISBN: 0868630721









SESSION TWO – Sculpting the clay

Teaching points

  • Aim for a consistent thickness of 1cm when rolling out the clay.


  • Join each section of the pot by using scoring and slip (see below).


  1. Prepare the clay by kneading it like bread dough.


  2. Roll out the clay to a 1cm thickness.


  3. Cut out a base shape either by using a straight-edged ruler to make a rectangle, or by cutting around a circular plaster board to make a circle.


  4. Form the rest of the clay into sausage shapes then roll them flat to 1cm.


  5. Cut these shapes to make the sides of the pot using the ruler as a guide (see photo).


  6. Join the clay pieces by scoring (roughening) the edges.


  7. Apply slip (wet clay sludge) to moisten the scored edges.


  8. Press the two edges together with a tool or with your fingers, and smooth the joins.


Objects made out of clay are called greenware. Greenware has been fashioned to the desired shape and dried slowly and naturally. It remains very fragile until fired in a kiln.













SESSION THREE – Decorating the pots

Teaching points

  • Is the shape of the pattern appropriate to the shape of the pot?


  • Do the two colours complement each other?


  • Has the glaze been applied with care?


Organisation

  • Clay glazes were stored in small, plastic-covered pots.


  • Students were asked to choose two colours.


  • They worked straight from the pots and washed their brushes between colours.


Georgia wrote these instructions on the whiteboard:

  1. Draw your pattern onto the pot lightly in pencil.


  2. Paint on your first colour.


  3. Wash your brush in water.


  4. Paint on your second colour.


Once students had decorated their pots, Georgia painted the finished pots with a clear glaze and fired them to 1137 to 1150C/cone 06. This firing transformed the bisqueware into finished ceramic, making it safe to wash and use as containers for a variety of purposes.




















Three stages of the ceramic process

Greenware
When the greenware was hard and dry, Georgia used the school kiln to fire students' pots. This low-temperature firing was in the range of 1100 to 1150°C, at cone 04.

Bisque
Ceramic pieces emerging from this firing are called bisque or bisqueware. They are now as strong as everyday ceramic pieces and ready for an application of ceramic glaze.

Before students drew patterns on their pots and applied coloured glazes, a white under-glaze was painted onto the bisqueware in crosshatch brushstrokes to ensure coverage.

After the bisque-fired ceramic pieces were glazed, they were ready for firing a second and last time, at cone 06 for glaze firing. The temperature of this firing was hot enough to melt the glaze and turn it into a hard "glass."

Cones
When firing clay, Orton pyrometric cones are placed in the kiln to gauge the temperature. These cones are elongated pyramids of clay and various fluxes. Each cone melts (bends) at a temperature corresponding to its number, when heated at the rate per hour indicated by the temperature equivalent chart.

Clay and glazes are formulated to be properly fired when they reach the heat required to bend the appropriate cone. Cones behave exactly like bisqueware and glazes.

To test the readiness of students' bisqueware inside the kiln, Georgia put a cone where she could see it near the pots, to indicate if they had been heated enough. The cone bent when it received the right degree of heat, and not just when the kiln reached a certain temperature.
